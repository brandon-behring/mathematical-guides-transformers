# Recurrent Networks and the Seq2Seq Lineage

**Where we are.** The notation chapter fixed the toolkit and the previous chapter built the inputs: any modality becomes a sequence of vectors in $\R^{n\times d}$. This chapter opens the guide's account of what to *do* with that sequence, and it begins where the field began — with recurrence. We define the recurrent network and unroll it into a depth-$n$ computation graph with tied weights; derive its training gradient, backpropagation through time, as a sum over time of chain-rule products; prove the geometric bound on those products that makes long-range gradients vanish or explode; construct the gated remedies, the LSTM and the GRU, and prove that their additive cell path — the constant error carousel — escapes the bound; assemble recurrent networks into the sequence-to-sequence encoder–decoder and prove that its fixed-vector interface is a genuine bottleneck, by a pigeonhole count; and construct the mechanism invented to dissolve that bottleneck, additive attention. That last construction is the hinge of the whole guide: softmax content addressing is born here, *inside* a recurrent model, and the chapters that follow ask what survives when the recurrence is taken away.

## The recurrent state

A recurrent network processes a sequence the way a reader processes a sentence: one element at a time, carrying a running summary forward. The summary is a single vector, the *state*; each step folds the next input into it by one fixed, learned map. All memory of the past must live in that vector — a design decision whose consequences, good and bad, drive this entire chapter.

Throughout this chapter we treat states and inputs as **column** vectors — a stated departure from the row convention, as in the softmax-Jacobian calculus of the notation chapter — so that Jacobians act by left multiplication. The inputs $\mathbf x_1,\dots,\mathbf x_n\in\R^{d}$ are the rows of an embedded sequence $\mathbf X\in\R^{n\times d}$ (<XRef id="def-tf1-embedding-interface" />), written as columns.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Recurrent neural network" id="def-tf2-rnn">
Fix a **state dimension** $d_s$, weights $\mathbf W_h\in\R^{d_s\times d_s}$, $\mathbf W_x\in\R^{d_s\times d}$, a bias $\mathbf b\in\R^{d_s}$, and an elementwise nonlinearity $\varphi$ (classically $\tanh$). Given inputs $\mathbf x_1,\dots,\mathbf x_n\in\R^{d}$ and the initial state $\mathbf h_0 = \mathbf 0$, the **recurrent neural network (RNN)** computes, for $t = 1,\dots,n$, the pre-activation and **hidden state**
$$
\mathbf a_t \defeq \mathbf W_h\mathbf h_{t-1} + \mathbf W_x\mathbf x_t + \mathbf b,
\qquad
\mathbf h_t \defeq \varphi(\mathbf a_t) \in\R^{d_s}.
$$
The **unrolled computation graph** of $\mathbf h_n$ is the depth-$n$ feed-forward network obtained by writing one copy of the update per step,
$$
\mathbf h_n = \varphi\big(\mathbf W_h\,\varphi(\mathbf W_h\,\varphi(\cdots) + \mathbf W_x\mathbf x_{n-1} + \mathbf b) + \mathbf W_x\mathbf x_n + \mathbf b\big),
$$
in which every layer shares the same parameters $(\mathbf W_h, \mathbf W_x, \mathbf b)$ — the weights are **tied** across time.<Sidenote>The simple recurrent architecture in this form is due to <Cite key="elman1990finding" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Tied depth">An RNN on $n$ tokens *is* an $n$-layer network whose layers are one shared map. Depth grows with the data, parameters do not.</MarginNote>

Two structural facts follow from the definition and shape everything below. First, the state is a *sufficient statistic by fiat*: the distribution of everything the network computes after step $t$ depends on $\mathbf x_1,\dots,\mathbf x_t$ only through $\mathbf h_t\in\R^{d_s}$, a vector whose size is fixed before the data arrive. Second, the unrolled graph is a *chain*: the only path from an early input to a late state passes through every intermediate state, so every long-range dependency must survive $T - t$ applications of the same map. The first fact becomes the seq2seq bottleneck; the second becomes the vanishing gradient. We take them in gradient order.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Vision">The recurrence never asks whether a row is token, patch, or code (<XRef id="rem-tf1-modality-blind" />); it only demands a total order — a patch grid must be raster-scanned first.</MarginNote>

## Backpropagation through time

Training minimizes a per-step loss summed over the sequence, $L = \sum_{t=1}^n L_t(\mathbf h_t)$ — for language modeling, $L_t$ is the cross-entropy of the next-token prediction read off $\mathbf h_t$ (<XRef id="def-cross-entropy" />). The gradient computation is nothing but the chain rule on the unrolled graph, with one wrinkle: the same $\mathbf W_h$ appears at every layer, so its total gradient is a *sum over the occurrences*. Working that out yields both the formula that exposes the pathology and the algorithm that computes it in one backward pass.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">$\mathbf W_h$ appears $n$ times in the unrolled graph. Before reading: what should $\nabla_{\mathbf W_h} L$ be, if each occurrence were an independent copy?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="Backpropagation through time" id="thm-tf2-bptt">
For the RNN of <XRef id="def-tf2-rnn" />, with $\varphi$ and each per-step loss differentiable and $L = \sum_{t=1}^{n} L_t(\mathbf h_t)$:

**(i) Jacobian factorization.** For $1 \le s < t \le n$,
$$
\frac{\partial \mathbf h_t}{\partial \mathbf h_s}
= \prod_{r=s+1}^{t} \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_r)\big)\,\mathbf W_h,
$$
the factors ordered with $r$ decreasing from left to right (leftmost $r = t$), and $\partial\mathbf h_t/\partial\mathbf h_t = \mathbf I$.

**(ii) Sum over time of chain-rule products.**
$$
\nabla_{\mathbf W_h} L
= \sum_{t=1}^{n}\ \sum_{s=1}^{t}\ \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s)\big)
\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf h_t}{\partial \mathbf h_s}\right)^{\!\top}
\big(\nabla_{\mathbf h_t} L_t\big)\ \mathbf h_{s-1}^\top .
$$

**(iii) Backward recurrence.** The totals $\mathbf g_t \defeq \nabla_{\mathbf h_t} L$ satisfy
$$
\mathbf g_n = \nabla_{\mathbf h_n} L_n,
\qquad
\mathbf g_t = \nabla_{\mathbf h_t} L_t + \mathbf W_h^\top \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_{t+1})\big)\,\mathbf g_{t+1},
$$
and $\nabla_{\mathbf W_h} L = \sum_{s=1}^{n} \operatorname{diag}(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s))\,\mathbf g_s\,\mathbf h_{s-1}^\top$, computable in one backward pass in $O\!\big(n\,d_s(d_s + d)\big)$ arithmetic.<Sidenote>The algorithm and the name are due to <Cite key="werbos1990bptt" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (one-step Jacobian).** $\mathbf h_r = \varphi(\mathbf a_r)$ with $\varphi$ elementwise, so $\partial\mathbf h_r/\partial\mathbf a_r = \operatorname{diag}(\varphi'(\mathbf a_r))$; and $\mathbf a_r$ is affine in $\mathbf h_{r-1}$ with linear part $\mathbf W_h$, so $\partial\mathbf a_r/\partial\mathbf h_{r-1} = \mathbf W_h$. By the chain rule,
$$
\frac{\partial \mathbf h_r}{\partial \mathbf h_{r-1}} = \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_r)\big)\,\mathbf W_h .
$$

**Step 2 (compose across the gap).** In the unrolled graph the unique path from $\mathbf h_s$ to $\mathbf h_t$ is $\mathbf h_s\to\mathbf h_{s+1}\to\cdots\to\mathbf h_t$, so the chain rule multiplies the one-step Jacobians of Step 1 in path order, giving (i).

**Step 3 (tied weights sum over occurrences).** Replace the $s$-th occurrence of $\mathbf W_h$ by an independent copy $\mathbf W^{(s)}$, making $L$ a function of $n$ copies that agrees with the original when all copies equal $\mathbf W_h$. By the multivariate chain rule, the derivative in the tied parameter is the sum of the derivatives in the copies:
$$
\nabla_{\mathbf W_h} L = \sum_{s=1}^{n} \nabla_{\mathbf W^{(s)}} L\ \Big|_{\mathbf W^{(1)}=\cdots=\mathbf W^{(n)}=\mathbf W_h}.
$$
The copy $\mathbf W^{(s)}$ affects $L$ only through $\mathbf a_s$, and $[\mathbf a_s]_i = \sum_j [\mathbf W^{(s)}]_{ij}[\mathbf h_{s-1}]_j + \cdots$ gives $\nabla_{\mathbf W^{(s)}} L = (\nabla_{\mathbf a_s} L)\,\mathbf h_{s-1}^\top$ (an outer product). Finally $\mathbf a_s$ reaches the loss through every $\mathbf h_t$ with $t \ge s$, so
$$
\nabla_{\mathbf a_s} L
= \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s)\big) \sum_{t=s}^{n} \left(\frac{\partial \mathbf h_t}{\partial \mathbf h_s}\right)^{\!\top} \nabla_{\mathbf h_t} L_t
\qquad\text{(Step 1; Step 2; transpose for gradients)}.
$$
Substituting and exchanging the order of the double sum ($s \le t$) gives (ii).

**Step 4 (backward recurrence).** Split the sum defining $\mathbf g_s = \sum_{t\ge s} (\partial\mathbf h_t/\partial\mathbf h_s)^\top \nabla_{\mathbf h_t} L_t$ into its $t = s$ term and the rest; each remaining term factors through the first hop, $(\partial\mathbf h_t/\partial\mathbf h_s)^\top = (\partial\mathbf h_{s+1}/\partial\mathbf h_s)^\top(\partial\mathbf h_t/\partial\mathbf h_{s+1})^\top$, and the common factor pulls out:
$$
\mathbf g_s = \nabla_{\mathbf h_s} L_s + \mathbf W_h^\top \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_{s+1})\big) \sum_{t=s+1}^{n} \left(\frac{\partial \mathbf h_t}{\partial \mathbf h_{s+1}}\right)^{\!\top} \nabla_{\mathbf h_t} L_t
= \nabla_{\mathbf h_s} L_s + \mathbf W_h^\top \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_{s+1})\big)\,\mathbf g_{s+1}.
$$
Each backward step is one diagonal scaling and one matrix–vector product, $O(d_s^2)$; accumulating the outer products $\operatorname{diag}(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s))\,\mathbf g_s\,\mathbf h_{s-1}^\top$ (and the analogous $\nabla_{\mathbf W_x} L$ terms, $O(d_s d)$ each) over $n$ steps gives the stated cost. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Two clocks">Read (ii) with two indices: $t$ ranges over where loss is *read out*, $s$ over where the parameter *acted*. Every pair $s \le t$ contributes one chain-rule product spanning the gap.</MarginNote>

The gradients with respect to $\mathbf W_x$ and $\mathbf b$ follow from the identical Step 3 argument with $\mathbf x_s^\top$ or $1$ in place of $\mathbf h_{s-1}^\top$. Everything pathological about the formula sits in one factor: the Jacobian product of (i), a product of $t - s$ matrices, every one of them the *same kind* of matrix. We now bound it.

## Vanishing and exploding gradients

A product of $T - t$ numbers, each of size $\rho$, is $\rho^{T-t}$: geometric in the gap. The Jacobian product of <XRef id="thm-tf2-bptt" /> behaves the same way with $\rho$ replaced by an operator norm, and the norm of each factor is capped by two quantities the architecture fixes — the largest singular value of $\mathbf W_h$ and the largest slope of $\varphi$. The consequence is that the credit a loss at time $T$ can assign to a state at time $t$ dies (or blows up) geometrically in $T - t$.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before the proof: $\norm{\mathbf A\mathbf B}_2 \le \norm{\mathbf A}_2\norm{\mathbf B}_2$. What is the largest possible spectral norm of $\operatorname{diag}(\varphi'(\mathbf a))\,\mathbf W_h$, over all activations $\mathbf a$?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="Geometric gradient bound" id="thm-tf2-gradient-bound">
Let $\varphi$ be differentiable with $\gamma \defeq \sup_{z}\lvert\varphi'(z)\rvert < \infty$, and let $\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h)$ be the largest singular value of $\mathbf W_h$. For all $t < T$,
$$
\norm{\frac{\partial \mathbf h_T}{\partial \mathbf h_t}}_2
\;\le\; \prod_{s=t+1}^{T} \norm{\mathbf W_h^\top \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s)\big)}_2
\;\le\; \big(\gamma\,\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h)\big)^{T-t}.
$$
Consequently:

**(i) Vanishing regime.** If $\gamma\,\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h) < 1$, then $\norm{\partial \mathbf h_T/\partial \mathbf h_t}_2 \to 0$ exponentially in the gap $T - t$.

**(ii) Exploding regime.** Gradient growth is possible only if $\gamma\,\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h) > 1$ (a necessary condition), and the geometric rate is attained: for the scalar linear RNN $\varphi = \operatorname{id}$, $\mathbf W_h = (\lambda)$ with $\lambda > 1$, one has $\partial h_T/\partial h_t = \lambda^{T-t} \to \infty$.<Sidenote>The difficulty was identified by <Cite key="bengio1994learning" />; the singular-value conditions are from <Cite key="pascanu2013difficulty" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (transpose invariance).** The spectral norm is invariant under transposition, so for each factor of <XRef id="thm-tf2-bptt" />(i),
$$
\norm{\operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s)\big)\,\mathbf W_h}_2
= \norm{\big(\operatorname{diag}(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s))\,\mathbf W_h\big)^{\!\top}}_2
= \norm{\mathbf W_h^\top \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s)\big)}_2,
$$
which is the form in which the factor multiplies gradients in the backward recurrence.

**Step 2 (submultiplicativity).** The spectral norm is submultiplicative, so the norm of the product in <XRef id="thm-tf2-bptt" />(i) is at most the product of the factor norms — the first inequality.

**Step 3 (bound one factor).** A diagonal matrix has spectral norm equal to its largest absolute entry: $\norm{\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf v)}_2 = \max_i \lvert v_i\rvert$ (Exercise 2.3). Hence, by submultiplicativity again,
$$
\norm{\mathbf W_h^\top \operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s)\big)}_2
\le \norm{\mathbf W_h^\top}_2\, \max_i \big\lvert\varphi'([\mathbf a_s]_i)\big\rvert
\le \sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h)\,\gamma,
$$
using $\norm{\mathbf W_h^\top}_2 = \sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h)$. Multiplying $T - t$ identical bounds gives the second inequality.

**Step 4 (regimes).** If $\gamma\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h) = \rho < 1$, the bound reads $\rho^{T-t} \to 0$ exponentially — (i). For (ii), the bound shows growth is impossible when $\gamma\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h) \le 1$, so $\gamma\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h) > 1$ is necessary; in the scalar linear case every factor equals $\lambda$ exactly, so the product is $\lambda^{T-t}$, unbounded for $\lambda > 1$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Slope caps">$\tanh' = 1 - \tanh^2 \le 1$, so $\gamma = 1$; the logistic sigmoid has $\gamma = 1/4$. A sigmoid RNN vanishes whenever $\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h) < 4$.</MarginNote>

<MarginNote variant="warning" label="One-sided">The first inequality is data-dependent (through the visited $\mathbf a_s$); the second is worst-case. The bound *proves* vanishing when $\gamma\sigma_{\max} < 1$; it only *permits* explosion otherwise.</MarginNote>

The two regimes are not symmetric in practice. Explosion is loud — the loss spikes — and is suppressed by clipping the gradient norm; vanishing is silent: training proceeds, the recent past is learned, and the long range is simply never credited. The worked trace below makes the silence audible on the smallest possible instance.

<Theorem kind="example" name="A two-step scalar gradient trace" id="ex-tf2-scalar-trace">
Take $d_s = d = 1$, $\varphi = \tanh$, $w_h = 0.5$, $w_x = 1$, $b = 0$, $h_0 = 0$, and inputs $x_1 = x_2 = 1$.

**Forward.** $a_1 = w_h h_0 + w_x x_1 = 1$, so $h_1 = \tanh(1) \approx 0.76159$ and $\varphi'(a_1) = 1 - h_1^2 \approx 0.41997$. Then $a_2 = 0.5\cdot 0.76159 + 1 = 1.38080$, so $h_2 = \tanh(1.38080) \approx 0.88113$ and $\varphi'(a_2) \approx 0.22361$.

**Backward, by <XRef id="thm-tf2-bptt" />(ii).** The parameter $w_x$ acts at both steps, and its gradient is the sum of the two chain-rule products:
$$
\frac{\partial h_2}{\partial w_x}
= \underbrace{\varphi'(a_2)\,x_2}_{s=2:\ 0.22361}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\varphi'(a_2)\,w_h\,\varphi'(a_1)\,x_1}_{s=1:\ 0.04696}
\;\approx\; 0.27057 .
$$
The one-step-older occurrence already contributes $0.04696/0.22361 \approx 0.21$ of the fresh one — the per-step attenuation factor is exactly $w_h\,\varphi'(a_1) \approx 0.20999$. The state-to-state Jacobian across the full gap is
$$
\frac{\partial h_2}{\partial h_0} = \varphi'(a_2)\,w_h\cdot\varphi'(a_1)\,w_h \approx 0.02348
\;\le\; \big(\gamma\,\sigma_{\max}\big)^2 = (1\cdot 0.5)^2 = 0.25,
$$
consistent with <XRef id="thm-tf2-gradient-bound" />. Compounded, the attenuation is ruinous: at this per-step factor a signal ten steps back contributes $\approx 0.21^{10} \approx 1.7\times 10^{-7}$ of a fresh signal, and even the bound's best case allows only $0.5^{20} \approx 9.5\times 10^{-7}$ across twenty steps. The network is structurally deaf to its own past.
</Theorem>

## Gated recurrences: LSTM and GRU

Diagnosis before cure: the failure is *multiplicative*. Every step of the chain multiplies the gradient by a fresh copy of $\mathbf W_h^\top\operatorname{diag}(\varphi')$, and a product of many similar factors is geometric. The remedy is to give the state an *additive* update — new information is added to the old state rather than substituted through a squashing map — and to put the only multiplication under the network's own control, as a **gate**: a data-dependent factor in $(0,1)$ that the network can hold open at $\approx 1$. That is the long short-term memory design.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Long short-term memory (LSTM)" id="def-tf2-lstm">
Let $\sigma(z) \defeq 1/(1+e^{-z})$ denote the elementwise logistic function (distinguish it from the singular value $\sigma_{\max}(\cdot)$, which always carries a matrix argument). The **LSTM** carries two states, a **cell state** $\mathbf c_t\in\R^{d_s}$ and a hidden state $\mathbf h_t\in\R^{d_s}$, updated for $t = 1,\dots,n$ by three **gates** and a candidate,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf f_t &\defeq \sigma(\mathbf W_f\mathbf x_t + \mathbf U_f\mathbf h_{t-1} + \mathbf b_f) && \text{(forget gate)},\\
\mathbf i_t &\defeq \sigma(\mathbf W_i\mathbf x_t + \mathbf U_i\mathbf h_{t-1} + \mathbf b_i) && \text{(input gate)},\\
\mathbf o_t &\defeq \sigma(\mathbf W_o\mathbf x_t + \mathbf U_o\mathbf h_{t-1} + \mathbf b_o) && \text{(output gate)},\\
\tilde{\mathbf c}_t &\defeq \tanh(\mathbf W_c\mathbf x_t + \mathbf U_c\mathbf h_{t-1} + \mathbf b_c) && \text{(candidate)},
\end{aligned}
$$
followed by the state updates
$$
\mathbf c_t \defeq \mathbf f_t \odot \mathbf c_{t-1} + \mathbf i_t \odot \tilde{\mathbf c}_t,
\qquad
\mathbf h_t \defeq \mathbf o_t \odot \tanh(\mathbf c_t),
$$
with $\mathbf W_\bullet\in\R^{d_s\times d}$, $\mathbf U_\bullet\in\R^{d_s\times d_s}$, $\mathbf b_\bullet\in\R^{d_s}$, and $\mathbf c_0 = \mathbf h_0 = \mathbf 0$.<Sidenote>The architecture and its analysis of the multiplicative failure are due to <Cite key="hochreiter1997lstm" />; their original cell has no forget gate — the gate $\mathbf f_t$ shown here was added by <Cite key="gers2000forget" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Gate = dial">Each gate is a vector in $(0,1)^{d_s}$ computed *from the data*: a per-coordinate dial between "keep" and "overwrite," learned rather than fixed.</MarginNote>

The crucial line is the cell update: $\mathbf c_t$ is a gated *sum*, not a squashed matrix product. Along the chain $\mathbf c_t \to \mathbf c_{t+1} \to \cdots$ the state is never pushed through $\varphi$ and never multiplied by a weight matrix. The next proposition isolates that path and computes its gain exactly.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before the proof: with the gates held fixed, the cell update is $\mathbf c_t = \mathbf f_t\odot\mathbf c_{t-1} + \text{const}$. What is the Jacobian of an elementwise product with a fixed vector?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Constant error carousel" id="prop-tf2-cec">
Fix $t < T$ and differentiate along the **cell path** — the path $\mathbf c_t \to \mathbf c_{t+1} \to \cdots \to \mathbf c_T$ in the unrolled graph that never passes through a hidden state (equivalently, hold the gate and candidate activations fixed). The Jacobian contribution of this path is
$$
\left.\frac{\partial \mathbf c_T}{\partial \mathbf c_t}\right|_{\text{cell path}}
= \prod_{s=t+1}^{T} \operatorname{diag}(\mathbf f_s)
= \operatorname{diag}\!\Big(\bigodot_{s=t+1}^{T} \mathbf f_s\Big),
$$
whose $i$-th diagonal entry is $\prod_{s=t+1}^{T} [\mathbf f_s]_i \in (0,1)$. The factor involves no weight matrix and no $\varphi'$: saturating the forget gates open drives the gain arbitrarily close to $1$ across an arbitrary gap $T - t$ — and in the original forget-gate-free cell of <Cite key="hochreiter1997lstm" />, whose update is $\mathbf c_t = \mathbf c_{t-1} + \mathbf i_t\odot\tilde{\mathbf c}_t$, the path gain is exactly $1$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (the update is affine along the path).** With $\mathbf f_s$, $\mathbf i_s$, $\tilde{\mathbf c}_s$ held fixed — they are functions of $(\mathbf x_s, \mathbf h_{s-1})$, and the cell path excludes the hidden states — the update $\mathbf c_s = \mathbf f_s\odot\mathbf c_{s-1} + \mathbf i_s\odot\tilde{\mathbf c}_s$ is affine in $\mathbf c_{s-1}$. The map $\mathbf c \mapsto \mathbf f_s\odot\mathbf c$ is linear with matrix $\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf f_s)$, so the path's one-step Jacobian is $\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf f_s)$.

**Step 2 (compose).** Diagonal matrices multiply entrywise: the path Jacobian across the gap is $\prod_s \operatorname{diag}(\mathbf f_s) = \operatorname{diag}(\bigodot_s \mathbf f_s)$, with entries $\prod_s [\mathbf f_s]_i$; each lies in $(0,1)$ since every $[\mathbf f_s]_i = \sigma(\cdot) \in (0,1)$, and approaches $1$ as the gates saturate open.

**Step 3 (contrast with the bound).** No factor is a weight matrix and no factor is a derivative of a squashing nonlinearity, so the mechanism of <XRef id="thm-tf2-gradient-bound" /> — a forced product of $\mathbf W_h^\top\operatorname{diag}(\varphi')$ terms — is structurally absent on this path. The gain is not a fixed constant $\rho^{T-t}$ but a learned, per-coordinate quantity the network sets by its gates. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

The proposition is honest about its scope: the *total* Jacobian $\partial\mathbf c_T/\partial\mathbf c_t$ adds further paths that pass through hidden states into the gates, and those paths do carry weight-matrix products. What the LSTM guarantees is the *existence* of one additive highway whose gain the network controls pointwise — enough to transport error across long gaps, and the architectural ancestor of the residual stream that will play the same role in the depth direction of the transformer.

The GRU compresses the same remedy into a single state vector: one **update gate** divides each coordinate between keeping the old state and writing a candidate, and a **reset gate** controls how much of the past the candidate may read.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Gated recurrent unit (GRU)" id="def-tf2-gru">
The **GRU** merges the cell and hidden state into one vector, using two gates:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf z_t &\defeq \sigma(\mathbf W_z\mathbf x_t + \mathbf U_z\mathbf h_{t-1} + \mathbf b_z) && \text{(update gate)},\\
\mathbf r_t &\defeq \sigma(\mathbf W_r\mathbf x_t + \mathbf U_r\mathbf h_{t-1} + \mathbf b_r) && \text{(reset gate)},\\
\tilde{\mathbf h}_t &\defeq \tanh\!\big(\mathbf W_c\mathbf x_t + \mathbf U_c(\mathbf r_t\odot\mathbf h_{t-1}) + \mathbf b_c\big) && \text{(candidate)},
\end{aligned}
$$
with the state update the gated interpolation
$$
\mathbf h_t \defeq \mathbf z_t\odot\mathbf h_{t-1} + (\mathbf 1 - \mathbf z_t)\odot\tilde{\mathbf h}_t .
$$
<Sidenote>Introduced by <Cite key="cho2014gru" />, in the same work that introduced the RNN encoder–decoder of the next section.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

The GRU update is an *entrywise convex combination*: each coordinate of $\mathbf h_t$ lies between the corresponding coordinates of $\mathbf h_{t-1}$ and $\tilde{\mathbf h}_t$ — the convex-combination tool of the notation chapter (<XRef id="prop-row-stochastic-convex" />) reappearing with weights $(z, 1 - z)$ per coordinate. Its carousel is Exercise 2.4: along the direct path the one-step gain is $\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf z_t)$, so $\mathbf z_t \approx \mathbf 1$ holds the state — and the gradient — almost unchanged. This convex-interpolation form is worth remembering; it returns near the end of the arc as the exact scalar reduction of a selective state space update.

## Sequence-to-sequence and the bottleneck

Gates fix the gradient; they do not enlarge the state. We now assemble recurrent networks into the architecture that exposed the state itself as the limiting resource. The task is sequence *transduction* — map an input sequence to an output sequence of a different length, as in translation — and the natural recurrent design reads the input once, summarizes it, and generates from the summary.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Sequence-to-sequence encoder–decoder" id="def-tf2-seq2seq">
A **seq2seq** model is a pair of recurrent networks (of any cell type above). The **encoder** reads the input $\mathbf x_1,\dots,\mathbf x_n$ and produces states $\mathbf h_1,\dots,\mathbf h_n\in\R^{d_s}$; the **context vector** is its final state,
$$
\mathbf c \defeq \mathbf h_n \in\R^{d_s}.
$$
The **decoder** is a recurrent network over its own outputs, initialized at $\mathbf s_0 = \mathbf c$, which generates $y_1,\dots,y_m$ autoregressively:
$$
\mathbf s_t \defeq \varphi\big(\mathbf W_s\mathbf s_{t-1} + \mathbf W_y \Emb(y_{t-1}) + \mathbf b'\big),
\qquad
p(y_t \mid y_{<t}, \mathbf x_{1:n}) \defeq \softmax(\mathbf W_{\text{out}}\,\mathbf s_t)_{y_t},
$$
with $y_0$ a distinguished start symbol of the output vocabulary and generation halting at a distinguished end symbol, which determines $m$. By construction, the joint conditional $p(y_{1:m}\mid \mathbf x_{1:n})$ depends on the input **only through** $\mathbf c$.<Sidenote>The RNN encoder–decoder is due to <Cite key="cho2014gru" />; its scaled realization for translation to <Cite key="sutskever2014seq2seq" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<Figure
  src="/figures/seq2seq-bottleneck.svg"
  caption="The seq2seq bottleneck. Every path from the input sequence to every decoder step passes through the single fixed-dimensional context vector c = h_n: an hourglass whose neck does not widen as n grows. Rendered from TikZ via the figures pipeline."
  id="fig-seq2seq-bottleneck"
  width="75%"
/>

The last clause of the definition is the architecture's signature and its flaw. The input may be arbitrarily long; the interface it must pass through has dimension $d_s$, fixed before the data arrive. Counting is enough to show this is a genuine — not merely aesthetic — limitation.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="The fixed-vector bottleneck" id="prop-tf2-bottleneck">
Fix the state dimension $d_s$ and a machine representation of $b$ bits per coordinate, and let the input vocabulary be $\mathcal V$. Any encoder computes a map $\mathcal V^{\,n}\to\R^{d_s}$ whose image contains at most $2^{b\,d_s}$ distinct context vectors. If
$$
n \,\log_2\lvert\mathcal V\rvert \;>\; b\,d_s,
$$
then there exist two distinct inputs $\mathbf x \ne \mathbf x'$ with $\mathbf c(\mathbf x) = \mathbf c(\mathbf x')$, and every decoder conditioned only on the context assigns them identical output distributions: $p(\cdot\mid\mathbf x) = p(\cdot\mid\mathbf x')$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (count the codes).** A context vector has $d_s$ coordinates of $b$ bits each, hence at most $2^{b d_s}$ representable values.

**Step 2 (count the inputs).** There are $\lvert\mathcal V\rvert^n = 2^{n\log_2\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}$ distinct input sequences of length $n$.

**Step 3 (pigeonhole and factorization).** If $2^{n\log_2\lvert\mathcal V\rvert} > 2^{b d_s}$, the map cannot be injective: two distinct inputs share a context. By <XRef id="def-tf2-seq2seq" /> the decoder's conditional factors through $\mathbf c$, so the two inputs receive the same conditional distribution over every output sequence. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="An instance">$d_s = 1000$, fp32 ($b = 32$), $\lvert\mathcal V\rvert = 2^{15}$: collisions are forced once $n > 32000/15 \approx 2133$, i.e. at $n = 2134$ tokens.</MarginNote>

The proposition is a worst-case identity statement — it exhibits two inputs the decoder *cannot* treat differently, however it is trained — and in practice the degradation arrives far earlier: empirically, fixed-vector translation quality falls off well before the counting threshold, on sentences of a few dozen words.<Sidenote>The length degradation and its remedy are the motivating observations of <Cite key="bahdanau2015attention" />.</Sidenote> The same counting argument returns at the end of the arc, sharpened into a recall bound that prices *any* fixed-size decode state against the growing cache it replaces. Here, its 2015 resolution created the object this guide is about.

## Additive attention

If one vector is too narrow an interface, let the decoder look back at *all* the encoder states — but not uniformly: at each output step it should select what is currently relevant. The selection mechanism must be differentiable, so instead of choosing one position it forms a *soft* choice: score every encoder state against the decoder's current state, normalize the scores to a distribution with $\softmax$, and take the resulting convex combination. Relevance is judged by *content*, not by position — the decoder addresses the input by what it says, and the fixed context $\mathbf c$ is replaced by a fresh, query-dependent context at every step.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Additive (Bahdanau) attention" id="def-tf2-additive-attention">
Let $\mathbf h_1,\dots,\mathbf h_n\in\R^{d_s}$ be encoder states and $\mathbf s_{t-1}\in\R^{d_s}$ the decoder state before emitting output $t$. Fix an alignment dimension $d_a$ and parameters $\mathbf W_a, \mathbf U_a\in\R^{d_a\times d_s}$, $\mathbf v_a\in\R^{d_a}$. The **alignment scores**, **attention weights**, and **context vector** at step $t$ are
$$
e_{tj} \defeq \mathbf v_a^\top\tanh\!\big(\mathbf W_a\mathbf s_{t-1} + \mathbf U_a\mathbf h_j\big),
\qquad
\alpha_{t} \defeq \softmax\big(e_{t1},\dots,e_{tn}\big),
\qquad
\mathbf c_t \defeq \sum_{j=1}^{n} \alpha_{tj}\,\mathbf h_j,
$$
and the decoder update consumes its own context, $\mathbf s_t \defeq \varphi\big(\mathbf W_s\mathbf s_{t-1} + \mathbf W_y\Emb(y_{t-1}) + \mathbf W_{\!c}\,\mathbf c_t + \mathbf b'\big)$. From here on $\mathbf c_t$ denotes this per-step context — the fixed seq2seq context $\mathbf c$ made query-dependent; the LSTM cell state of <XRef id="def-tf2-lstm" /> does not appear again in this chapter.<Sidenote>Introduced by <Cite key="bahdanau2015attention" /> as "learning to align and translate."</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

Each weight vector $\alpha_t$ is a probability distribution over input positions (<XRef id="def-softmax" />), so $\mathbf c_t$ is a convex combination of the encoder states (<XRef id="prop-row-stochastic-convex" />) — stacking the $\alpha_t$ as rows gives a row-stochastic matrix, our third tool returning in its natural habitat. The bottleneck of <XRef id="prop-tf2-bottleneck" /> is dissolved, not merely widened: the decoder's interface to the input now *grows with the input*, one $d_s$-vector per position, and the pigeonhole count no longer applies because no fixed-dimensional summary sits between encoder and decoder.

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="What addressing needs">The scoring–normalizing–averaging machinery consumes one query vector and $n$ key vectors. Nothing in it requires that those vectors come from a recurrence.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Keep the addressing, drop the recurrence" id="rem-tf2-toward-attention">
Look at what <XRef id="def-tf2-additive-attention" /> uses and what it does not. The addressing core — score, $\softmax$, convex combination — makes no use of recurrence at all: it consumes a query vector ($\mathbf s_{t-1}$) and a set of candidate vectors ($\mathbf h_j$), and the two recurrent networks serve only to *manufacture* those vectors, at the cost of the sequential chain and its geometric gradients (<XRef id="thm-tf2-gradient-bound" />). The move that defines the rest of this guide is to keep the content addressing and delete the manufacturer: let learned linear projections produce queries, keys, and values directly from the embedded sequence, replace the additive score $\mathbf v_a^\top\tanh(\mathbf W_a\mathbf s + \mathbf U_a\mathbf h)$ by a scaled dot product, and let the addressing itself do all of the mixing — scaled dot-product attention (<XRef id="def-tf4-attention" />), the subject of Part 3. Before taking that step, the next chapter follows the other branch of the fork: keep the recurrence, but make it *linear*, so that the chain can be computed in parallel and its timescales controlled — a branch that rejoins this one, selective and content-aware, at the end of the arc.
</Theorem>

## Exercises

Attempt each before expanding its solution.

**Exercise 2.1 (Compute).** Write out the unrolled two-step expression for $h_2$ from <XRef id="def-tf2-rnn" />, identifying the tied occurrences of $(w_h, w_x, b)$. Then rerun the trace of <XRef id="ex-tf2-scalar-trace" /> with $w_h = 0.8$, $w_x = 1$, $b = 0$, $h_0 = 0$, $\varphi = \tanh$, and inputs $x_1 = 0.5$, $x_2 = -0.5$: compute $h_1$, $h_2$, and $\partial h_2/\partial h_0$, and verify the bound of <XRef id="thm-tf2-gradient-bound" />.

<SolutionBox>

**Unrolling.** $h_2 = \varphi\big(w_h\,\varphi(w_h h_0 + w_x x_1 + b) + w_x x_2 + b\big)$: the same triple $(w_h, w_x, b)$ appears at both depths — a depth-$2$ feed-forward network with tied weights, per <XRef id="def-tf2-rnn" />.

**Forward.** $a_1 = 0.5$, so $h_1 = \tanh(0.5) \approx 0.46212$ and $\varphi'(a_1) = 1 - h_1^2 \approx 0.78645$. Then $a_2 = 0.8\cdot 0.46212 - 0.5 = -0.13031$, so $h_2 = \tanh(-0.13031) \approx -0.12957$ and $\varphi'(a_2) = 1 - h_2^2 \approx 0.98321$.

**Backward.** By <XRef id="thm-tf2-bptt" />(i),
$$
\frac{\partial h_2}{\partial h_0} = \varphi'(a_2)\,w_h\cdot\varphi'(a_1)\,w_h
= 0.98321\cdot 0.8\cdot 0.78645\cdot 0.8 \approx 0.49488 .
$$
The bound gives $(\gamma\,\sigma_{\max})^2 = (1\cdot 0.8)^2 = 0.64 \ge 0.49488$ — satisfied, and not tight, because the visited slopes $\varphi'(a_s)$ fall below the cap $\gamma = 1$. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 2.2 (Derive).** From <XRef id="thm-tf2-bptt" />(ii), derive the backward recurrence of part (iii) — $\mathbf g_t = \nabla_{\mathbf h_t} L_t + \mathbf W_h^\top\operatorname{diag}(\varphi'(\mathbf a_{t+1}))\,\mathbf g_{t+1}$ — and show that the full gradient computation costs $O\!\big(n\,d_s(d_s + d)\big)$ arithmetic, i.e. one backward pass, not one pass per pair $(s,t)$.

<SolutionBox>

Define $\mathbf g_s \defeq \sum_{t\ge s} (\partial\mathbf h_t/\partial\mathbf h_s)^\top \nabla_{\mathbf h_t} L_t$, the inner sum of (ii) without the leading diagonal factor. Split off $t = s$ (where the Jacobian is $\mathbf I$) and factor every remaining term through the first hop $\partial\mathbf h_{s+1}/\partial\mathbf h_s = \operatorname{diag}(\varphi'(\mathbf a_{s+1}))\mathbf W_h$:
$$
\mathbf g_s = \nabla_{\mathbf h_s} L_s + \mathbf W_h^\top\operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_{s+1})\big)\sum_{t\ge s+1}\left(\frac{\partial\mathbf h_t}{\partial\mathbf h_{s+1}}\right)^{\!\top}\nabla_{\mathbf h_t} L_t
= \nabla_{\mathbf h_s} L_s + \mathbf W_h^\top\operatorname{diag}\!\big(\varphi'(\mathbf a_{s+1})\big)\,\mathbf g_{s+1}.
$$
Each backward step costs one matrix–vector product and a diagonal scaling, $O(d_s^2)$; the per-step accumulations $\operatorname{diag}(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s))\mathbf g_s\mathbf h_{s-1}^\top$ and $\operatorname{diag}(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s))\mathbf g_s\mathbf x_s^\top$ are outer products of cost $O(d_s^2)$ and $O(d_s d)$. Over $n$ steps the total is $O(n\,d_s(d_s + d))$ — linear in $n$, though the double sum of (ii) has $\Theta(n^2)$ terms, because the recurrence shares every suffix product. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 2.3 (Prove).** Prove $\norm{\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf v)}_2 = \max_i\lvert v_i\rvert$, complete the second inequality of <XRef id="thm-tf2-gradient-bound" />, and conclude that a logistic-sigmoid RNN ($\varphi = \sigma$) has vanishing gradients whenever $\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h) < 4$.

<SolutionBox>

For any $\mathbf x$, $\norm{\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf v)\mathbf x}^2 = \sum_i v_i^2 x_i^2 \le (\max_i v_i^2)\norm{\mathbf x}^2$, and the standard basis vector at a maximizing index attains equality, so $\norm{\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf v)}_2 = \max_i\lvert v_i\rvert$. Then, by submultiplicativity and transpose invariance of the spectral norm,
$$
\norm{\mathbf W_h^\top\operatorname{diag}(\varphi'(\mathbf a_s))}_2 \le \sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h)\max_i\lvert\varphi'([\mathbf a_s]_i)\rvert \le \sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h)\,\gamma,
$$
and the product over $s = t+1,\dots,T$ gives $(\gamma\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h))^{T-t}$. For the logistic function, $\sigma' = \sigma(1-\sigma) \le 1/4$ (maximized at $\sigma = 1/2$), so $\gamma = 1/4$ and $\gamma\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h) < 1$ holds whenever $\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h) < 4$; by <XRef id="thm-tf2-gradient-bound" />(i) the gradient then vanishes exponentially. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 2.4 (Prove).** For the GRU (<XRef id="def-tf2-gru" />): show that $\mathbf h_t$ is an entrywise convex combination of $\mathbf h_{t-1}$ and $\tilde{\mathbf h}_t$; show that along the direct path (gates and candidate held fixed) the one-step Jacobian is $\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf z_t)$; and conclude the GRU's analogue of <XRef id="prop-tf2-cec" />.

<SolutionBox>

Coordinatewise, $[\mathbf h_t]_i = [\mathbf z_t]_i[\mathbf h_{t-1}]_i + (1 - [\mathbf z_t]_i)[\tilde{\mathbf h}_t]_i$ with $[\mathbf z_t]_i = \sigma(\cdot)\in(0,1)$: nonnegative weights summing to one, hence a convex combination per coordinate, so $[\mathbf h_t]_i$ lies between $[\mathbf h_{t-1}]_i$ and $[\tilde{\mathbf h}_t]_i$. Holding $\mathbf z_t$ and $\tilde{\mathbf h}_t$ fixed, the update is affine in $\mathbf h_{t-1}$ with linear part $\mathbf h\mapsto\mathbf z_t\odot\mathbf h$, whose matrix is $\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf z_t)$. Composing across a gap, the direct-path Jacobian is $\operatorname{diag}\big(\bigodot_{s}\mathbf z_s\big)$ — diagonal, weight-free, and $\varphi'$-free, with entries approaching $1$ as $\mathbf z_s\to\mathbf 1$ (the "copy" setting). This is the GRU's constant error carousel: one gate plays both the forget and input roles, with $\mathbf f = \mathbf z$ tied to $\mathbf i = \mathbf 1 - \mathbf z$. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 2.5 (Count).** A seq2seq model has $d_s = 512$ and stores its context in fp16 ($b = 16$) over a vocabulary of size $\lvert\mathcal V\rvert = 2^{16}$. Count the distinct representable contexts and the input sequences of length $n$, and find the smallest $n$ at which <XRef id="prop-tf2-bottleneck" /> forces a collision.

<SolutionBox>

The context takes at most $2^{b d_s} = 2^{16\cdot 512} = 2^{8192}$ values; there are $\lvert\mathcal V\rvert^n = 2^{16n}$ inputs of length $n$. A collision is forced when $16n > 8192$, i.e. $n > 512$: at $n = 513$, two distinct 513-token inputs must share a context vector, and the decoder must emit identical distributions for them. (The threshold $n = b\,d_s/\log_2\lvert\mathcal V\rvert = 512$ tokens is small by modern standards — and the practical failure precedes the counting failure by a wide margin.) $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 2.6 (Compute).** At some decoder step the alignment scores against $n = 3$ scalar encoder states $h_1 = 2$, $h_2 = 0$, $h_3 = -1$ come out as $e = (2, 0, -1)$. Compute the attention weights $\alpha$ (numerically stably) and the context $c = \sum_j \alpha_j h_j$, and verify the convex-combination property of <XRef id="prop-row-stochastic-convex" />.

<SolutionBox>

Subtract the maximum score $2$ before exponentiating (exact by translation invariance, <XRef id="prop-softmax-translation-inv" />): $e - 2\mathbf 1 = (0, -2, -3)$ exponentiates to $(1, e^{-2}, e^{-3}) \approx (1, 0.1353, 0.0498)$ with sum $\approx 1.1851$, so
$$
\alpha \approx (0.8438,\ 0.1142,\ 0.0420).
$$
The context is $c = 0.8438\cdot 2 + 0.1142\cdot 0 + 0.0420\cdot(-1) \approx 1.6456$, which lies in the convex hull $[-1, 2]$ of the encoder states, as <XRef id="prop-row-stochastic-convex" /> requires: attention interpolates and cannot reach outside the hull of what it attends to. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
