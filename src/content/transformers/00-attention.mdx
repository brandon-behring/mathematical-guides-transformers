# The Attention Mechanism

**Where we are.** Attention is the transformer's one genuinely new operation. The idea is simple — each token should look at every other token and aggregate selectively — but the construction rewards care. We build it from one nonlinearity (softmax), one similarity (the dot product), and one piece of normalization (the $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling), and we prove the property that makes each true.

**Notation.** Vectors are bold lowercase **row** vectors $\mathbf x \in \R^{d}$; matrices are bold uppercase. $\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}$ is the Euclidean inner product, $\softmax$ the normalized exponential, and $d_k$ the per-head key dimension.

## Softmax

<Theorem kind="definition" n="1" name="Softmax">
For $\mathbf z = (z_1,\dots,z_K)\in\R^K$, the **softmax** $\softmax:\R^K\to\R^K$ is
$$
\softmax(\mathbf z)_i \defeq \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} e^{z_j}}.
$$
Each output is positive and the entries sum to $1$, so $\softmax(\mathbf z)$ is a probability distribution over $K$ categories.<Sidenote>This is the standard probabilistic reading of network logits <Cite key="bridle1990probabilistic" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" n="1" name="Translation invariance" id="prop-softmax-translation">
For any scalar $c\in\R$, $\softmax(\mathbf z + c\mathbf 1) = \softmax(\mathbf z)$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Multiply numerator and denominator of entry $i$ by $e^{c}$ and cancel:
$$
\softmax(\mathbf z + c\mathbf 1)_i
= \frac{e^{z_i + c}}{\sum_j e^{z_j + c}}
= \frac{e^{c}e^{z_i}}{e^{c}\sum_j e^{z_j}}
= \softmax(\mathbf z)_i. \qquad\blacksquare
$$
This is why subtracting $\max_j z_j$ before exponentiating is numerically safe — it changes nothing.
</Theorem>

## Scaled dot-product attention

<Theorem kind="definition" n="2" name="Scaled dot-product attention">
Given queries $\mathbf Q\in\R^{n\times d_k}$, keys $\mathbf K\in\R^{n\times d_k}$, and values $\mathbf V\in\R^{n\times d_v}$,
$$
\operatorname{Attn}(\mathbf Q,\mathbf K,\mathbf V) \defeq \softmax\!\Big(\tfrac{\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\Big)\,\mathbf V,
$$
with softmax applied row-wise to the $n\times n$ logit matrix.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="note" label="Known-math anchor">
Because each row of $\softmax(\cdot)$ is non-negative and sums to one, output row $i$ is a **convex combination** of the value rows — i.e. attention is a data-dependent **Nadaraya–Watson kernel-weighted average**, with kernel $\exp(\inner{\mathbf q_i}{\mathbf k_j}/\sqrt{d_k})$. The map is exact: *kernel weights* ↦ *attention row*, *kernel regression estimate* ↦ *attention output*. Where it breaks: the "kernel" is learned and need not be symmetric or positive-definite, so classical kernel-smoothing guarantees do not transfer.
</MarginNote>

## Why the $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling

Without scaling, dot-product logits grow with $d_k$ and push softmax into saturation. The scaling is chosen to *exactly* cancel that growth.

<Theorem kind="theorem" n="1" name="Variance preservation" id="thm-variance-preservation">
Let $\mathbf q,\mathbf k\in\R^{d_k}$ have independent components with $\E[q_m]=\E[k_m]=0$, $\Var(q_m)=\Var(k_m)=1$, and $q_m \perp k_m$. Then $\E[\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}]=0$, $\Var(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k})=d_k$, and $\Var\!\big(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}/\sqrt{d_k}\big)=1$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Three steps; each names the rule it uses.

**Mean.** By linearity and independence,
$$
\E\Big[\textstyle\sum_{m} q_m k_m\Big] = \sum_m \E[q_m]\,\E[k_m] = 0
\qquad\text{(linearity; } q_m\perp k_m\text{)}.
$$

**Variance.** Since the mean is $0$, $\Var(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}) = \E[(\sum_m q_m k_m)^2]$. Expanding, the cross terms $\E[q_m k_m q_n k_n]$ with $m\neq n$ factor into a product containing a mean-zero factor and vanish, leaving
$$
\sum_m \E[q_m^2]\,\E[k_m^2] = \sum_{m=1}^{d_k} 1\cdot 1 = d_k
\qquad\text{(independence; } \Var=\E[\,\cdot\,^2]\text{ at mean }0).
$$

**Scaling.** $\Var$ is $2$-homogeneous, so dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ divides the variance by $d_k$:
$$
\Var\!\Big(\tfrac{\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\Big) = \tfrac{1}{d_k}\,\Var(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}) = \tfrac{d_k}{d_k} = 1. \qquad\blacksquare
$$
</Theorem>

So $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ is not a tuning constant — it is the unique scale that holds logit variance at $1$ as $d_k$ grows, which is what keeps softmax gradients alive <Cite key="vaswani2017attention" />.
