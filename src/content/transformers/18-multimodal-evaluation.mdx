# Multimodal Evaluation

**Where we are.** This short capstone makes good on the dual-footing thesis one last time, now for *measurement*. The metrics that score vision-language models look modality-specific â€” imageâ€“text retrieval recall, captioning perplexity, zero-shot classification accuracy â€” but each is a quantity we already defined for text or for detection, evaluated on cross-modal inputs. Retrieval is a ranking problem, and its headline number, recall@1, is exactly the in-batch accuracy of the contrastive objective (<XRef id="def-tf7-contrastive" />); its pairwise form is the AUROC rank statistic (<XRef id="prop-tf7-auroc-rank" />). Captioning quality is perplexity, the exponential of the conditional cross-entropy of the training chapter (<XRef id="def-tf6-ce-loss" />), and minimizing it minimizes a conditional KL â€” so it is a proper scoring rule, while the surface-overlap metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, SPICE) are not. Zero-shot classification is an encoder-only softmax classifier (<XRef id="def-tf7-encoder-classifier" />) whose weight columns are supplied, with no label training, by the text encoder, and it is correct precisely because the contrastive objective aligned the modalities on a shared sphere (<XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-alignment" />). We write $r(q)$ for a retrieval rank, $\mathrm{PPL}$ for perplexity, and $\mathbf t_c$ for a class-name text embedding.

## Retrieval is a ranking statistic

Cross-modal retrieval ranks candidates of one modality by similarity to a query of the other â€” find the caption for an image, or the image for a caption. The metrics are the standard ones; the content of this section is that they are not new.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Retrieval rank, recall@k, and MRR" id="def-tf13-retrieval">
For a query $q$ and a candidate pool $\{c_1,\dots,c_N\}$ with similarity scores $s(q,c_j)$ (e.g. the cosine of <XRef id="def-tf7-cosine-temp" />), rank the candidates in decreasing score and let $r(q)\in\{1,\dots,N\}$ be the rank of the true match. (This chapter reuses $N$ for the pool size and $c$ for candidates and class indices â€” stated departures from the standing table, where they are the block count and channel count.) Over a query set $\mathcal Q$,
$$
\mathrm{recall}@k \defeq \frac{1}{|\mathcal Q|}\sum_{q\in\mathcal Q}\mathbf 1\{r(q)\le k\},
\qquad
\mathrm{MRR} \defeq \frac{1}{|\mathcal Q|}\sum_{q\in\mathcal Q}\frac{1}{r(q)},
$$
the fraction of queries whose true match falls in the top $k$, and the mean reciprocal rank.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Recall@1 is in-batch contrastive accuracy; the pairwise hit is AUROC" id="prop-tf13-recall-accuracy">
Evaluate retrieval over a batch of $B$ matched pairs with the similarity matrix $\mathbf M$ of <XRef id="def-tf7-contrastive" />, query $i$ ranking the $B$ candidates by $M_{i,:}$.
1. **Recall@1 = accuracy.** $\mathrm{recall}@1$ for query $i$ is $\mathbf 1\{\argmax_j M_{ij}=i\}$, which is exactly the top-1 correctness of the in-batch softmax classifier of <XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-ce" />. Averaged over the batch, retrieval recall@1 equals in-batch contrastive accuracy.
2. **Pairwise hit = AUROC.** The probability that a *random* matched score exceeds a *random* mismatched score â€” positives $\{M_{ii}\}$, negatives $\{M_{i'j} : j\ne i'\}$, the two drawn independently (cross-row comparisons included) â€” is the Mannâ€“Whitney/AUROC statistic of <XRef id="prop-tf7-auroc-rank" />. It is a different statistic from recall@1: a relaxation counting every pairwise ordering, not just the per-row top spot.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
(1) Recall@1 fires for query $i$ iff the top-ranked candidate is the true match, i.e. $\argmax_j M_{ij}=i$. The in-batch classifier of <XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-ce" /> predicts $\hat\jmath(i)=\argmax_j p(j\mid i)=\argmax_j\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j=\argmax_j M_{ij}$, the last equality because softmax is strictly increasing and so preserves the argmax (under any fixed tie-breaking rule applied identically to both sides). Thus the recall@1 indicator equals the indicator that the classifier's top-1 prediction is correct; averaging over $i$ gives accuracy. (2) Take positives $\{M_{ii}\}_{i=1}^{B}$ and negatives $\{M_{i'j}:j\ne i'\}$ and apply <XRef id="prop-tf7-auroc-rank" />: the empirical AUROC is the normalized count over all $B \cdot B(B-1)$ matchedâ€“mismatched pairs (cross-row pairs $M_{ii}$ vs $M_{i'j}$, $i'\ne i$, included, with the $\tfrac12$ tie term) â€” the probability a random true match outranks a random distractor. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Two names, one relaxation">In-batch contrastive accuracy and retrieval recall@1 are the same number read two ways; the pooled AUROC is its pairwise-ranking relaxation â€” the cross-modal payoff of <XRef id="rem-tf7-crossmodal-canonical" />.</MarginNote>

## Captioning quality is a likelihood

A caption model is an autoregressive conditional $p_\theta(\mathbf y\mid \mathbf I)$ (the captioning example of <XRef id="ex-tf6-captioning" />). The intrinsic measure of such a model is its held-out likelihood, reported as perplexity â€” and this is a likelihood we already understand.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Conditional perplexity" id="def-tf13-perplexity">
For a conditional model $p_\theta(\mathbf y\mid\mathbf c)$ over a target sequence $\mathbf y=(y_1,\dots,y_T)$, the **perplexity** is the exponential of the per-token conditional cross-entropy,
$$
\mathrm{PPL}(\mathbf y\mid\mathbf c) \defeq \exp\!\Big(\!-\frac1T\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{<t},\mathbf c)\Big) = \exp\big(\widehat H\big),
$$
where $\widehat H$ denotes the empirical cross-entropy loss â€” ch. 6's $\mathcal L(\theta)$ (<XRef id="def-tf6-ce-loss" />), renamed to emphasize it is an empirical entropy estimate â€” with conditioning enlarged from $y_{<t}$ to $(y_{<t},\mathbf c)$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Minimizing perplexity minimizes conditional KL" id="prop-tf13-perplexity-kl">
Since $\exp$ is strictly increasing, $\mathrm{PPL}$ and the per-token cross-entropy $\widehat H$ have the same minimizer over $\theta$. In expectation over the data, $\widehat H$ equals the conditional KL from the data to the model plus the data's conditional entropy (<XRef id="prop-tf6-ce-kl-training" />), the latter independent of $\theta$. Hence minimizing the expected log-loss minimizes the conditional KL $D_{\mathrm{KL}}\big(p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\,\|\,p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\big)$: the log-loss is a **proper scoring rule**, uniquely minimized in expectation by the true conditional, and perplexity â€” reported as $\exp$ *of the expected loss* â€” inherits that minimizer. (The order matters: $\E[\exp\widehat H]$, expectation outside, is generally minimized elsewhere.)
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
$\mathrm{PPL}=\exp(\widehat H)$ with $\widehat H$ strictly increasing-transformed, so $\argmin_\theta\mathrm{PPL}=\argmin_\theta\widehat H$. Taking expectation over targets drawn from $p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)$ at a context $\mathbf c$ in the data support, the per-token cross-entropy decomposes as $\E[\widehat H]=H\big(p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\big)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}\big(p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\,\Vert\,p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\big)$ by <XRef id="prop-tf6-ce-kl-training" />. The entropy term does not depend on $\theta$, and KL is nonnegative with equality iff $p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)$, so the expected loss is minimized exactly at the true conditional â€” the proper-scoring-rule property of the log-loss. Perplexity, as the monotone $\exp$ applied to that expected loss, shares the minimizer. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Surface-overlap metrics are not proper scoring rules" id="rem-tf13-surface-metrics">
The standard captioning metrics â€” BLEU's $n$-gram precision, CIDEr's tf-idf $n$-gram cosine, SPICE's scene-graph $F$-score <Cite key="papineni2002bleu" /><Cite key="vedantam2015cider" /><Cite key="anderson2016spice" /> â€” score the *surface overlap* of a generated string with references, not the model's probability distribution. They are not proper scoring rules: a model can raise them by matching reference $n$-grams without its distribution approaching $p_{\mathrm{data}}$, and they are not minimized in expectation only by the true distribution. They correlate with human judgment imperfectly and are gameable, which is why intrinsic perplexity (<XRef id="prop-tf13-perplexity-kl" />) and task accuracies (retrieval, zero-shot) are reported alongside them. The honest reading is that overlap metrics measure resemblance to references, not calibration of the model.
</Theorem>

## Zero-shot classification is a softmax classifier with text weights

The most striking evaluation of a contrastive model uses no training labels at all: to classify an image among $C$ named classes, embed the class *names* with the text encoder and pick the nearest. We show this is literally the encoder classifier of the detection chapter, with its head handed over by the text tower.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Zero-shot classification" id="def-tf13-zeroshot">
Given class names with text embeddings $\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_C\in\R^{d}$ (each the pooled encoding of a prompt like "a photo of a $\langle$class$\rangle$", <XRef id="def-tf7-pooled-rep" />) and an image embedding $\mathbf v\in\R^d$ â€” all embeddings $L^2$-normalized, as in the contrastive training of <XRef id="def-tf7-contrastive" />, so inner products are cosines â€” the **zero-shot classifier** predicts
$$
\hat y \defeq \argmax_{c\in\{1,\dots,C\}} \inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_c},
\qquad
p(c\mid\mathbf v) \defeq \softmax\!\big(\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_1}/\tau,\dots,\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_C}/\tau\big)_c,
$$
using no target-domain labels â€” the class representations come entirely from the text encoder.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Zero-shot is an encoder classifier with text-embedding head" id="prop-tf13-zeroshot-classifier">
The zero-shot classifier of <XRef id="def-tf13-zeroshot" /> is the encoder-only classifier of <XRef id="def-tf7-encoder-classifier" /> applied to the image embedding $\mathbf v$, with classifier head $\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}=[\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_C]\in\R^{d\times C}$ (columns the class-name text embeddings) and zero bias. It needs no label training because the head is produced by the text encoder. Its *motivation* is the contrastive geometry: the training optimum (<XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-alignment" />) aligns matched imageâ€“caption pairs and separates mismatched ones, and class-name prompts inherit that alignment to the extent they resemble training captions â€” a transfer heuristic validated empirically <Cite key="radford2021clip" />, not a correctness theorem.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
With $\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}=[\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_C]$ and bias zero, the encoder-classifier logits of <XRef id="def-tf7-encoder-classifier" /> are $\mathbf o=\mathbf v\,\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}$ with $o_c=\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_c}$, and $\hat y=\argmax_c o_c$ is exactly the zero-shot *decision* of <XRef id="def-tf13-zeroshot" /> for every $\tau>0$ (dividing logits by $\tau$ is a monotone rescaling); the probabilistic outputs coincide at $\tau=1$, the ch. 7 classifier carrying no temperature. The head columns are the text encoder's outputs on the class names, not parameters fit to target labels, so no label training occurs. For the alignment reading: the symmetric contrastive objective (<XRef id="def-tf7-contrastive" />) at its minimizer drives matched *training-pair* cosines up and mismatched ones down (<XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-alignment" />); insofar as a class-name prompt embeds near its class's caption cluster, $\argmax_c\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_c}$ recovers the true class. That transfer step is the zero-shot leap â€” empirical, not proved. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Every metric reduced" id="rem-tf13-all-reduced">
The capstone closes the loop. Retrieval recall and its pairwise form reduced to in-batch accuracy and the AUROC rank statistic of the detection chapter (<XRef id="prop-tf13-recall-accuracy" />); perplexity is the conditional cross-entropy and KL of the training chapter (<XRef id="prop-tf13-perplexity-kl" />); zero-shot accuracy is the encoder classifier of the detection chapter with a text-supplied head (<XRef id="prop-tf13-zeroshot-classifier" />). No vision-language metric needed new probability theory â€” only the choice of which already-proven statistic to apply to cross-modal embeddings and likelihoods. This is the evaluation-side statement of the dual-footing thesis (<XRef id="rem-tf1-modality-blind" />): the same mathematics scores the same model, whatever the modality of its inputs. The original contrastive and zero-shot constructions are CLIP's <Cite key="radford2021clip" />; the retrieval-as-ranking protocol originates with Hodosh, Young, and Hockenmaier <Cite key="hodosh2013framing" />, adopted for alignment models by <Cite key="karpathy2015deepvisual" />.
</Theorem>

## Exercises

Attempt each before expanding its solution.

**Exercise 13.1 (Prove).** Prove clause (1) of <XRef id="prop-tf13-recall-accuracy" />: in-batch retrieval recall@1 equals in-batch contrastive top-1 accuracy. Where exactly is the monotonicity of softmax used?

<SolutionBox>

Recall@1 for query $i$ is $\mathbf 1\{r(i)=1\}=\mathbf 1\{\argmax_j M_{ij}=i\}$, since the top-ranked candidate is the one with the largest similarity. The in-batch classifier (<XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-ce" />) predicts $\hat\jmath(i)=\argmax_j\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j$. Because softmax is a strictly increasing function of each logit and shares the same normalizer across $j$, it preserves the order of the logits, so $\argmax_j\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j=\argmax_j M_{ij}$ â€” this is the only place monotonicity is used. Hence the recall@1 indicator equals the classifier-correct indicator $\mathbf 1\{\hat\jmath(i)=i\}$, and averaging over the batch gives recall@1 $=$ accuracy. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 13.2 (Compute).** A batch has $B=4$ pairs with similarity matrix rows $\mathbf M_{1,:}=(0.9,0.2,0.1,0.3)$, $\mathbf M_{2,:}=(0.4,0.8,0.2,0.1)$, $\mathbf M_{3,:}=(0.2,0.5,0.3,0.1)$, $\mathbf M_{4,:}=(0.1,0.0,0.2,0.7)$ (true match on the diagonal). Compute recall@1 and confirm it equals the in-batch top-1 accuracy.

<SolutionBox>

Take the argmax of each row: row 1 $\to$ index 1 ($0.9$); row 2 $\to$ index 2 ($0.8$); row 3 $\to$ index 2 ($0.5>0.3$), but the true match is index 3 â€” a miss; row 4 $\to$ index 4 ($0.7$). So three of four queries rank their true match first: $\mathrm{recall}@1=3/4=0.75$. The in-batch classifier predicts each row's argmax index, and it is correct exactly for rows $1,2,4$ â€” top-1 accuracy $3/4=0.75$, identical to recall@1, as <XRef id="prop-tf13-recall-accuracy" /> requires. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 13.3 (Prove).** Prove the proper-scoring-rule property behind perplexity (<XRef id="prop-tf13-perplexity-kl" />): the expected log-loss $\E[\widehat H]$ is minimized over $\theta$ exactly when $p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)$, and conclude that perplexity reported as $\exp(\E[\widehat H])$ shares that minimizer. Why must the expectation be taken *before* exponentiating?

<SolutionBox>

By <XRef id="prop-tf6-ce-kl-training" />, $\E[\widehat H]=H(p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c))+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\Vert p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c))$. The entropy term is independent of $\theta$, and KL $\ge 0$ with equality iff the two conditionals coincide (Gibbs' inequality), so $\E[\widehat H]$ is minimized exactly at $p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)$ â€” the proper-scoring-rule property for the cross-entropy. Since $\exp$ is strictly increasing, $\exp(\E[\widehat H])$ has the same minimizer as $\E[\widehat H]$, so perplexity-of-the-expected-loss inherits the unique minimizer. The order is essential: $\E[\exp\widehat H]$ â€” expectation *outside* â€” re-weights heavy losses (Jensen) and is generally minimized at a different $\theta$, which is why perplexity is reported as $\exp$ of the average loss, never the average of $\exp$. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 13.4 (Construct).** Using <XRef id="prop-tf13-zeroshot-classifier" />, explain how to turn a trained contrastive imageâ€“text model into a $1000$-class image classifier without any labeled training images, and state the geometric condition under which it is accurate.

<SolutionBox>

For each of the $1000$ classes, form a text prompt ("a photo of a $\langle$class$\rangle$") and encode it with the text tower and pooling (<XRef id="def-tf7-pooled-rep" />) to get $\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_{1000}$; stack them as the classifier head $\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}=[\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_{1000}]$. Classify an image by its embedding $\mathbf v$ via $\hat y=\argmax_c\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_c}$ â€” the encoder classifier of <XRef id="def-tf7-encoder-classifier" /> with a text-supplied, zero-bias head, requiring no labeled images (<XRef id="prop-tf13-zeroshot-classifier" />). It is accurate exactly when the contrastive geometry holds: each image embedding is closer (in cosine) to its true class-name embedding than to any other (<XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-alignment" />), i.e. the modalities are aligned and the class-name embeddings are well separated on the shared sphere. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
