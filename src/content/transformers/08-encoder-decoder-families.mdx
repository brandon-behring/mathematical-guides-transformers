# Encoders and Decoders Across Architecture Families

**Where we are.** The previous chapter organized transformer architectures by two binary switches — is self-attention causal, and is cross-attention present (<XRef id="rem-tf7-three-archs" />) — and neither switch mentions attention. This chapter abstracts both. We define the **sequence mixer** and state causality as one functional equation, **prefix-consistency**, proving which standard mixers satisfy it and that it survives composition, position-wise maps, and residual connections. We then define the **encoder**, **decoder**, and **encoder–decoder** patterns over an arbitrary mixer and exhibit the $3\times 3$ lattice their instantiations form across the attention, RNN, and SSM families. The payoff theorems follow: a causal stack yields all $n$ next-token conditionals from **one evaluation** — the license behind teacher forcing — while the families separate in the **sequential depth** of that evaluation: $\Theta(n)$ vs $\Theta(\log n)$ vs $\Theta(1)$. Turning the causal switch off, we define the **bidirectional scan** and **masked language modeling**, and construct explicit two-token conditional tables no joint distribution realizes — the precise sense in which an encoder-only model is not a generative model. Finally we prove the **prefix-reuse** proposition that makes the KV-cache correct, and close by assembling four proved ingredients into the case for the decoder-only corner of the lattice. Throughout, $n$, $d$, and the block conventions are as fixed earlier; $\mathbf X_{1:m}\in\R^{m\times d}$ denotes the first $m$ rows of $\mathbf X$.

## The sequence mixer and prefix-consistency

Every layer type met so far — attention, the RNN layer, the SSM layer, even the position-wise feed-forward network — is at bottom the same kind of object: a rule that turns $n$ input rows into $n$ output rows, stated uniformly in $n$. We give that object a name, so that the architectural theorems of this chapter can be proved once and inherited by every family.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Sequence mixer" id="def-tf8-mixer">
A **sequence mixer** is a family $F = (F_n)_{n\ge 1}$ of maps
$$
F_n:\R^{n\times d}\to\R^{n\times d},
$$
one per sequence length, computed by a single rule with parameters shared across all lengths. We write $F_n(\mathbf X)_{1:m}$ for the first $m$ output rows and $F_n(\mathbf X)_t$ for the $t$-th.
</Theorem>

Self-attention with or without its mask (<XRef id="def-tf4-attention" />, <XRef id="def-tf4-causal-mask" />), the recurrent layer of <XRef id="def-tf2-rnn" />, the discretized state-space layer of <XRef id="def-tf3-ssm" />, and the position-wise FFN (a mixer that happens not to mix) are all instances: each is one rule, applicable at every $n$.

The property that separates decoders from encoders is not a property of any particular formula; it is a constraint on the family as a whole. The picture is temporal: if the input grows by a token, the outputs already produced must not change — the past is settled. One equation carries both this reading and its sibling, "row $t$ depends only on rows $1,\dots,t$."

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Causal mixer (prefix-consistency)" id="def-tf8-causal">
A sequence mixer $F$ is **causal** if it is **prefix-consistent**: for every $n$, every $\mathbf X\in\R^{n\times d}$, and every $m\le n$,
$$
F_n(\mathbf X)_{1:m} \;=\; F_m(\mathbf X_{1:m}).
$$
Equivalently, extending the input never rewrites earlier outputs, and each output row is a function of the input prefix ending at its own position.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Prefix-consistency">$F_n(\mathbf X)_{1:m} = F_m(\mathbf X_{1:m})$: the length-$n$ evaluation and the length-$m$ evaluation agree on the first $m$ rows.</MarginNote>

The definition quantifies over *all* prefixes at *all* lengths, packaging two facts at once — per-row dependence on the past only, and consistency of the rule across lengths — and it is exactly the property the one-pass and prefix-reuse results below consume. We now sort the standard mixers against it.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before the proof: from which input rows can output row $i$ of *masked* attention gather information — through the weights, and through the values? And which single entry of the *unmasked* weight matrix already dooms prefix-consistency?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Which mixers are causal" id="prop-tf8-causal-instances">
1. Causal (masked) self-attention (<XRef id="def-tf4-causal-mask" />), with position-wise query/key/value/output projections, is a causal mixer.
2. The recurrent layer of <XRef id="def-tf2-rnn" /> and the discrete state-space recurrence $\statevec_t = \discA\statevec_{t-1} + \discB\mathbf x_t$, $\mathbf y_t = \outputmat\statevec_t$ (<XRef id="def-tf3-ssm" />, <XRef id="thm-tf3-zoh" />) are causal mixers.
3. Unmasked self-attention (<XRef id="def-tf4-attention" />) is **not** causal. Witness: $d = d_k = d_v = 1$, identity projections, $\mathbf X = (0, c)^\top$ with $c\neq 0$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (masked attention).** Fix $n$, $m\le n$, and a row $i\le m$. The projections are position-wise, so the first $m$ rows of $\mathbf Q,\mathbf K,\mathbf V$ computed from $\mathbf X_{1:m}$ coincide with those computed from $\mathbf X$. The masked logit row $i$ has finite entries only at $j\le i$ (definition of the mask), each $\inner{\mathbf q_i}{\mathbf k_j}/\sqrt{d_k}$ a function of rows $i,j\le m$; the softmax row is therefore supported on $j\le i$, and the output row $\sum_{j\le i} w_{ij}\mathbf v_j$ consumes value rows $j\le i\le m$ only. Every quantity entering row $i$ is thus identical in the length-$n$ and length-$m$ evaluations (the length-$m$ mask is the leading $m\times m$ block of the length-$n$ mask), which is prefix-consistency row by row.

**Step 2 (recurrences).** Both recurrences define their states by a recursion consuming one input row per step. By induction on $t\le m$: the state at $t$ is a function of the state at $t-1$ and $\mathbf x_t$ (recursion), and the initial state is fixed, so the states for $t\le m$ computed on $\mathbf X$ and on $\mathbf X_{1:m}$ coincide; the output row at $t$ is a position-wise function of the state at $t$ (readout), so the output prefixes coincide.

**Step 3 (unmasked witness).** With identity projections and $d=1$, queries, keys, and values all equal the input. On $\mathbf X_{1:1} = (0)$: the single logit is $0$, the weight is $1$, and $F_1(\mathbf X_{1:1})_1 = v_1 = 0$. On $\mathbf X = (0,c)^\top$: row $1$ has logits $(0\cdot 0,\ 0\cdot c) = (0,0)$, hence weights $(\tfrac12,\tfrac12)$, and
$$
F_2(\mathbf X)_1 = \tfrac12\cdot 0 + \tfrac12\cdot c = \tfrac{c}{2} \neq 0 \qquad (c\neq 0);
$$
for $c = 4$, appending one token moves the settled output from $0$ to $2$. Prefix-consistency fails at $m=1$, $n=2$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="warning" label="Property of the mask">Causality is a property of the *masked operator*, not of attention: the same formula with the mask deleted reads the future through both its weights and its values.</MarginNote>

A block is never a bare mixer: it wraps the mixer in normalization, a feed-forward map, and residual connections. Causality would be useless if this dressing could destroy it. It cannot — causality is closed under exactly the operations blocks are built from.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Closure of causality" id="prop-tf8-causal-closure">
Let $F$ and $G$ be causal mixers and let $\psi:\R^{d}\to\R^{d}$ be any map. Then:
1. **Composition:** the family $(G_n\circ F_n)_{n\ge 1}$ is causal;
2. **Position-wise maps:** the family $\Psi_n(\mathbf X)_t \defeq \psi(\mathbf X_t)$ is causal;
3. **Residual connections:** the family $\mathbf X\mapsto \mathbf X + F_n(\mathbf X)$ is causal.

Consequently any finite stack of causal mixers interleaved with position-wise maps (FFN, layer norm — both act within a row) and residual connections is a causal mixer.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Each part is one identity. **Composition:** for $m\le n$,
$$
\big(G_n\circ F_n\big)(\mathbf X)_{1:m}
= G_n\big(F_n(\mathbf X)\big)_{1:m}
= G_m\big(F_n(\mathbf X)_{1:m}\big)
= G_m\big(F_m(\mathbf X_{1:m})\big),
$$
the second equality by causality of $G$ (applied to the input $F_n(\mathbf X)$), the third by causality of $F$. **Position-wise maps:** row $t$ of $\Psi_n(\mathbf X)$ is $\psi(\mathbf X_t)$, a function of row $t$ alone, so restriction to the first $m$ rows commutes with $\Psi$. **Residual:** restriction to the first $m$ rows is linear, so $(\mathbf X + F_n(\mathbf X))_{1:m} = \mathbf X_{1:m} + F_n(\mathbf X)_{1:m} = \mathbf X_{1:m} + F_m(\mathbf X_{1:m})$. The consequence follows by induction on the depth of the stack, applying (1)–(3) at each layer. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Closure checklist">Causal $\circ$ causal, position-wise, and $\mathrm{id} + $ causal all stay causal — so a whole pre-norm block does, and so does any stack of them.</MarginNote>

## Three patterns over any mixer

With causality abstracted, the two switches of <XRef id="rem-tf7-three-archs" /> become settings available to *every* mixer family, and the three standard architectures become three patterns definable without naming attention.

Only the bare chain rule is needed here. For a fixed sequence length $n$,
$$
p_\theta(x_{1:n}) = \prod_{t=1}^{n} p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t}),
$$
and, conversely, any family of normalized next-token conditionals defines a normalized joint through this product (sum successively over $x_n,x_{n-1},\dots,x_1$). The training chapter will package this identity into the full autoregressive-language-model definition and objective; here the identity itself is enough to characterize the decoder pattern.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Encoder, decoder, and encoder–decoder patterns" id="def-tf8-patterns">
Fix a mixer family and stack its blocks as in <XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-closure" />.
1. The **encoder pattern** is a stack whose mixers are unconstrained — typically bidirectional, so each output row may depend on the whole input. Its product is the full representation $\mathbf H\in\R^{n\times d}$, consumed by a head or by another stack.
2. The **decoder pattern** is a stack whose every mixer is causal, with a position-wise readout turning row $t$ into a next-token conditional $p_\theta(\,\cdot\mid x_{1:t})$; these conditionals compose into the chain-rule joint displayed above.
3. The **encoder–decoder pattern** pairs an encoder producing $\mathbf H$ with a causal decoder whose blocks additionally apply a **conditioning channel**: a map that lets each decoder row consult all of $\mathbf H$ but no future decoder rows — the requirement being that for fixed $\mathbf H$ the dressed decoder remains a causal mixer in the decoder sequence. Cross-attention achieves this position-wise (for fixed $\mathbf H$ it acts within a decoder row, covered by <XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-closure" />); channels that route $\mathbf H$ through the decoder's own state — an initial state set from $\mathbf H$, or additive attention queried by the decoder state — are causal directly, consulting only settled decoder rows.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="The 3×3 instantiation lattice" id="prop-tf8-instantiations">
Each pattern of <XRef id="def-tf8-patterns" /> is realized by each of the three mixer families:

| Pattern | Attention | RNN | SSM |
|---|---|---|---|
| Encoder | transformer encoder (<XRef id="def-tf7-encoder" />): BERT <Cite key="devlin2019bert" /> | bidirectional RNN (<XRef id="def-tf8-bidirectional-scan" />) <Cite key="schuster1997bidirectional" /> | bidirectional SSM scan (<XRef id="def-tf8-bidirectional-scan" />) |
| Decoder | decoder-only transformer (<XRef id="def-tf7-decoder-only" />): GPT <Cite key="radford2018improving" /> | RNN language model (<XRef id="def-tf2-rnn" /> + readout) | SSM language model (<XRef id="def-tf3-ssm" />, stacked) |
| Encoder–decoder | <XRef id="def-tf7-enc-dec" /> with channel <XRef id="def-tf7-cross-attention" />: T5 <Cite key="raffel2020t5" />, the original transformer <Cite key="vaswani2017attention" /> | seq2seq (<XRef id="def-tf2-seq2seq" />) with channel <XRef id="def-tf2-additive-attention" /> | encoder scan + causal decoder, channel via state initialization |

In every cell the causality (or bidirectionality) required by the row is supplied by the column's mixer as classified in <XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-instances" /> and <XRef id="def-tf8-bidirectional-scan" />.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
We verify column by column. **Attention.** Masked self-attention is causal (<XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-instances" />) and unmasked self-attention reads all positions, giving the decoder and encoder rows; cross-attention reads the *fixed* $\mathbf H$ and is position-wise in the decoder rows, so by <XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-closure" /> the decoder stack of <XRef id="def-tf7-decoder-block" /> stays causal — the encoder–decoder cell. **RNN.** The forward recursion is causal; the bidirectional scan below adds a reversed copy and is deliberately not causal (the encoder cell); seq2seq conditions a causal decoder recurrence on the encoder output through the final state and additive attention — for fixed encoder output the decoder remains causal, and <XRef id="prop-tf2-bottleneck" /> is precisely about the width of that channel. **SSM.** The discrete recurrence is causal, and reversing the scan direction gives the bidirectional encoder as for the RNN. The ninth cell is structurally available — bidirectional-scan encoder, causal SSM decoder, channel by initializing the decoder state from a summary of $\mathbf H$ — though the literature has no canonical named instance; we record it as the pattern's prediction rather than as a citation. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Two orthogonal axes">Pattern (causal switch + conditioning switch) and mixer family are independent coordinates: every named model is one cell of the lattice.</MarginNote>

## One pass and sequential depth

Teacher forcing — the training move (next chapter, <XRef id="def-tf9-teacher-forcing" />) that conditions every position on the gold prefix and scores all positions in a single forward pass — rests on a theorem about causal stacks, which we prove here. Prefix-consistency is exactly the hypothesis it needs: the conditional at position $t$ requires the *length-$t$* evaluation, and causality says the length-$n$ evaluation already contains all $n$ of them.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before the proof: the model defines $p_\theta(\,\cdot\mid x_{1:t})$ by running on the *prefix* $x_{1:t}$. What single property guarantees that running once on the *full* sequence reproduces row $t$ of every prefix run?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="One evaluation yields all conditionals" id="thm-tf8-one-pass">
Let $F$ be a causal stack (<XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-closure" />), let the embedding assign row $s$ a function of $(x_s, s)$ only, let $\mathbf W_{\text{out}}$ be a position-wise readout, and define, for every prefix length $t$,
$$
p_\theta(\,\cdot\mid x_{1:t}) \defeq \softmax\!\big(F_t(\mathrm{emb}(x_{1:t}))_t\,\mathbf W_{\text{out}}\big).
$$
Then a **single** evaluation of $F_n$ on the full sequence yields all $n$ conditionals simultaneously:
$$
\softmax\!\big(F_n(\mathrm{emb}(x_{1:n}))_t\,\mathbf W_{\text{out}}\big) = p_\theta(\,\cdot\mid x_{1:t}),
\qquad t = 1,\dots,n.
$$
For the bidirectional instances of this chapter — unmasked attention and the nondegenerate bidirectional scan — the identity fails whenever the readout separates the differing rows: row $t$ of the full evaluation is then a nonconstant function of $x_{t+1:n}$, so it is not a function of $x_{1:t}$ and defines no conditional on the prefix.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (embedding restricts).** Row $s$ of $\mathrm{emb}(x_{1:n})$ is a function of $(x_s,s)$; prefix restriction preserves both the token and its position label, so $\mathrm{emb}(x_{1:n})_{1:t} = \mathrm{emb}(x_{1:t})$.

**Step 2 (the stack restricts).** By prefix-consistency of $F$ and Step 1,
$$
F_n\big(\mathrm{emb}(x_{1:n})\big)_{1:t}
= F_t\big(\mathrm{emb}(x_{1:n})_{1:t}\big)
= F_t\big(\mathrm{emb}(x_{1:t})\big).
$$

**Step 3 (readout is position-wise).** Taking row $t$ of the identity in Step 2 and applying $\mathbf W_{\text{out}}$ and the softmax — both functions of row $t$ alone — gives the claimed equality of distributions.

**Failure for the bidirectional instances.** The witness of <XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-instances" /> makes the failure concrete (with a readout separating the differing rows — one that annihilated the discrepancy could mask it): under unmasked attention, row $1$ of the length-$2$ evaluation equals $c/2$, a nonconstant function of the second token. A teacher-forced "loss" at position $t$ would then score a quantity that has already read the target $x_{t+1}$ it is scored against — the chain-rule factorization above no longer describes what the network computes. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

The theorem holds for every column of the lattice — an RNN decoder enjoys the same one-pass license as a transformer. What separates the columns is the *cost structure* of that one pass: how much of it must happen serially.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Sequential depth of the standard circuits" id="prop-tf8-sequential-depth">
Fix one layer of each family and its **standard evaluation circuit** on $n$ positions: the RNN's defining recursion, the LTI SSM's parallel scan over the operator $\bullet$ (<XRef id="def-tf3-scan-op" />), and attention's matrix products. Count **sequential depth** as the length of the longest chain of dependent stages, where one stage is a matrix multiplication, a position-wise map, or one parallel round of $\bullet$ applications. Then the depths are
$$
\text{RNN: } \Theta(n), \qquad
\text{SSM: } \Theta(\log n), \qquad
\text{attention: } \Theta(1)\ \text{matrix multiplications}.
$$
The statement is scoped to these standard circuits; it asserts no lower bound over all conceivable evaluation strategies.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (RNN).** In the recursion of <XRef id="def-tf2-rnn" />, the state at $t$ consumes the state at $t-1$ through the nonlinearity, so the circuit is an $n$-fold composition in which no step's input is available before the previous step's output: a dependence chain of length $n$.

**Step 2 (SSM).** All $n$ prefix states are prefix compositions of the associative operator $\bullet$, and associativity licenses the doubling scan proved in <XRef id="prop-tf3-scan" />: $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ rounds, each round combining every position with its offset-$2^{r-1}$ neighbor in parallel — one stage per round — plus one position-wise readout stage.

**Step 3 (attention).** One layer performs the three input projections, the product $\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top$, a row-wise softmax, the product with $\mathbf V$, and the output projection: a fixed count of stages independent of $n$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="warning" label="Why no RNN scan">The RNN's transition is nonlinear, so composing two steps has no fixed-size summary; associative re-bracketing — the SSM's escape — is unavailable to the standard circuit.</MarginNote>

<MarginNote variant="computational-note" label="Depth vs work">Depth is not work: the scan's $\Theta(\log n)$ depth costs $\Theta(n\log n)$ operator applications under doubling ($\Theta(n)$ with a work-efficient scan), and attention's $\Theta(1)$ depth costs $\Theta(n^2 d)$ arithmetic (<XRef id="prop-tf7-complexity" />).</MarginNote>

The pair decouples two properties that are easy to conflate: one-pass training is a *causality* property and all three families have it; parallel training is a *depth* property and they differ. The transformer's training-time fit is <XRef id="thm-tf8-one-pass" /> *plus* the $\Theta(1)$ column; the RNN has the theorem but must still serialize each pass.

## Bidirectional mixing and the masked objective

Turning the causal switch off is trivial for attention — delete the mask — but a recurrence is one-directional by construction. The classical remedy is to run it twice.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Bidirectional scan" id="def-tf8-bidirectional-scan">
Let $F$ be a causal recurrence mixer (RNN or discretized SSM). Its **bidirectional scan** runs one copy forward and an independent copy (its own parameters) on the reversed sequence, and merges position-wise: with $\overrightarrow{\mathbf h}_t$ the forward state after $\mathbf x_1,\dots,\mathbf x_t$ and $\overleftarrow{\mathbf h}_t$ the backward state after $\mathbf x_n,\dots,\mathbf x_t$, the output row is a position-wise map of the concatenation $[\,\overrightarrow{\mathbf h}_t;\ \overleftarrow{\mathbf h}_t\,]$.<Sidenote>Introduced for RNNs by <Cite key="schuster1997bidirectional" />; the same reversal applies verbatim to a linear state-space recurrence.</Sidenote> A bidirectional scan is not causal: row $t$ depends on $x_{t+1:n}$ through $\overleftarrow{\mathbf h}_t$ whenever the backward recurrence is nondegenerate. Attention needs no second scan — omitting the mask already lets every row read every position.
</Theorem>

An encoder therefore forfeits <XRef id="thm-tf8-one-pass" />: its rows cannot define next-token conditionals, so it cannot be trained as a language model in the autoregressive sense. What it *can* do is predict a position from both sides, and the standard objective does exactly that on a corrupted input.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Masked language modeling" id="def-tf8-mlm">
Fix a corruption rule: sample a mask set $\mathcal M\subset\{1,\dots,n\}$ and replace each $x_t$, $t\in\mathcal M$, by a reserved mask symbol, producing $\tilde x$. **Masked language modeling** trains a bidirectional stack with a position-wise readout to maximize
$$
\sum_{t\in\mathcal M}\log q_t\big(x_t \mid \tilde x\big),
\qquad
q_t(\,\cdot\mid \tilde x) \defeq \softmax\!\big(F_n(\mathrm{emb}(\tilde x))_t\,\mathbf W_{\text{out}}\big),
$$
each masked position predicted from its full two-sided context <Cite key="devlin2019bert" />.
</Theorem>

MLM learns conditionals of the form $p(x_t\mid x_{-t})$ — one token given *both* sides. Here is the structural asymmetry with the decoder: the causal family $\{p(x_t\mid x_{1:t-1})\}_t$ *always* assembles into a joint, because the chain-rule product displayed at the start of this section is normalized for any collection of normalized next-token conditionals. Two-sided conditionals instead carry a hidden compatibility constraint — they must all be conditionals *of one and the same* joint — and an unconstrained parameterization is free to violate it.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before the example: chain-rule conditionals compose into a joint by multiplication. What could even go wrong for two-sided conditionals — what shared object are they all answerable to?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="example" name="Two conditional tables no joint realizes" id="ex-tf8-incompatible-conditionals">
Take $n = 2$ and the binary vocabulary $\{\mathtt a,\mathtt b\}$. An MLM over two positions learns two tables: $p_1(x_1\mid x_2)$ and $p_2(x_2\mid x_1)$. Consider

| $p_1(x_1\mid x_2)$ | $x_2=\mathtt a$ | $x_2=\mathtt b$ |
|---|---|---|
| $x_1 = \mathtt a$ | $3/4$ | $1/4$ |
| $x_1 = \mathtt b$ | $1/4$ | $3/4$ |

| $p_2(x_2\mid x_1)$ | $x_1=\mathtt a$ | $x_1=\mathtt b$ |
|---|---|---|
| $x_2 = \mathtt a$ | $1/2$ | $1/2$ |
| $x_2 = \mathtt b$ | $1/2$ | $1/2$ |

Each table is, on its own, a perfectly legitimate pair of conditional distributions. We claim **no joint distribution has both**.

Suppose $p$ on $\{\mathtt a,\mathtt b\}^2$ realizes them, and write $p_{ij} \defeq p(x_1{=}i, x_2{=}j)$. For the tables to be conditionals of $p$, both marginals must be positive, and then every cell is positive: $p_{ij} = p_1(i\mid j)\,p(x_2{=}j) > 0$ since every displayed entry is positive. Now trace the four ratio constraints:

**Step 1 (first table, column $\mathtt a$).** $p_1(\mathtt a\mid\mathtt a) = 3/4$ means $p_{\mathtt{aa}}/(p_{\mathtt{aa}}+p_{\mathtt{ba}}) = 3/4$, i.e. $p_{\mathtt{aa}} = 3\,p_{\mathtt{ba}}$.

**Step 2 (second table, row $x_1{=}\mathtt b$).** $p_2(\mathtt a\mid\mathtt b) = 1/2$ means $p_{\mathtt{ba}} = p_{\mathtt{bb}}$.

**Step 3 (first table, column $\mathtt b$).** $p_1(\mathtt a\mid\mathtt b) = 1/4$ means $p_{\mathtt{ab}}/(p_{\mathtt{ab}}+p_{\mathtt{bb}}) = 1/4$, i.e. $p_{\mathtt{bb}} = 3\,p_{\mathtt{ab}}$.

**Step 4 (second table, row $x_1{=}\mathtt a$).** $p_2(\mathtt a\mid\mathtt a) = 1/2$ means $p_{\mathtt{aa}} = p_{\mathtt{ab}}$.

Chaining Steps 1–4: $p_{\mathtt{aa}} = 3\,p_{\mathtt{ba}} = 3\,p_{\mathtt{bb}} = 9\,p_{\mathtt{ab}} = 9\,p_{\mathtt{aa}}$, forcing $p_{\mathtt{aa}} = 0$ — contradicting positivity. No joint exists.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="The invariant">Both tables determine the odds ratio $\dfrac{p_{\mathtt{aa}}p_{\mathtt{bb}}}{p_{\mathtt{ab}}p_{\mathtt{ba}}}$: the first gives $3\cdot 3 = 9$, the second $1\cdot 1 = 1$. Compatibility requires they agree.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="MLM as pseudolikelihood, and what it does not buy" id="rem-tf8-pseudolikelihood">
The MLM objective is a masked, stochastic estimate of the **pseudolikelihood** $\sum_t \log p(x_t\mid x_{-t})$ — up to the corruption of co-masked positions: with more than one mask per sample, each conditional is learned given the *observed* subset rather than the full complement — proposed by <Cite key="besag1975pseudolikelihood" /> as a tractable surrogate for maximum likelihood: there, the conditionals all derive from *one parametric joint*, and maximizing their product consistently estimates that joint's parameters. MLM inverts the logic — it parameterizes the conditionals directly and never writes a joint — and the example shows the resulting family need not cohere. One scope limit keeps the reading honest: this is not a claim that MLM's conditionals are poor estimates — the data's true conditionals are coherent, and each table is separately well-supervised; it is a claim about the *model class*, which carries no coherence guarantee, no canonical sequence probability, and no principled sampler. That is the precise sense in which encoder-only models are representation learners rather than generative models.
</Theorem>

## Conditioning and reuse at decode time

We return to the causal side of the lattice, where generation lives, and prove the fact that production decoding rests on. During autoregressive generation the input grows one token per step; prefix-consistency says the growing input never disturbs what was already computed — at *every* layer.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before the proof: when a token is appended, which layer-$\ell$ rows could conceivably change? What does causality of layers $1,\dots,\ell$ say about them?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Prefix reuse" id="prop-tf8-prefix-reuse">
Let a decoder stack be built from causal layers as in <XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-closure" />, with an embedding assigning row $s$ a function of $(x_s, s)$ only (as in <XRef id="thm-tf8-one-pass" />), and write $\mathbf H^{(\ell)}(x_{1:t})$ for its layer-$\ell$ activations on the prefix $x_{1:t}$. Then for every layer $\ell$ and all $t\le n$,
$$
\mathbf H^{(\ell)}(x_{1:n})_{1:t} \;=\; \mathbf H^{(\ell)}(x_{1:t}).
$$
Consequently every quantity that is a function of the prefix activations alone — position-wise ones like attention's key and value rows $\mathbf k_j, \mathbf v_j$ for $j\le t$, and prefix-aggregating ones like the RNN/SSM state after $t$ tokens — retains, at every later decode step, exactly the value a full recomputation would produce; step $n+1$ must compute only the new position. In particular the KV-cache (<XRef id="def-tf12-kv-cache" />) is **correct**: the cached rows equal the rows the uncached evaluation of <XRef id="prop-tf12-decode-cost" /> would recompute, so cached and uncached decoding emit identical distributions.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (activations restrict, by induction on $\ell$).** The base case $\ell = 0$ is the embedding, which restricts by Step 1 of <XRef id="thm-tf8-one-pass" />. For the step, assume $\mathbf H^{(\ell-1)}(x_{1:n})_{1:t} = \mathbf H^{(\ell-1)}(x_{1:t})$. Layer $\ell$ is a causal mixer (hypothesis plus <XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-closure" /> for its dressing), so
$$
\mathbf H^{(\ell)}(x_{1:n})_{1:t}
= \operatorname{layer}^{(\ell)}_t\!\big(\mathbf H^{(\ell-1)}(x_{1:n})_{1:t}\big)
= \operatorname{layer}^{(\ell)}_t\!\big(\mathbf H^{(\ell-1)}(x_{1:t})\big)
= \mathbf H^{(\ell)}(x_{1:t}),
$$
the first equality by prefix-consistency of the layer, the second by the induction hypothesis.

**Step 2 (cached quantities are frozen).** A cached key or value row at position $j$ is a position-wise function of row $j$ of some $\mathbf H^{(\ell)}$; by Step 1 that row has the same value in every evaluation whose length is $\ge j$. The recurrent state after $t$ tokens is a function of $\mathbf H^{(\ell-1)}(\,\cdot\,)_{1:t}$, fixed by the same argument.

**Step 3 (cached decoding is exact).** At decode step $n+1$, the new query row attends against the cached $\mathbf K_{1:n}, \mathbf V_{1:n}$; by Steps 1–2 these coincide with what recomputing from scratch on $x_{1:n+1}$ would produce for positions $1,\dots,n$, and the new position's projections are computed fresh. Every layer's inputs therefore agree with the uncached evaluation, so the output distribution agrees; the cache changes the cost (<XRef id="prop-tf12-decode-cost" />), not the function. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="What the cache stores">Per layer, the cache holds position-wise images of settled rows; prefix-consistency is the statement that "settled" is permanent.</MarginNote>

The proposition is family-agnostic: for attention the reusable object is the *full* set of prefix rows ($2nd$ per layer, <XRef id="prop-tf12-kv-memory" />), for a recurrence the fixed-size state — one theorem at two points on a memory spectrum. One further pattern interpolates between the causal and bidirectional corners *within a single stack*.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Prefix language model" id="def-tf8-prefix-lm">
Fix a split point $s$: positions $1,\dots,s$ hold given context, positions $s+1,\dots,n$ are generated. The **prefix-LM mask** $\mathbf M^{(s)}\in\R^{n\times n}$ allows position $i$ to read position $j$ iff $j\le\max(i,s)$:
$$
M^{(s)}_{ij} \defeq \begin{cases} 0 & j \le \max(i,s),\\ -\infty & \text{otherwise},\end{cases}
$$
so reading is bidirectional within the prefix, while each suffix position reads the whole prefix and its own past.<Sidenote>The single-stack unification of <Cite key="dong2019unilm" />; studied as an architectural variant alongside encoder–decoder models by <Cite key="raffel2020t5" />.</Sidenote> For fixed prefix content, the map from suffix rows to suffix outputs is prefix-consistent in the suffix indices, so <XRef id="thm-tf8-one-pass" /> and <XRef id="prop-tf8-prefix-reuse" /> apply verbatim to the generated segment; positions $i < s$ forfeit both and are computed once, like an encoder; the boundary row $s$ reads exactly $j \le s$, so it alone among the prefix rows defines a conditional — it supplies $p_\theta(y_1\mid c)$, the seam where the encoder-like prefix hands off to the causal suffix.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Three routes to conditional generation" id="rem-tf8-conditioning-routes">
All conditional generation computes one object — a family of next-token conditionals $p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1}, c)$ over the output given context $c$ — and the lattice offers three routes to it. **(i) Encoder–decoder:** $c$ enters a bidirectional encoder, and a conditioning channel feeds the causal decoder (<XRef id="def-tf7-enc-dec" />); BART instantiates the route with a denoising pretraining objective <Cite key="lewis2020bart" />. **(ii) Prefix LM:** one stack, block mask (<XRef id="def-tf8-prefix-lm" />): $c$ read bidirectionally, $y$ causally. **(iii) Decoder-only:** $c$ is a prompt, read causally like everything else (<XRef id="def-tf7-decoder-only" />). The routes differ in which directions read $c$, in whether the reader of $c$ and the generator of $y$ share parameters, and in what is computed once versus per step: the encoder output once, the prefix rows once, and in route (iii) the prompt's cached keys and values once — <XRef id="prop-tf8-prefix-reuse" /> makes conditioning and generation share a single cache.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Why decoder-only, assembled from proved parts" id="rem-tf8-decoder-only">
The dominance of the decoder-only pattern for general-purpose generation is a corollary of four facts already on the table; nothing new is claimed here. **(1) Exact, fully supervised factorization:** the chain rule makes the causal conditionals compose into a genuine joint with every position supervised, as shown at the start of this section, where MLM supervises only the masked subset and learns conditionals with no coherence guarantee (<XRef id="ex-tf8-incompatible-conditionals" />). **(2) One-pass training:** all $n$ conditionals from a single evaluation (<XRef id="thm-tf8-one-pass" />). **(3) Exact cached decoding:** prefix reuse makes generation incremental at no cost in correctness (<XRef id="prop-tf8-prefix-reuse" />, priced by <XRef id="prop-tf12-decode-cost" />). **(4) Closure:** causality survives arbitrary depth and dressing (<XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-closure" />), so (1)–(3) are free at any scale. Controlled comparisons of architectures and objectives point the same way for zero-shot generative use <Cite key="wang2022architecture" />. The scope limit: the lattice has no globally best cell — bidirectional encoders remain the right pattern when the product is a representation of given text rather than a continuation of it.
</Theorem>

## Exercises

Attempt each before expanding its solution.

**Exercise 8.1 (Prove).** Prove the three closure claims of <XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-closure" /> — composition, position-wise maps, residual connections — and conclude that a full pre-norm decoder block (masked self-attention, layer norms, FFN, two residual connections) is a causal mixer.

<SolutionBox>

**Composition:** $(G_n\circ F_n)(\mathbf X)_{1:m} = G_n(F_n(\mathbf X))_{1:m} = G_m(F_n(\mathbf X)_{1:m}) = G_m(F_m(\mathbf X_{1:m}))$, using causality of $G$ on the input $F_n(\mathbf X)$, then causality of $F$. **Position-wise:** row $t$ of $\Psi_n(\mathbf X)$ is $\psi(\mathbf X_t)$, so restriction commutes with $\Psi$. **Residual:** restriction is linear, so $(\mathbf X + F_n(\mathbf X))_{1:m} = \mathbf X_{1:m} + F_m(\mathbf X_{1:m})$. A pre-norm block is $\mathbf X\mapsto \mathbf Z = \mathbf X + \operatorname{MHA}^{\text{causal}}(\operatorname{LN}(\mathbf X))$ followed by $\mathbf Z\mapsto \mathbf Z + \operatorname{FFN}(\operatorname{LN}(\mathbf Z))$: layer norm and the FFN act within a row (position-wise), masked attention is causal (<XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-instances" />), and the two sublayers compose — every constituent is covered by one of the three claims, so the block, and by induction any stack of such blocks, is causal. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 8.2 (Show).** With $d = d_k = d_v = 1$, identity projections, and $\mathbf X = (0, c)^\top$, show that unmasked attention gives $F_2(\mathbf X)_1 = c/2$ while $F_1(\mathbf X_{1:1})_1 = 0$, and verify that adding the causal mask restores prefix-consistency on this input.

<SolutionBox>

On $\mathbf X_{1:1} = (0)$: one logit $0\cdot 0 = 0$, one weight $1$, output $v_1 = 0$. On $\mathbf X = (0,c)^\top$: row $1$'s logits are $(0\cdot 0,\ 0\cdot c) = (0,0)$, so the softmax weights are $(\tfrac12,\tfrac12)$ and $F_2(\mathbf X)_1 = \tfrac12(0) + \tfrac12(c) = c/2 \neq 0$ for $c\neq 0$ — prefix-consistency fails at $m=1$. With the causal mask, row $1$'s logit at $j=2$ is $-\infty$, which exponentiates to $0$: the weight row becomes $(1, 0)$, so $F_2^{\text{causal}}(\mathbf X)_1 = v_1 = 0 = F_1(\mathbf X_{1:1})_1$. The mask deletes exactly the two channels through which the future entered: the weight on $j=2$ and, with it, the value row $v_2$. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 8.3 (Compute).** For $n = 2^{20}$, compute the sequential depth of one RNN layer, one LTI SSM layer under the parallel scan, and one attention layer, per <XRef id="prop-tf8-sequential-depth" />; give the RNN-to-SSM depth ratio.

<SolutionBox>

RNN: the recursion is a dependence chain of length $n = 2^{20} = 1{,}048{,}576$ stages. SSM: $\lceil\log_2 2^{20}\rceil = 20$ parallel scan rounds (plus one position-wise readout stage). Attention: a fixed constant — the three projections, $\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top$, the row softmax, the product with $\mathbf V$, and the output projection — independent of $n$. Depth ratio RNN/SSM $= 1{,}048{,}576/20 = 52{,}428.8 \approx 5.2\times 10^4$. The work per pass tells a different story (attention's constant depth costs $\Theta(n^2 d)$ arithmetic, <XRef id="prop-tf7-complexity" />): depth and work are separate currencies. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 8.4 (Construct).** Decide whether the tables $p_1(x_1{=}\mathtt a\mid x_2{=}\mathtt a) = 2/3$, $p_1(x_1{=}\mathtt a\mid x_2{=}\mathtt b) = 1/3$ and $p_2(x_2{=}\mathtt a\mid x_1{=}\mathtt a) = 2/3$, $p_2(x_2{=}\mathtt a\mid x_1{=}\mathtt b) = 1/3$ are compatible, and construct the joint if so.

<SolutionBox>

Run the ratio constraints of <XRef id="ex-tf8-incompatible-conditionals" />. From $p_2$: $p_{\mathtt{aa}} = 2p_{\mathtt{ab}}$ (row $\mathtt a$) and $p_{\mathtt{bb}} = 2p_{\mathtt{ba}}$ (row $\mathtt b$). From $p_1$: $p_{\mathtt{aa}} = 2p_{\mathtt{ba}}$ (column $\mathtt a$) and $p_{\mathtt{bb}} = 2p_{\mathtt{ab}}$ (column $\mathtt b$). The four equations are consistent: they force $p_{\mathtt{ab}} = p_{\mathtt{ba}}$ and $p_{\mathtt{aa}} = p_{\mathtt{bb}} = 2p_{\mathtt{ab}}$. (Equivalently, both tables give odds ratio $2\cdot 2 = 4$.) Setting $p_{\mathtt{ba}} = q$: the joint is $(2q, q, q, 2q)$ with sum $6q = 1$, so $q = 1/6$ and
$$
p = \big(p_{\mathtt{aa}}, p_{\mathtt{ab}}, p_{\mathtt{ba}}, p_{\mathtt{bb}}\big) = \big(\tfrac13, \tfrac16, \tfrac16, \tfrac13\big).
$$
Check: $p_1(\mathtt a\mid\mathtt a) = \tfrac{1/3}{1/3+1/6} = \tfrac{2}{3}$, $p_1(\mathtt a\mid\mathtt b) = \tfrac{1/6}{1/6+1/3} = \tfrac{1}{3}$, $p_2(\mathtt a\mid\mathtt a) = \tfrac{1/3}{1/3+1/6} = \tfrac{2}{3}$, $p_2(\mathtt a\mid\mathtt b) = \tfrac{1/6}{1/6+1/3} = \tfrac{1}{3}$ — all four match. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 8.5 (Prove).** Prove that the one-pass identity of <XRef id="thm-tf8-one-pass" /> fails for a bidirectional stack: show that under either the unmasked-attention witness or a nondegenerate bidirectional scan, the row-$t$ readout on $x_{1:n}$ is a nonconstant function of $x_{t+1}$, and explain what a teacher-forced loss at position $t$ would then be scoring.

<SolutionBox>

Unmasked attention: by <XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-instances" />, with identity projections and $\mathbf X = (0,c)^\top$, row $1$ of the length-$2$ evaluation is $c/2$, which varies with the second token; the readout $\softmax(F_2(\mathbf X)_1\mathbf W_{\text{out}})$ is therefore a nonconstant function of $x_2$ and cannot equal $p_\theta(\,\cdot\mid x_1)$, a function of $x_1$ alone. Bidirectional scan: row $t$ merges $\overleftarrow{\mathbf h}_t$, whose recursion consumes $\mathbf x_n,\dots,\mathbf x_{t+1},\mathbf x_t$; if the backward recurrence is nondegenerate, varying $x_{t+1}$ moves $\overleftarrow{\mathbf h}_t$ and hence the readout. In either case a teacher-forced "loss" at $t$ scores a quantity that has already read $x_{t+1}$ — the model can drive the loss down by copying the visible target rather than predicting the next-token conditional in the chain-rule joint. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 8.6 (Identify).** Place each of the following in the lattice of <XRef id="prop-tf8-instantiations" />, naming its pattern and mixer and the property licensing the placement: (a) BERT; (b) an RNN language model; (c) T5; (d) a bidirectional SSM scan with a linear probe on its rows; (e) a single stack under the prefix-LM mask of <XRef id="def-tf8-prefix-lm" />.

<SolutionBox>

(a) Encoder $\times$ attention: unmasked self-attention, bidirectional by construction; product is the representation $\mathbf H$. (b) Decoder $\times$ RNN: the recursion is causal (<XRef id="prop-tf8-causal-instances" />), readout position-wise. (c) Encoder–decoder $\times$ attention: bidirectional encoder plus causal decoder joined by cross-attention, the position-wise channel of <XRef id="def-tf8-patterns" />. (d) Encoder $\times$ SSM: the bidirectional scan (<XRef id="def-tf8-bidirectional-scan" />) forfeits causality, and the probe consumes representations — the lattice's ninth-cell column with its encoder row. (e) None of the three rows exactly: the prefix-LM mask is encoder-like on positions $i < s$ and causal on the suffix — the within-stack interpolation of <XRef id="def-tf8-prefix-lm" />, showing the rows of the lattice are corners of a design space, not a partition of it. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 8.7 (Derive).** For the prefix-LM mask of <XRef id="def-tf8-prefix-lm" />, verify that $M^{(s)}_{ij} = 0$ iff $j\le\max(i,s)$ gives bidirectional reading within the prefix and causal reading on the suffix, and derive from <XRef id="prop-tf8-prefix-reuse" /> that cached decoding of the suffix is exact once the prefix rows are computed.

<SolutionBox>

Case $i\le s$: $\max(i,s) = s$, so position $i$ reads exactly $j\le s$ — every prefix position reads every other prefix position (bidirectional within the prefix) and no suffix position. Case $i > s$: $\max(i,s) = i$, so position $i$ reads $j\le i$ — the whole prefix plus its own past (causal on the suffix). For fixed prefix content, view the stack as a map from suffix rows to suffix outputs: appending a suffix token adds a row that no earlier position can read ($j\le\max(i,s)$ excludes $j > i$ for $i>s$, and $j>s$ for $i\le s$), so the map is prefix-consistent in the suffix indices. The induction of <XRef id="prop-tf8-prefix-reuse" /> then applies with "prefix" meaning "the $s$ context rows plus the settled suffix rows": all cached keys and values — the prefix's, computed once, and the suffix's, appended step by step — equal what full recomputation would produce, so cached suffix decoding emits identical distributions. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
