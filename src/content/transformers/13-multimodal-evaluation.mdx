# Multimodal Evaluation

**Where we are.** This short capstone makes good on the dual-footing thesis one last time, now for *measurement*. The metrics that score vision-language models look modality-specific — image–text retrieval recall, captioning perplexity, zero-shot classification accuracy — but each is a quantity we already defined for text or for detection, evaluated on cross-modal inputs. Retrieval is a ranking problem, and its headline number, recall@1, is exactly the in-batch accuracy of the contrastive objective (<XRef id="def-tf7-contrastive" />); its pairwise form is the AUROC rank statistic (<XRef id="prop-tf7-auroc-rank" />). Captioning quality is perplexity, the exponential of the conditional cross-entropy of the training chapter (<XRef id="def-tf6-ce-loss" />), and minimizing it minimizes a conditional KL — so it is a proper scoring rule, while the surface-overlap metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, SPICE) are not. Zero-shot classification is an encoder-only softmax classifier (<XRef id="def-tf7-encoder-classifier" />) whose weight columns are supplied, with no label training, by the text encoder, and it is correct precisely because the contrastive objective aligned the modalities on a shared sphere (<XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-alignment" />). We write $r(q)$ for a retrieval rank, $\mathrm{PPL}$ for perplexity, and $\mathbf t_c$ for a class-name text embedding.

## Retrieval is a ranking statistic

Cross-modal retrieval ranks candidates of one modality by similarity to a query of the other — find the caption for an image, or the image for a caption. The metrics are the standard ones; the content of this section is that they are not new.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Retrieval rank, recall@k, and MRR" id="def-tf13-retrieval">
For a query $q$ and a candidate pool $\{c_1,\dots,c_N\}$ with similarity scores $s(q,c_j)$ (e.g. the cosine of <XRef id="def-tf7-cosine-temp" />), rank the candidates in decreasing score and let $r(q)\in\{1,\dots,N\}$ be the rank of the true match. Over a query set $\mathcal Q$,
$$
\mathrm{recall}@k = \frac{1}{|\mathcal Q|}\sum_{q\in\mathcal Q}\mathbf 1\{r(q)\le k\},
\qquad
\mathrm{MRR} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal Q|}\sum_{q\in\mathcal Q}\frac{1}{r(q)},
$$
the fraction of queries whose true match falls in the top $k$, and the mean reciprocal rank.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Recall@1 is in-batch contrastive accuracy; the pairwise hit is AUROC" id="prop-tf13-recall-accuracy">
Evaluate retrieval over a batch of $B$ matched pairs with the similarity matrix $\mathbf M$ of <XRef id="def-tf7-contrastive" />, query $i$ ranking the $B$ candidates by $M_{i,:}$.
1. **Recall@1 = accuracy.** $\mathrm{recall}@1$ for query $i$ is $\mathbf 1\{\argmax_j M_{ij}=i\}$, which is exactly the top-1 correctness of the in-batch softmax classifier of <XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-ce" />. Averaged over the batch, retrieval recall@1 equals in-batch contrastive accuracy.
2. **Pairwise hit = AUROC.** The probability that the matched score exceeds a random mismatched score, $\Pr[M_{ii} > M_{ij}]$ for $j\ne i$, is the Mann–Whitney/AUROC statistic of <XRef id="prop-tf7-auroc-rank" /> with positives the matched similarities and negatives the mismatched ones.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
(1) Recall@1 fires for query $i$ iff the top-ranked candidate is the true match, i.e. $\argmax_j M_{ij}=i$. The in-batch classifier of <XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-ce" /> predicts $\hat\jmath(i)=\argmax_j p(j\mid i)=\argmax_j\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j=\argmax_j M_{ij}$, the last equality because softmax is strictly increasing and so preserves the argmax (under any fixed tie-breaking rule applied identically to both sides). Thus the recall@1 indicator equals the indicator that the classifier's top-1 prediction is correct; averaging over $i$ gives accuracy. (2) Take positives $\{M_{ii}\}$ and negatives $\{M_{ij}:j\ne i\}$ and apply <XRef id="prop-tf7-auroc-rank" />: the empirical AUROC is the normalized count of pairs with $M_{ii}>M_{ij}$, i.e. the empirical $\Pr[M_{ii}>M_{ij}]$, the probability the true match outranks a random distractor. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="One statistic, three names">In-batch contrastive accuracy, retrieval recall@1, and the top of the ROC curve are the same number read three ways — the cross-modal payoff of <XRef id="rem-tf7-crossmodal-canonical" />.</MarginNote>

## Captioning quality is a likelihood

A caption model is an autoregressive conditional $p_\theta(\mathbf y\mid \mathbf I)$ (the captioning example of <XRef id="ex-tf6-captioning" />). The intrinsic measure of such a model is its held-out likelihood, reported as perplexity — and this is a likelihood we already understand.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Conditional perplexity" id="def-tf13-perplexity">
For a conditional model $p_\theta(\mathbf y\mid\mathbf c)$ over a target sequence $\mathbf y=(y_1,\dots,y_T)$, the **perplexity** is the exponential of the per-token conditional cross-entropy,
$$
\mathrm{PPL}(\mathbf y\mid\mathbf c) = \exp\!\Big(\!-\frac1T\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{<t},\mathbf c)\Big) = \exp\big(\widehat H\big),
$$
where $\widehat H$ is the empirical cross-entropy loss of <XRef id="def-tf6-ce-loss" /> with conditioning enlarged from $y_{<t}$ to $(y_{<t},\mathbf c)$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Minimizing perplexity minimizes conditional KL" id="prop-tf13-perplexity-kl">
Since $\exp$ is strictly increasing, $\mathrm{PPL}$ and the per-token cross-entropy $\widehat H$ have the same minimizer over $\theta$. In expectation over the data, $\widehat H$ equals the conditional KL from the data to the model plus the data's conditional entropy (<XRef id="prop-tf6-ce-kl-training" />), the latter independent of $\theta$. Hence minimizing perplexity minimizes the conditional KL $\mathrm{KL}\big(p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\,\Vert\,p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\big)$: perplexity is a **proper scoring rule**, uniquely minimized (in expectation) by the true conditional distribution.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
$\mathrm{PPL}=\exp(\widehat H)$ with $\widehat H$ strictly increasing-transformed, so $\argmin_\theta\mathrm{PPL}=\argmin_\theta\widehat H$. Taking expectation over targets drawn from $p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)$ at a context $\mathbf c$ in the data support, the per-token cross-entropy decomposes as $\mathbb E[\widehat H]=H\big(p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\big)+\mathrm{KL}\big(p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\,\Vert\,p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\big)$ by <XRef id="prop-tf6-ce-kl-training" />. The entropy term does not depend on $\theta$, and KL is nonnegative with equality iff $p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)$, so the expected loss — hence perplexity — is minimized exactly at the true conditional, which is the definition of a proper scoring rule. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Surface-overlap metrics are not proper scoring rules" id="rem-tf13-surface-metrics">
The standard captioning metrics — BLEU's $n$-gram precision, CIDEr's tf-idf $n$-gram cosine, SPICE's scene-graph $F$-score <Cite key="papineni2002bleu" /><Cite key="vedantam2015cider" /><Cite key="anderson2016spice" /> — score the *surface overlap* of a generated string with references, not the model's probability distribution. They are not proper scoring rules: a model can raise them by matching reference $n$-grams without its distribution approaching $p_{\mathrm{data}}$, and they are not minimized in expectation only by the true distribution. They correlate with human judgment imperfectly and are gameable, which is why intrinsic perplexity (<XRef id="prop-tf13-perplexity-kl" />) and task accuracies (retrieval, zero-shot) are reported alongside them. The honest reading is that overlap metrics measure resemblance to references, not calibration of the model.
</Theorem>

## Zero-shot classification is a softmax classifier with text weights

The most striking evaluation of a contrastive model uses no training labels at all: to classify an image among $C$ named classes, embed the class *names* with the text encoder and pick the nearest. We show this is literally the encoder classifier of the detection chapter, with its head handed over by the text tower.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Zero-shot classification" id="def-tf13-zeroshot">
Given class names with text embeddings $\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_C\in\R^{d}$ (each the pooled encoding of a prompt like "a photo of a $\langle$class$\rangle$", <XRef id="def-tf7-pooled-rep" />) and an image embedding $\mathbf v\in\R^d$, the **zero-shot classifier** predicts
$$
\hat y = \argmax_{c\in\{1,\dots,C\}} \langle \mathbf v,\mathbf t_c\rangle,
\qquad
p(c\mid\mathbf v) = \softmax\!\big(\langle\mathbf v,\mathbf t_1\rangle/\tau,\dots,\langle\mathbf v,\mathbf t_C\rangle/\tau\big)_c,
$$
using no target-domain labels — the class representations come entirely from the text encoder.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Zero-shot is an encoder classifier with text-embedding head" id="prop-tf13-zeroshot-classifier">
The zero-shot classifier of <XRef id="def-tf13-zeroshot" /> is the encoder-only classifier of <XRef id="def-tf7-encoder-classifier" /> applied to the image embedding $\mathbf v$, with classifier head $\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}=[\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_C]\in\R^{d\times C}$ (columns the class-name text embeddings) and zero bias. It needs no label training because the head is produced by the text encoder, and it is correct because the contrastive minimizer (<XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-alignment" />) aligns each image embedding with its matching class-name embedding and separates it from the others, so the true class is the nearest text embedding.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
With $\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}=[\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_C]$ and bias zero, the encoder-classifier logits of <XRef id="def-tf7-encoder-classifier" /> are $\mathbf o=\mathbf v\,\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}$ with $o_c=\langle\mathbf v,\mathbf t_c\rangle$, and $\hat y=\argmax_c o_c$ together with $\softmax(\mathbf o/\tau)$ are exactly the zero-shot predictions of <XRef id="def-tf13-zeroshot" />. The head columns are the text encoder's outputs on the class names, not parameters fit to target labels, so no label training occurs. For correctness, the symmetric contrastive objective (<XRef id="def-tf7-contrastive" />) at its minimizer drives the matched cosine $\langle\mathbf v,\mathbf t_{c^\star}\rangle$ toward its maximum and the mismatched cosines $\langle\mathbf v,\mathbf t_c\rangle$, $c\ne c^\star$, downward (<XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-alignment" />); hence $\argmax_c\langle\mathbf v,\mathbf t_c\rangle=c^\star$, the true class, so the zero-shot classifier is correct on the aligned geometry. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Every metric reduced" id="rem-tf13-all-reduced">
The capstone closes the loop. Retrieval recall and its pairwise form reduced to in-batch accuracy and the AUROC rank statistic of the detection chapter (<XRef id="prop-tf13-recall-accuracy" />); perplexity is the conditional cross-entropy and KL of the training chapter (<XRef id="prop-tf13-perplexity-kl" />); zero-shot accuracy is the encoder classifier of the detection chapter with a text-supplied head (<XRef id="prop-tf13-zeroshot-classifier" />). No vision-language metric needed new probability theory — only the choice of which already-proven statistic to apply to cross-modal embeddings and likelihoods. This is the evaluation-side statement of the dual-footing thesis (<XRef id="rem-tf1-modality-blind" />): the same mathematics scores the same model, whatever the modality of its inputs. The original contrastive and zero-shot constructions are CLIP's <Cite key="radford2021clip" />; the retrieval-as-ranking view goes back to image–caption alignment <Cite key="karpathy2015deepvisual" />.
</Theorem>

## Exercises

Attempt each before expanding its solution.

**Exercise 13.1 (Prove).** Prove clause (1) of <XRef id="prop-tf13-recall-accuracy" />: in-batch retrieval recall@1 equals in-batch contrastive top-1 accuracy. Where exactly is the monotonicity of softmax used?

<SolutionBox>

Recall@1 for query $i$ is $\mathbf 1\{r(i)=1\}=\mathbf 1\{\argmax_j M_{ij}=i\}$, since the top-ranked candidate is the one with the largest similarity. The in-batch classifier (<XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-ce" />) predicts $\hat\jmath(i)=\argmax_j\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j$. Because softmax is a strictly increasing function of each logit and shares the same normalizer across $j$, it preserves the order of the logits, so $\argmax_j\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j=\argmax_j M_{ij}$ — this is the only place monotonicity is used. Hence the recall@1 indicator equals the classifier-correct indicator $\mathbf 1\{\hat\jmath(i)=i\}$, and averaging over the batch gives recall@1 $=$ accuracy. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 13.2 (Compute).** A batch has $B=4$ pairs with similarity matrix rows $\mathbf M_{1,:}=(0.9,0.2,0.1,0.3)$, $\mathbf M_{2,:}=(0.4,0.8,0.2,0.1)$, $\mathbf M_{3,:}=(0.2,0.5,0.3,0.1)$, $\mathbf M_{4,:}=(0.1,0.0,0.2,0.7)$ (true match on the diagonal). Compute recall@1 and confirm it equals the in-batch top-1 accuracy.

<SolutionBox>

Take the argmax of each row: row 1 $\to$ index 1 ($0.9$); row 2 $\to$ index 2 ($0.8$); row 3 $\to$ index 2 ($0.5>0.3$), but the true match is index 3 — a miss; row 4 $\to$ index 4 ($0.7$). So three of four queries rank their true match first: $\mathrm{recall}@1=3/4=0.75$. The in-batch classifier predicts each row's argmax index, and it is correct exactly for rows $1,2,4$ — top-1 accuracy $3/4=0.75$, identical to recall@1, as <XRef id="prop-tf13-recall-accuracy" /> requires. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 13.3 (Prove).** Prove that perplexity is a proper scoring rule (<XRef id="prop-tf13-perplexity-kl" />): its expected value over the data is minimized over $\theta$ exactly when $p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)$. Why does the monotonicity of $\exp$ matter for the argument?

<SolutionBox>

By <XRef id="prop-tf6-ce-kl-training" />, $\mathbb E[\widehat H]=H(p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c))+\mathrm{KL}(p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)\Vert p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c))$. The entropy term is independent of $\theta$, and KL $\ge 0$ with equality iff the two conditionals coincide (Gibbs' inequality), so $\mathbb E[\widehat H]$ is minimized exactly at $p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(\cdot\mid\mathbf c)$ — the proper-scoring-rule property for the cross-entropy. Since $\mathrm{PPL}=\exp(\widehat H)$ and $\exp$ is strictly increasing, $\argmin_\theta\mathrm{PPL}=\argmin_\theta\widehat H$, so perplexity inherits the same unique minimizer; without monotonicity the exponential could move the minimizer, and the reduction would fail. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 13.4 (Construct).** Using <XRef id="prop-tf13-zeroshot-classifier" />, explain how to turn a trained contrastive image–text model into a $1000$-class image classifier without any labeled training images, and state the geometric condition under which it is accurate.

<SolutionBox>

For each of the $1000$ classes, form a text prompt ("a photo of a $\langle$class$\rangle$") and encode it with the text tower and pooling (<XRef id="def-tf7-pooled-rep" />) to get $\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_{1000}$; stack them as the classifier head $\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}=[\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_{1000}]$. Classify an image by its embedding $\mathbf v$ via $\hat y=\argmax_c\langle\mathbf v,\mathbf t_c\rangle$ — the encoder classifier of <XRef id="def-tf7-encoder-classifier" /> with a text-supplied, zero-bias head, requiring no labeled images (<XRef id="prop-tf13-zeroshot-classifier" />). It is accurate exactly when the contrastive geometry holds: each image embedding is closer (in cosine) to its true class-name embedding than to any other (<XRef id="prop-tf7-contrastive-alignment" />), i.e. the modalities are aligned and the class-name embeddings are well separated on the shared sphere. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
