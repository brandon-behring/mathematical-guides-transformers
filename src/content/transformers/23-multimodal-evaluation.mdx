# Multimodal Evaluation

**Where we are.** This short capstone makes good on the dual-footing thesis one last time, now for *measurement*. The metrics that score vision-language models look modality-specific — image–text retrieval recall, captioning perplexity, zero-shot classification accuracy — but each is a quantity we already defined for text or for detection, evaluated on cross-modal inputs. Retrieval is a ranking problem, and its headline number, recall@1, is exactly the in-batch accuracy of the contrastive objective (<XRef id="def-contrastive" />); its tie-adjusted pairwise form is the AUROC rank statistic (<XRef id="prop-auroc-rank" />). Captioning quality is reported by corpus perplexity, the exponential of expected conditional cross-entropy from the training chapter (<XRef id="def-ce-loss" />); it preserves the conditional-KL minimizer of the strictly proper log-loss, while the surface-overlap metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, SPICE) do not. Zero-shot classification is an encoder-only softmax classifier (<XRef id="def-encoder-classifier" />) whose weight columns are supplied, with no label training, by the text encoder; its transfer succeeds to the extent that contrastive training places novel images near appropriate class-name prompts on the shared sphere (<XRef id="prop-contrastive-alignment" />), an empirical condition rather than a correctness guarantee. We write $r(q)$ for a retrieval rank, $\mathrm{PPL}$ for perplexity, and $\mathbf t_c$ for a class-name text embedding.

## Retrieval is a ranking statistic

Cross-modal retrieval ranks candidates of one modality by similarity to a query of the other — find the caption for an image, or the image for a caption. The metrics are the standard ones; the content of this section is that they are not new.

<NotationOverride scope="Multimodal Evaluation">
$N$ is the retrieval-pool size, not the transformer block count. The letter $c$ indexes a candidate or class, not an image channel.
</NotationOverride>

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Retrieval rank, recall@k, and MRR" id="def-retrieval">
For a query $q$ and a candidate pool $\{c_1,\dots,c_N\}$ with similarity scores $s(q,c_j)$ (e.g. the cosine of <XRef id="def-cosine-temp" />), rank the candidates in decreasing score, resolving equal scores by one fixed deterministic candidate order, and let $r(q)\in\{1,\dots,N\}$ be the rank of the true match. For $1\le k\le N$ and a finite **nonempty** query set $\mathcal Q$,
$$
\mathrm{recall}@k \defeq \frac{1}{|\mathcal Q|}\sum_{q\in\mathcal Q}\mathbf 1\{r(q)\le k\},
\qquad
\mathrm{MRR} \defeq \frac{1}{|\mathcal Q|}\sum_{q\in\mathcal Q}\frac{1}{r(q)},
$$
the fraction of queries whose true match falls in the top $k$, and the mean reciprocal rank. The same deterministic tie rule is used by retrieval ranks and every $\argmax$ comparison below.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Recall@1 is in-batch contrastive accuracy; the pairwise hit is AUROC" id="prop-recall-accuracy">
Evaluate retrieval over a batch of $B\ge2$ matched pairs with the similarity matrix $\mathbf M$ of <XRef id="def-contrastive" />, query $i$ ranking the $B$ candidates by $M_{i,:}$ under the shared deterministic tie rule.
1. **Recall@1 = accuracy.** $\mathrm{recall}@1$ for query $i$ is $\mathbf 1\{\argmax_j M_{ij}=i\}$, which is exactly the top-1 correctness of the in-batch softmax classifier of <XRef id="prop-contrastive-ce" />. Averaged over the batch, retrieval recall@1 equals in-batch contrastive accuracy.
2. **Tie-adjusted pairwise hit = AUROC.** Draw a matched score $S^+$ uniformly from $\{M_{ii}\}$ and, independently, a mismatched score $S^-$ uniformly from $\{M_{i'j}:j\ne i'\}$. Then
   $$
   \Pr(S^+>S^-)+\tfrac12\Pr(S^+=S^-)
   $$
   is exactly the empirical Mann–Whitney/AUROC statistic of <XRef id="prop-auroc-rank" /> (cross-row comparisons included). It differs from recall@1: it counts every pairwise ordering with half credit for ties, not only the per-row top spot.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Proof.**
(1) Recall@1 fires for query $i$ iff the top-ranked candidate is the true match, i.e. $\argmax_j M_{ij}=i$. The in-batch classifier of <XRef id="prop-contrastive-ce" /> predicts $\hat\jmath(i)=\argmax_j p(j\mid i)=\argmax_j\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j=\argmax_j M_{ij}$, the last equality because softmax is strictly increasing and so preserves the argmax (under any fixed tie-breaking rule applied identically to both sides). Thus the recall@1 indicator equals the indicator that the classifier's top-1 prediction is correct; averaging over $i$ gives accuracy. (2) Take positives $\{M_{ii}\}_{i=1}^{B}$ and negatives $\{M_{i'j}:j\ne i'\}$ and apply <XRef id="prop-auroc-rank" />: the empirical AUROC is the normalized count over all $B \cdot B(B-1)$ matched–mismatched pairs (cross-row pairs $M_{ii}$ vs $M_{i'j}$, $i'\ne i$, included, with the $\tfrac12$ tie term) — exactly the tie-adjusted probability in clause (2). $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Two names, one relaxation">In-batch contrastive accuracy and retrieval recall@1 are the same number read two ways; the pooled AUROC is its pairwise-ranking relaxation — the cross-modal payoff of <XRef id="rem-crossmodal-canonical" />.</MarginNote>

## Captioning quality is a likelihood

A caption model is an autoregressive conditional $p_\theta(\mathbf y\mid \mathbf I)$ (the captioning example of <XRef id="ex-captioning" />). The intrinsic measure of such a model is its held-out likelihood, reported as perplexity — and this is a likelihood we already understand.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Conditional perplexity" id="def-perplexity">
For a conditional model $p_\theta(\mathbf y\mid\mathbf c)$ over a target sequence $\mathbf y=(y_1,\dots,y_T)$, the **perplexity** is the exponential of the per-token conditional cross-entropy,
$$
\mathrm{PPL}(\mathbf y\mid\mathbf c) \defeq \exp\!\Big(\!-\frac1T\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{<t},\mathbf c)\Big) = \exp\big(\widehat H\big),
$$
where $\widehat H$ denotes the empirical cross-entropy loss — the training chapter's $\mathcal L(\theta)$ (<XRef id="def-ce-loss" />), renamed to emphasize it is an empirical entropy estimate — with conditioning enlarged from $y_{<t}$ to $(y_{<t},\mathbf c)$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Minimizing perplexity minimizes conditional KL" id="prop-perplexity-kl">
Fix a context $\mathbf c$ and target length $T$, and write $P_{\mathbf c}(\mathbf y)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf y\mid\mathbf c)$ and $Q_{\theta,\mathbf c}(\mathbf y)=p_\theta(\mathbf y\mid\mathbf c)$ for distributions on complete length-$T$ target sequences. Then
$$
\E_{\mathbf y\sim P_{\mathbf c}}[\widehat H]
=\frac1T\Big(H(P_{\mathbf c})+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_{\mathbf c}\Vert Q_{\theta,\mathbf c})\Big).
$$
Thus the expected per-token log-loss minimizes the conditional KL; the factor $1/T$ is exact and does not change the minimizer. If the model family realizes $P_{\mathbf c}$ and the minimum is attained, every minimizing **distribution** equals $P_{\mathbf c}$, though multiple parameters may represent it. If the family does not realize $P_{\mathbf c}$, any attained minimizer is instead a best-in-family KL projection. The log-loss is strictly proper over unrestricted predictive distributions, and corpus perplexity $\exp(\E[\widehat H])$ inherits its minimizer because $\exp$ is strictly increasing. The order matters: $\E[\exp(\widehat H)]$ is a different objective and can have a different minimizer.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Proof.**
The autoregressive factorization gives $-\sum_{t=1}^T\log p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{<t},\mathbf c)=-\log Q_{\theta,\mathbf c}(\mathbf y)$. Therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\E_{P_{\mathbf c}}[\widehat H]
&=-\frac1T\sum_{\mathbf y}P_{\mathbf c}(\mathbf y)\log Q_{\theta,\mathbf c}(\mathbf y)\\
&=\frac1T\left[-\sum_{\mathbf y}P_{\mathbf c}(\mathbf y)\log P_{\mathbf c}(\mathbf y)
+\sum_{\mathbf y}P_{\mathbf c}(\mathbf y)\log\frac{P_{\mathbf c}(\mathbf y)}{Q_{\theta,\mathbf c}(\mathbf y)}\right]\\
&=\frac1T\big(H(P_{\mathbf c})+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_{\mathbf c}\Vert Q_{\theta,\mathbf c})\big),
\end{aligned}
$$
with the usual extended-real convention if $Q_{\theta,\mathbf c}$ assigns zero probability where $P_{\mathbf c}$ is positive. The entropy and positive factor $1/T$ do not affect optimization. Over all predictive distributions, KL is nonnegative and vanishes only at $Q=P$, so the log-loss is strictly proper. If that distribution lies in the model family and a minimum is attained, the minimizing distribution is $P_{\mathbf c}$ (not necessarily at a unique parameter); otherwise an attained minimizer minimizes KL within the family. Finally, strict monotonicity of $\exp$ makes $\exp(\E[\widehat H])$ share the same minimizers, whereas moving the expectation outside the exponential changes the objective. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Surface-overlap metrics are not proper scoring rules" id="rem-surface-metrics">
The standard captioning metrics — BLEU's $n$-gram precision, CIDEr's tf-idf $n$-gram cosine, SPICE's scene-graph $F$-score <Cite key="papineni2002bleu" /><Cite key="vedantam2015cider" /><Cite key="anderson2016spice" /> — score the *surface overlap* of a generated string with references, not the model's probability distribution. They are not proper scoring rules: a model can raise them by matching reference $n$-grams without its distribution approaching $p_{\mathrm{data}}$, and they are not minimized in expectation only by the true distribution. They correlate with human judgment imperfectly and are gameable, which is why intrinsic perplexity (<XRef id="prop-perplexity-kl" />) and task accuracies (retrieval, zero-shot) are reported alongside them. The honest reading is that overlap metrics measure resemblance to references, not calibration of the model.
</Theorem>

## Zero-shot classification is a softmax classifier with text weights

The most striking evaluation of a contrastive model uses no training labels at all: to classify an image among $C$ named classes, embed the class *names* with the text encoder and pick the nearest. We show this is literally the encoder classifier of the detection chapter, with its head handed over by the text tower.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Zero-shot classification" id="def-zeroshot">
Given class names with text embeddings $\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_C\in\R^{d}$ (each the pooled encoding of a prompt like "a photo of a $\langle$class$\rangle$", <XRef id="def-pooled-rep" />) and an image embedding $\mathbf v\in\R^d$ — all embeddings $L^2$-normalized, as in the contrastive training of <XRef id="def-contrastive" />, so inner products are cosines — the **zero-shot classifier** predicts
$$
\hat y \defeq \argmax_{c\in\{1,\dots,C\}} \inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_c},
\qquad
p(c\mid\mathbf v) \defeq \softmax\!\big(\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_1}/\tau,\dots,\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_C}/\tau\big)_c,
$$
using no target-domain labels — the class representations come entirely from the text encoder.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Zero-shot is an encoder classifier with text-embedding head" id="prop-zeroshot-classifier">
The zero-shot classifier of <XRef id="def-zeroshot" /> is the encoder-only classifier of <XRef id="def-encoder-classifier" /> applied to the image embedding $\mathbf v$, with classifier head $\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}=[\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_C]\in\R^{d\times C}$ (columns the class-name text embeddings) and zero bias. It needs no label training because the head is produced by the text encoder. Its *motivation* is the contrastive geometry: the objective supplies alignment/separation pressure on matched and mismatched training pairs (<XRef id="prop-contrastive-alignment" />), and class-name prompts inherit useful ordering only to the extent they transfer from that training geometry — a heuristic validated empirically <Cite key="radford2021clip" />, not a correctness theorem.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Proof.**
With $\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}=[\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_C]$ and bias zero, the encoder-classifier logits of <XRef id="def-encoder-classifier" /> are $\mathbf o=\mathbf v\,\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}$ with $o_c=\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_c}$, and $\hat y=\argmax_c o_c$ is exactly the zero-shot *decision* of <XRef id="def-zeroshot" /> for every $\tau>0$ (dividing logits by $\tau$ is a monotone rescaling); the probabilistic outputs coincide at $\tau=1$, the detection/readouts chapter's classifier carrying no temperature. The head columns are the text encoder's outputs on the class names, not parameters fit to target labels, so no label training occurs. For the alignment reading: during optimization the symmetric contrastive objective (<XRef id="def-contrastive" />) pushes matched *training-pair* logits up and mismatched ones down in the coordinatewise sense of <XRef id="prop-contrastive-alignment" />; insofar as a class-name prompt embeds near its class's caption cluster, $\argmax_c\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_c}$ recovers the true class. That transfer step is the zero-shot leap — empirical, not proved. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Every metric reduced" id="rem-all-reduced">
The capstone closes the loop. Retrieval recall and its pairwise form reduced to in-batch accuracy and the AUROC rank statistic of the detection chapter (<XRef id="prop-recall-accuracy" />); perplexity is the conditional cross-entropy and KL of the training chapter (<XRef id="prop-perplexity-kl" />); zero-shot accuracy is the encoder classifier of the detection chapter with a text-supplied head (<XRef id="prop-zeroshot-classifier" />). No vision-language metric needed new probability theory — only the choice of which already-proven statistic to apply to cross-modal embeddings and likelihoods. This is the evaluation-side statement of the dual-footing thesis (<XRef id="rem-modality-blind" />): the same mathematics scores the same model, whatever the modality of its inputs. The original contrastive and zero-shot constructions are CLIP's <Cite key="radford2021clip" />; the retrieval-as-ranking protocol originates with Hodosh, Young, and Hockenmaier <Cite key="hodosh2013framing" />, adopted for alignment models by <Cite key="karpathy2015deepvisual" />.
</Theorem>

## Exercises

Attempt each before expanding its solution.

**Exercise 23.1 (Prove).** Prove clause (1) of <XRef id="prop-recall-accuracy" />: in-batch retrieval recall@1 equals in-batch contrastive top-1 accuracy. Where exactly is the monotonicity of softmax used?

<SolutionBox>

Recall@1 for query $i$ is $\mathbf 1\{r(i)=1\}=\mathbf 1\{\argmax_j M_{ij}=i\}$, since the top-ranked candidate is the one with the largest similarity. The in-batch classifier (<XRef id="prop-contrastive-ce" />) predicts $\hat\jmath(i)=\argmax_j\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j$. Because softmax is a strictly increasing function of each logit and shares the same normalizer across $j$, it preserves the order of the logits, so $\argmax_j\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j=\argmax_j M_{ij}$ — this is the only place monotonicity is used. Hence the recall@1 indicator equals the classifier-correct indicator $\mathbf 1\{\hat\jmath(i)=i\}$, and averaging over the batch gives recall@1 $=$ accuracy. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 23.2 (Compute).** A batch has $B=4$ pairs with similarity matrix rows $\mathbf M_{1,:}=(0.9,0.2,0.1,0.3)$, $\mathbf M_{2,:}=(0.4,0.8,0.2,0.1)$, $\mathbf M_{3,:}=(0.2,0.5,0.3,0.1)$, $\mathbf M_{4,:}=(0.1,0.0,0.2,0.7)$ (true match on the diagonal). Compute recall@1 and confirm it equals the in-batch top-1 accuracy.

<SolutionBox>

Take the argmax of each row: row 1 $\to$ index 1 ($0.9$); row 2 $\to$ index 2 ($0.8$); row 3 $\to$ index 2 ($0.5>0.3$), but the true match is index 3 — a miss; row 4 $\to$ index 4 ($0.7$). So three of four queries rank their true match first: $\mathrm{recall}@1=3/4=0.75$. The in-batch classifier predicts each row's argmax index, and it is correct exactly for rows $1,2,4$ — top-1 accuracy $3/4=0.75$, identical to recall@1, as <XRef id="prop-recall-accuracy" /> requires. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 23.3 (Prove).** For fixed context $\mathbf c$ and length $T$, derive the exact $1/T$ conditional-entropy-plus-KL decomposition behind <XRef id="prop-perplexity-kl" />. State the minimizer both when the true conditional is realizable and attained and when the model family is restricted; distinguish uniqueness of the predictive distribution from uniqueness of its parameters. Conclude that $\exp(\E[\widehat H])$ shares the minimizer, and explain why the expectation must precede exponentiation.

<SolutionBox>

Let $P(\mathbf y)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf y\mid\mathbf c)$ and $Q_\theta(\mathbf y)=p_\theta(\mathbf y\mid\mathbf c)$ on length-$T$ sequences. Since $\widehat H(\mathbf y)=-(1/T)\log Q_\theta(\mathbf y)$,
$$
\E_P[\widehat H]=\frac1T\left(H(P)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\Vert Q_\theta)\right).
$$
The entropy term and positive factor $1/T$ are independent of $\theta$. If $P$ belongs to the model family and the minimum is attained, Gibbs' inequality makes the unique minimizing **distribution** $Q=P$; parameters need not be unique because different $\theta$ may encode the same $Q$. In a restricted family not containing $P$, any attained optimizer is a best-in-family minimizer of $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\Vert Q_\theta)$, not the true conditional. Since $\exp$ is strictly increasing, $\exp(\E_P[\widehat H])$ has precisely the same minimizers. The order is essential: $\E_P[\exp\widehat H]$ reweights large sample losses and is a different objective, so it need not select the KL projection. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 23.4 (Construct).** Using <XRef id="prop-zeroshot-classifier" />, explain how to turn a trained contrastive image–text model into a $1000$-class image classifier without any labeled training images, and state the geometric condition under which it is accurate.

<SolutionBox>

For each of the $1000$ classes, form a text prompt ("a photo of a $\langle$class$\rangle$") and encode it with the text tower and pooling (<XRef id="def-pooled-rep" />) to get $\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_{1000}$; stack them as the classifier head $\mathbf W_{\mathsf{cls}}=[\mathbf t_1,\dots,\mathbf t_{1000}]$. Classify an image by its embedding $\mathbf v$ via $\hat y=\argmax_c\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_c}$ — the encoder classifier of <XRef id="def-encoder-classifier" /> with a text-supplied, zero-bias head, requiring no labeled images (<XRef id="prop-zeroshot-classifier" />). For a given image, correctness is equivalent to the geometric condition $\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_y}>\max_{c\ne y}\inner{\mathbf v}{\mathbf t_c}$ (up to the chosen tie rule): the true class-name embedding must be its closest candidate in cosine. Contrastive training encourages analogous ordering for matched **training** pairs (<XRef id="prop-contrastive-alignment" />), but does not guarantee that novel images and class-name prompts satisfy this condition; zero-shot accuracy is therefore an empirical transfer result. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
