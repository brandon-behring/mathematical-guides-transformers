# Discrete Visual Tokenization

**Where we are.** The connectors of the previous chapter produced *soft* visual tokens — arbitrary vectors in $\R^{d}$. This chapter takes the opposite route: make the image **discrete**, a finite sequence of symbols drawn from a fixed vocabulary, exactly like text. The payoff is total reuse. Once an image is a sequence over an alphabet of size $K$, the autoregressive language model of the training chapter applies *verbatim* — same factorization, same softmax head, same cross-entropy, same teacher forcing — with the vocabulary $\mathcal V$ swapped for the visual codebook. Three things stand between us and that payoff. First, the map that snaps a continuous encoder output to the nearest codebook entry is not differentiable, so we cannot train through it by ordinary backpropagation; the **straight-through estimator** is the standard repair. Second, the codebook itself must be learned, and we show its update rule is exactly a step of k-means. Third, and most importantly, we prove the dual-footing claim in its sharpest form: image generation *is* autoregressive language modeling over a visual vocabulary, with modality confined to a fixed image-to-token codec. We write $\mathcal C$ for the codebook, $q$ for the quantizer, $\mathbf z_e$ and $\mathbf z_q$ for the encoder output before and after quantization, $\Phi,\Psi$ for the encoder and decoder, $K$ for the codebook size, and $L$ for the code length.

## From a continuous autoencoder to a discrete waist

A continuous autoencoder already has the two modality-specific maps this chapter needs: an encoder compresses pixels to a latent grid and a decoder reconstructs pixels from that grid. VQ-VAE changes one interface — it forces every latent row through a finite codebook before decoding — and that finite waist is what later makes the image available to a categorical transformer.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Continuous autoencoder and VQ-VAE insertion" id="def-autoencoder">
Let $\mathcal X$ be an image space. A **continuous autoencoder** consists of an encoder and decoder
$$
\Phi:\mathcal X\to\R^{L\times d_c},
\qquad
\Psi:\R^{L\times d_c}\to\mathcal X,
$$
with encoder grid $\mathbf z_e\defeq\Phi(x)$ and reconstruction $\widehat x_{\mathrm{cont}}\defeq\Psi(\mathbf z_e)$. Fix a reconstruction-loss function $\ell:\mathcal X\times\mathcal X\to\R_{\ge0}$; the continuous path incurs $\ell(x,\widehat x_{\mathrm{cont}})$. A **VQ-VAE** <Cite key="vandenoord2017vqvae" /> inserts the grid quantizer $q_L$, obtained by applying a single-row finite quantizer $q$ to each latent row, between these maps:
$$
\mathbf z_q\defeq q_L(\mathbf z_e),
\qquad
\widehat x\defeq\Psi(\mathbf z_q).
$$
The architecture is therefore encoder $\to$ quantizer $\to$ decoder; the next definition constructs the discrete middle map.
</Theorem>

## The codebook quantizer

Starting from <XRef id="def-autoencoder" />, replace each of the encoder's $L$ latent rows by its nearest learned code. After that replacement the image is fully described by $L$ integers in $\{1,\dots,K\}$.

<NotationOverride scope="Discrete Visual Tokenization">
$K$ is codebook size; $\beta$ is commitment weight; $q$ is the single-row quantizer and $q_L$ its gridwise extension; and $\Phi,\Psi$ are codec encoder/decoder maps. The vector $\mathbf e_k\in\R^{d_c}$ is a learned **code vector**, overriding the standing one-hot meaning of $\mathbf e_k\in\R^K$.
</NotationOverride>

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Codebook and quantizer" id="def-codebook">
A **codebook** is a finite set $\mathcal C = \{\mathbf e_1,\dots,\mathbf e_K\}\subset\R^{d_c}$ of $K$ learned vectors, where $d_c$ is the code (latent) dimension. The **quantizer** $q:\R^{d_c}\to\mathcal C$ snaps a vector to its nearest codebook entry,
$$
q(\mathbf z) \defeq \mathbf e_{k^\star(\mathbf z)},
\qquad
k^\star(\mathbf z) \defeq \argmin_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}} \norm{\mathbf z - \mathbf e_k}.
$$
Ties (points equidistant from two or more codes) are broken by the smallest index, so $q$ is a well-defined function on all of $\R^{d_c}$. Define the row-wise **index map** and **codebook lookup**
$$
\operatorname{idx}_{\mathcal C}(\mathbf Z)\defeq\big(k^\star(\mathbf Z^{(1)}),\dots,k^\star(\mathbf Z^{(L)})\big)\in\{1,\dots,K\}^{L},
\qquad
\mathcal E_{\mathcal C}(\mathbf z)\defeq(\mathbf e_{z_1};\dots;\mathbf e_{z_L})\in\R^{L\times d_c}.
$$
The row-wise extension $q_L:\R^{L\times d_c}\to\mathcal C^L$ is
$$
[q_L(\mathbf Z)]^{(t)}\defeq q(\mathbf Z^{(t)}),
\qquad
q_L(\mathbf Z)=\mathcal E_{\mathcal C}(\operatorname{idx}_{\mathcal C}(\mathbf Z)).
$$
For the encoder grid $\mathbf z_e=\Phi(x)$ of <XRef id="def-autoencoder" />, the index sequence is $\operatorname{idx}_{\mathcal C}(\mathbf z_e)$ and the quantized grid is $\mathbf z_q=q_L(\mathbf z_e)$. The decoder reconstructs $\hat x=\Psi(\mathbf z_q)$.<Sidenote>The lookup $z\mapsto\mathbf e_z=\mathbf C[z,:]$ is the codebook embedding of <XRef id="def-codebook-embedding" />, run in reverse to produce indices. The LM-side table $\mathbf C\in\R^{K\times d}$ lives in model space, while $\mathcal C\subset\R^{d_c}$ here lives in codec latent space: distinct tables sharing one index alphabet.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Same lookup, both directions">Quantization produces an index; <XRef id="def-codebook-embedding" /> consumes one. The codebook is the visual analogue of a token vocabulary, and a quantized image is a sentence in it.</MarginNote>

The index sequence is the object the rest of the chapter cares about. Everything modality-specific — pixels, patches, the encoder $\Phi$ and decoder $\Psi$ — lives outside it; inside, we have a sequence over a finite alphabet, and the transformer is blind to where it came from (<XRef id="rem-modality-blind" />).

## The non-differentiable obstacle

To train the autoencoder end to end we must push a reconstruction gradient from the decoder back through the quantizer to the encoder. But the quantizer is piecewise constant, and its derivative is useless.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="The quantizer is a Voronoi projection with zero Jacobian a.e." id="prop-voronoi">
The quantizer $q$ of <XRef id="def-codebook" /> is the Euclidean nearest-point projection onto the finite set $\mathcal C$. The closed **Voronoi regions**
$$
R_k = \{\mathbf z\in\R^{d_c}: \norm{\mathbf z-\mathbf e_k}\le \norm{\mathbf z-\mathbf e_j}\ \ \forall j\},
$$
cover $\R^{d_c}$ and overlap only where nearest codes tie; the deterministic tie rule assigns those boundary points to make a disjoint partition. On the interior of each region, $q\equiv\mathbf e_k$ is constant. Consequently $\partial q/\partial\mathbf z=\mathbf 0$ at every interior point. The exceptional set is the Voronoi boundary where two or more **nearest** codes tie; it is contained in a finite union of pairwise bisector hyperplanes and has Lebesgue measure zero. Thus $\partial q/\partial\mathbf z=\mathbf 0$ almost everywhere — backpropagation through $q$ delivers no gradient to the encoder.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
By definition $q(\mathbf z)=\arg\min_{\mathbf e\in\mathcal C}\norm{\mathbf z-\mathbf e}$, which is the nearest-point (Euclidean) projection onto the finite set $\mathcal C$; the set of points for which $\mathbf e_k$ is a nearest code is exactly $R_k$. The regions cover the space. They can overlap only when multiple codes attain the same minimum, and the smallest-index rule assigns each such point to one output. Fix $\mathbf z$ off that boundary. Its nearest code is unique, so strict inequalities persist on a neighborhood; $q$ is constant there and every partial derivative vanishes. If two nearest codes $\mathbf e_k,\mathbf e_j$ tie, then $\mathbf z$ lies on their bisector $\{\norm{\mathbf z-\mathbf e_k}=\norm{\mathbf z-\mathbf e_j}\}$, an affine hyperplane of dimension $d_c-1$. The Voronoi boundary is contained in the finite union of these hyperplanes, hence has measure zero. Therefore the Jacobian is the zero matrix almost everywhere. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

A zero gradient almost everywhere would freeze the encoder: the reconstruction loss could never tell $\Phi$ how to move. The fix is to replace the quantizer's useless Jacobian with a useful one, only on the backward pass.

## The straight-through estimator

The straight-through estimator keeps the exact quantization in the forward pass but *pretends*, during backpropagation, that the quantizer was the identity. The stop-gradient operator $\sg$ — identity in the forward pass, zero Jacobian in the backward pass — expresses this in one line.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Straight-through estimator" id="def-ste">
Let $\sg[\cdot]$ be the **stop-gradient** operator: $\sg[\mathbf u]=\mathbf u$ in the forward pass and $\partial\,\sg[\mathbf u]/\partial\mathbf u = \mathbf 0$ in the backward pass. The **straight-through estimator (STE)** defines the quantized latent passed to the decoder as
$$
\mathbf z_q \defeq \mathbf z_e + \sg\!\big[\,q_L(\mathbf z_e) - \mathbf z_e\,\big].
$$
The decoder receives $\mathbf z_q$; gradients are computed as if $\mathbf z_q=\mathbf z_e$.<Sidenote>The STE dates to <Cite key="bengio2013estimating" /> and is the gradient estimator that makes VQ-VAE trainable end to end <Cite key="vandenoord2017vqvae" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

Backward (dashed): the reconstruction gradient is copied around the non-differentiable quantizer onto the encoder, because the STE Jacobian is the identity. <Theorem kind="theorem" name="STE: forward quantizes, backward is the identity" id="thm-ste-identity">
For the STE of <XRef id="def-ste" />:
1. **Forward.** $\mathbf z_q = q_L(\mathbf z_e)$ exactly — the decoder sees the true quantized grid.
2. **Backward.** $\partial\mathbf z_q/\partial\mathbf z_e = \mathbf I$, so for any loss $\mathcal L$ the reconstruction gradient is copied unchanged from the quantized latent to the encoder output: $\partial\mathcal L/\partial\mathbf z_e = \partial\mathcal L/\partial\mathbf z_q$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
(1) In the forward pass $\sg$ is the identity, so $\mathbf z_q=\mathbf z_e+\big(q_L(\mathbf z_e)-\mathbf z_e\big)=q_L(\mathbf z_e)$. (2) Differentiate $\mathbf z_q=\mathbf z_e+\sg[q_L(\mathbf z_e)-\mathbf z_e]$ with respect to the vectorization of $\mathbf z_e$. The first term contributes $\mathbf I$. The second term is a stop-gradient, whose backward Jacobian is $\mathbf 0$ by definition — this discards the row-wise quantizer's useless true Jacobian together with the $-\mathbf I$ inside the bracket. Hence $\partial\mathbf z_q/\partial\mathbf z_e=\mathbf I+\mathbf 0=\mathbf I$, and by the chain rule $\partial\mathcal L/\partial\mathbf z_e=\partial\mathcal L/\partial\mathbf z_q$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Why it works">The estimator is biased — it ignores the quantization map's true derivative — but it points the encoder in a sane direction: "produce latents the decoder, fed their nearest code, can reconstruct." The bias is the price of a discrete waist.</MarginNote>

The STE moves the reconstruction gradient to the encoder, but it says nothing about *where the codes should sit*. That is the job of the loss.

## The vector-quantization loss and its gradient split

The encoder is trained for reconstruction (via the STE), but two more things must happen: the codebook entries must chase the encoder outputs they are responsible for, and the encoder must not drift away from the codes it is being quantized to. The stop-gradient operator splits one squared distance into these two responsibilities by freezing opposite arguments.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Vector-quantization loss" id="def-vq-loss">
Let $\mathbf Z_{\mathcal C}\defeq q_L(\mathbf z_e)=\mathcal E_{\mathcal C}(\operatorname{idx}_{\mathcal C}(\mathbf z_e))$ be the assigned-code grid, and let $\norm{\mathbf Z}_F^2\defeq\sum_{t=1}^L\norm{\mathbf Z^{(t)}}^2$. With scalar reconstruction loss $\mathcal L_{\mathrm{rec}}\defeq\ell(x,\Psi(\mathbf z_q))$ (e.g. $\norm{x-\hat x}^2$), the **VQ loss** is
$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{VQ}} \defeq \underbrace{\mathcal L_{\mathrm{rec}}}_{\text{reconstruction}}
\;+\; \underbrace{\norm{\sg[\mathbf z_e] - \mathbf Z_{\mathcal C}}_F^2}_{\text{codebook}}
\;+\; \beta\,\underbrace{\norm{\mathbf z_e - \sg[\mathbf Z_{\mathcal C}]}_F^2}_{\text{commitment}},
$$
with **commitment weight** $\beta>0$. The codebook and commitment terms are the same summed rowwise squared distance $\norm{\mathbf z_e-\mathbf Z_{\mathcal C}}_F^2$ with the stop-gradient placed on opposite arguments.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="The VQ loss routes three gradients" id="prop-vq-gradient">
Away from assignment boundaries—equivalently, holding the discrete assignments fixed locally—the three terms of <XRef id="def-vq-loss" /> produce three separately routed gradient contributions:
1. **Reconstruction → encoder.** $\partial\mathcal L_{\mathrm{rec}}/\partial\mathbf z_e = \partial\mathcal L_{\mathrm{rec}}/\partial\mathbf z_q$, copied through the STE (<XRef id="thm-ste-identity" />); it also trains the decoder $\Psi$.
2. **Codebook → codes.** If $S_k=\{t:k^\star(\mathbf z_e^{(t)})=k\}$, then the codebook term has
   $$
   \frac{\partial\mathcal L_{\mathrm{codebook}}}{\partial\mathbf e_k}
   =2\sum_{t\in S_k}(\mathbf e_k-\mathbf z_e^{(t)}),
   $$
   and no gradient with respect to $\mathbf z_e$ (frozen by $\sg$).
3. **Commitment → encoder.** For $t\in S_k$, the commitment term has row gradient $2\beta(\mathbf z_e^{(t)}-\mathbf e_k)$ and no gradient with respect to the code vectors; it pulls each encoder row toward its assigned code.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
(1) is <XRef id="thm-ste-identity" /> applied to $\mathcal L_{\mathrm{rec}}$, since $\mathbf z_q$ feeds the decoder and the STE Jacobian to $\mathbf z_e$ is the identity; the decoder parameters receive the ordinary reconstruction gradient. (2) Expand the Frobenius norm as $\sum_t\norm{\sg[\mathbf z_e^{(t)}]-\mathbf Z_{\mathcal C}^{(t)}}^2$. In the backward pass each $\sg[\mathbf z_e^{(t)}]$ is constant, so differentiation gives $2(\mathbf Z_{\mathcal C}^{(t)}-\mathbf z_e^{(t)})$ for the assigned code and zero for the encoder row. If several rows share a code, those gradients sum on the one shared parameter. (3) Symmetrically, expanding $\beta\norm{\mathbf z_e-\sg[\mathbf Z_{\mathcal C}]}_F^2$ gives rowwise encoder gradient $2\beta(\mathbf z_e^{(t)}-\mathbf Z_{\mathcal C}^{(t)})$ and no codebook gradient. Reconstruction and commitment both update the encoder, but as distinct additive contributions; only the two stop-gradient distance terms isolate codebook from encoder. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

The codebook term is more than a regularizer — its minimizer has a name.

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="The codebook update is a k-means step" id="thm-codebook-kmeans">
Fix the encoder, and let $S_k = \{i : k^\star(\mathbf z_e^{(i)}) = k\}$ be the set of latent positions currently assigned to code $k$ (the Voronoi assignment of <XRef id="prop-voronoi" />). The codebook term of <XRef id="def-vq-loss" />, summed over positions, is $\sum_k \sum_{i\in S_k}\norm{\mathbf z_e^{(i)} - \mathbf e_k}^2$, and for each **nonempty** cell ($|S_k|\ge 1$) it is minimized over $\mathbf e_k$ by the **cell centroid**
$$
\mathbf e_k^\star = \frac{1}{|S_k|}\sum_{i\in S_k}\mathbf z_e^{(i)}.
$$
(An empty cell leaves its code unconstrained by this term; in practice such codes are reinitialized.)
Alternating the assignment step ($i\mapsto k^\star(\mathbf z_e^{(i)})$, nearest code) with this centroid step is exactly one iteration of **Lloyd's algorithm** for k-means on the encoder's latent outputs. Learning the codebook by the codebook loss is therefore online k-means.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
For fixed assignments $S_k$, the objective separates over $k$, and for a nonempty cell the $k$-th block $\sum_{i\in S_k}\norm{\mathbf z_e^{(i)}-\mathbf e_k}^2$ is a sum of $|S_k|\ge 1$ convex quadratics in $\mathbf e_k$, with positive-definite Hessian $2|S_k|\mathbf I$. Its gradient is $\sum_{i\in S_k} 2(\mathbf e_k-\mathbf z_e^{(i)})$; setting it to zero gives $|S_k|\,\mathbf e_k=\sum_{i\in S_k}\mathbf z_e^{(i)}$, i.e. $\mathbf e_k^\star=\frac{1}{|S_k|}\sum_{i\in S_k}\mathbf z_e^{(i)}$, the centroid, which is the unique minimizer by strict convexity. This is the centroid (M-)step of Lloyd's algorithm; the Voronoi nearest-code rule of <XRef id="prop-voronoi" /> is its assignment (E-)step. A gradient step on the codebook term (clause 2 of <XRef id="prop-vq-gradient" />, $2(\mathbf e_k-\mathbf z_e^{(i)})$) moves $\mathbf e_k$ toward this centroid, so codebook learning is the stochastic/online form of k-means. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="EMA codebooks">In practice the centroid step is often run as an exponential moving average of assigned encoder outputs — a streaming Lloyd update — rather than through the codebook-loss gradient, which is the same fixed point.</MarginNote>

## Dual footing: image generation is autoregressive language modeling

We can now state the chapter's headline. Once the codec $(\Phi,\operatorname{idx}_{\mathcal C},\mathcal E_{\mathcal C},\Psi)$ is fixed, an image *is* represented by a sequence of $L$ integers over an alphabet of size $K$. Generating images means modeling the distribution of those sequences — and that is the autoregressive language-modeling problem of the training chapter, with the text vocabulary replaced by the visual codebook. Nothing in the language-model construction refers to what a token *means*; only the size of the alphabet enters. So the entire apparatus transfers without a single new theorem.

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="VQ image generation is autoregressive LM over a visual vocabulary" id="thm-dual-footing">
Fix the encoder and decoder of <XRef id="def-autoencoder" /> and the index/lookup maps of <XRef id="def-codebook" />, and let $\mathbf z = (z_1,\dots,z_L)\in\{1,\dots,K\}^{L}$ be the index sequence of an image. Modeling the **codec-induced code distribution** reduces exactly to autoregressive language modeling (<XRef id="def-autoregressive-lm" />) over the visual vocabulary $\mathcal V = \{1,\dots,K\}$, $|\mathcal V| = K$:
$$
p_\theta(\mathbf z) = \prod_{t=1}^{L} p_\theta(z_t\mid z_{<t}),
\qquad
p_\theta(z_t\mid z_{<t}) = \softmax(\mathbf h_t\mathbf W_{\text{out}})_{z_t},
$$
trained by the cross-entropy loss of <XRef id="def-ce-loss" /> (whose expectation over the data minimizes $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{data}}\,\|\,p_\theta)$, <XRef id="prop-ce-kl-training" />) with teacher forcing (<XRef id="def-teacher-forcing" />). Every theorem of the training chapter holds verbatim with $|\mathcal V|=K$; modality enters **only** through the fixed codec, which translates pixels to indices by $\operatorname{idx}_{\mathcal C}\circ\Phi$ and code vectors back to pixels by $\Psi$. To sample, use any tokenwise rule of <XRef id="def-token-decoding" /> to draw $\mathbf z$ autoregressively, form the latent grid $\mathbf z_q=\mathcal E_{\mathcal C}(\mathbf z)$, and decode $\hat x=\Psi(\mathbf z_q)$. This models the distribution of codec reconstructions; no prior can recover image distinctions discarded by the lossy codec.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
The fixed deterministic map $x\mapsto \mathbf z=(\operatorname{idx}_{\mathcal C}\circ\Phi)(x)\in\{1,\dots,K\}^L$ pushes the image distribution forward to a distribution over index sequences on the finite alphabet $\mathcal V=\{1,\dots,K\}$. An autoregressive model over $\mathcal V^L$ is, by definition (<XRef id="def-autoregressive-lm" />), the chain-rule factorization $p_\theta(\mathbf z)=\prod_t p_\theta(z_t\mid z_{<t})$ with each conditional a softmax over $|\mathcal V|=K$ logits. The construction of the autoregressive loss uses only: (i) the chain rule, which is alphabet-agnostic; (ii) the softmax over the finite label set, here of size $K$; (iii) the identity that the *expected* loss over the data equals the data entropy plus $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{data}}\,\|\,p_\theta)$, up to the $1/T$ average (<XRef id="prop-ce-kl-training" />), which references the alphabet only through the sum over its symbols; and (iv) teacher forcing (<XRef id="def-teacher-forcing" />), which conditions on ground-truth prefixes and is likewise indifferent to token meaning. None of (i)–(iv) mentions text, so each transfers unchanged under $\mathcal V\to\{1,\dots,K\}$. Sampling composes the learned autoregressive sampler with the fixed codebook lookup $\mathcal E_{\mathcal C}$ and decoder $\Psi$. This exactly models the pushed-forward code law and its decoded reconstruction law, not information the codec erased. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="What is genuinely new versus reused" id="rem-new-vs-reused">
The reused part is everything inside the sequence model: <XRef id="thm-dual-footing" /> shows the transformer, its loss, and its sampler are the text apparatus untouched — this is the dual-footing thesis (<XRef id="rem-modality-blind" />) made exact for generation. The genuinely new mathematics is everything *around* the sequence: the quantizer and its Voronoi geometry (<XRef id="prop-voronoi" />), the straight-through estimator (<XRef id="thm-ste-identity" />) that makes the discrete waist trainable, and the k-means codebook (<XRef id="thm-codebook-kmeans" />). This is the precise sense in which "image generation as a language model" is both a real reduction and a real new construction — the codec is new, the language model is not. The same recipe with a convolutional/transformer codec and an autoregressive prior underlies VQ-VAE-2 and VQGAN <Cite key="razavi2019vqvae2" /><Cite key="esser2021vqgan" />; DALL·E <Cite key="ramesh2021dalle" /> keeps the tokenize-then-model pipeline but trains its discrete VAE with a Gumbel-softmax relaxation in place of the VQ/straight-through codec.
</Theorem>

## The rate of the code

A discrete codec is lossy, and its discreteness caps how much it can say about an image. The bound is elementary information theory and sets the fidelity ceiling no prior over codes can break.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Rate of the visual code" id="prop-rate">
An index sequence of length $L$ over a codebook of size $K$ carries at most
$$
H(\mathbf z) \;\le\; L\log_2 K \quad\text{bits},
$$
with equality iff the codes are independent and uniformly distributed (uniform maximum from <XRef id="def-entropy" />; the joint-entropy subadditivity supplying the independence half is standard and taken as given). Consequently the image information recoverable from the code is at most $L\log_2 K$ bits (the bottleneck bound of <XRef id="prop-connector-data-processing-bound" /> with $\mathbf S=\mathbf z$), and two images sharing an index sequence are identical after decoding. Only a larger $K$ or $L$ can raise this **information-rate upper bound**; at fixed rate, a better encoder, codebook, or decoder can still reduce attained reconstruction distortion. Codebook size and code length are the rate knobs in a rate–distortion trade.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
The entropy of a sequence of $L$ symbols satisfies $H(\mathbf z)\le\sum_{t=1}^L H(z_t)$ by subadditivity of joint entropy (standard; not proved in this guide), and each $H(z_t)\le\log_2 K$ because a distribution on $K$ outcomes has maximal entropy $\log_2 K$ at the uniform; equality throughout requires independence (for the first inequality) and uniform marginals (for the second), i.e. i.i.d. uniform codes. By <XRef id="prop-connector-data-processing-bound" /> with discrete bottleneck $\mathbf S=\mathbf z$, $I(X;\hat X)\le H(\mathbf z)\le L\log_2 K$. Finally, decoding is a function of $\mathbf z$ alone, so any two images with the same $\mathbf z$ map to the same $\hat x$. Their distinguishing information may already have been lost by a non-injective encoder, and additional latent distinctions can be collapsed by $q_L$; how much pixel-space error those losses induce depends on the encoder and decoder geometry, but *what the codec discarded cannot be recovered* by any prior over the codes. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

**Exercise 21.1 (Prove).** Prove that the quantizer $q$ of <XRef id="def-codebook" /> has $\partial q/\partial\mathbf z = \mathbf 0$ at every point interior to a Voronoi cell, and characterize the exceptional set. Why does this make end-to-end training impossible without the STE?

<SolutionBox>

Fix $\mathbf z$ interior to $R_k$, so $\mathbf e_k$ is the strict nearest code on a neighborhood $U$ of $\mathbf z$. On $U$, $q\equiv\mathbf e_k$ is constant, hence every partial derivative is zero and $\partial q/\partial\mathbf z=\mathbf 0$ (<XRef id="prop-voronoi" />). The exceptional set is the Voronoi boundary where two or more nearest codes tie. It is **contained in** the finite union of pairwise bisector hyperplanes $\{\mathbf z:\norm{\mathbf z-\mathbf e_k}=\norm{\mathbf z-\mathbf e_j}\}$, so it has Lebesgue measure zero; a bisector point where some third code is strictly nearer need not be exceptional. Because the Jacobian is zero a.e., the true chain rule sends the reconstruction gradient through a zero rowwise quantizer Jacobian: the encoder receives no reconstruction gradient and cannot learn, which is exactly what the STE's identity Jacobian (<XRef id="thm-ste-identity" />) repairs. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 21.2 (Prove).** From the stop-gradient definition (<XRef id="def-ste" />), prove both clauses of <XRef id="thm-ste-identity" />: the forward pass returns $q_L(\mathbf z_e)$ and the backward Jacobian is $\mathbf I$. Then explain in one sentence why the estimator is biased.

<SolutionBox>

Forward: $\sg$ is the identity, so $\mathbf z_q=\mathbf z_e+(q_L(\mathbf z_e)-\mathbf z_e)=q_L(\mathbf z_e)$. Backward: $\partial\mathbf z_q/\partial\mathbf z_e=\partial\mathbf z_e/\partial\mathbf z_e+\partial\,\sg[\cdot]/\partial\mathbf z_e=\mathbf I+\mathbf 0=\mathbf I$, since the stop-gradient term has zero Jacobian by definition. So $\partial\mathcal L/\partial\mathbf z_e=\partial\mathcal L/\partial\mathbf z_q$. It is biased because the *true* Jacobian of the forward rowwise quantizer $q_L$ is $\mathbf 0$ a.e. (<XRef id="prop-voronoi" />), not $\mathbf I$ — the STE substitutes a deliberately wrong but informative derivative. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 21.3 (Derive).** Using the stop-gradient placement in <XRef id="def-vq-loss" />, derive the aggregate gradient for each code vector $\mathbf e_k$ and the commitment gradient for each encoder row $\mathbf z_e^{(t)}$, confirming <XRef id="prop-vq-gradient" />. Explain why omitting the commitment term ($\beta=0$) can let encoder outputs drift away from the codebook.

<SolutionBox>

For fixed assignments $S_k$, the codebook term involving $\mathbf e_k$ is $\sum_{t\in S_k}\norm{\sg[\mathbf z_e^{(t)}]-\mathbf e_k}^2$. The stopped encoder rows are constants in backpropagation, so
$$
\frac{\partial\mathcal L_{\mathrm{codebook}}}{\partial\mathbf e_k}
=2\sum_{t\in S_k}(\mathbf e_k-\mathbf z_e^{(t)}),
\qquad
\frac{\partial\mathcal L_{\mathrm{codebook}}}{\partial\mathbf z_e^{(t)}}=\mathbf 0.
$$
For $t\in S_k$, the commitment summand is $\beta\norm{\mathbf z_e^{(t)}-\sg[\mathbf e_k]}^2$, hence its encoder gradient is $2\beta(\mathbf z_e^{(t)}-\mathbf e_k)$ and its codebook gradient is zero. With $\beta=0$ there is no direct force pulling an encoder row toward its assigned code: reconstruction still trains the encoder through the STE and the codebook term moves codes toward encoder rows, but the encoder itself can drift relative to the codebook and destabilize assignments. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 21.4 (Prove).** Prove the codebook-loss minimizer is the Voronoi-cell centroid (<XRef id="thm-codebook-kmeans" />), and identify the assignment and update steps with the two phases of Lloyd's algorithm.

<SolutionBox>

For fixed assignments $S_k$, the codebook objective $\sum_{i\in S_k}\norm{\mathbf z_e^{(i)}-\mathbf e_k}^2$ is strictly convex in $\mathbf e_k$ (a sum of quadratics with positive-definite Hessian $2|S_k|\mathbf I$). Its gradient $\sum_{i\in S_k}2(\mathbf e_k-\mathbf z_e^{(i)})$ vanishes iff $\mathbf e_k=\frac{1}{|S_k|}\sum_{i\in S_k}\mathbf z_e^{(i)}$, the centroid, which is therefore the unique minimizer. The nearest-code assignment $i\mapsto k^\star(\mathbf z_e^{(i)})$ (<XRef id="prop-voronoi" />) is Lloyd's E-step (assign points to nearest centroid); the centroid update is Lloyd's M-step (recompute centroids as cluster means). Alternating them is k-means. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 21.5 (Compute).** A VQGAN-style codec emits $L = 16\times 16 = 256$ indices over a codebook of size $K = 8192 = 2^{13}$. (a) Bound the bits per image the code can carry (<XRef id="prop-rate" />). (b) Using <XRef id="thm-dual-footing" />, state what quantity an autoregressive prior over these codes is trained to minimize, and why a lower per-token cross-entropy means a better image model.

<SolutionBox>

(a) By <XRef id="prop-rate" />, $H(\mathbf z)\le L\log_2 K = 256\cdot\log_2(2^{13}) = 256\cdot 13 = 3328$ bits per image (attained only by i.i.d. uniform codes; real codes carry fewer). (b) By <XRef id="thm-dual-footing" /> the prior is an autoregressive language model over $\mathcal V=\{1,\dots,8192\}$, trained to minimize the per-token cross-entropy $-\frac1L\sum_t\log p_\theta(z_t\mid z_{<t})$, whose *expectation over the data* equals the data entropy plus $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{data}}\,\|\,p_\theta)$, up to the $1/L$ average (<XRef id="prop-ce-kl-training" />). Lower cross-entropy means the model assigns higher likelihood to the true code sequences — its samples $\mathbf z\sim p_\theta$ match the distribution of real images' codes more closely, so looking up $\mathbf z_q=\mathcal E_{\mathcal C}(\mathbf z)$ and decoding $\Psi(\mathbf z_q)$ yields more realistic images. The fidelity ceiling is still set by the codec's $3328$-bit rate (<XRef id="prop-rate" />); the prior only controls how well that budget is used. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 21.6 (Compute).** A two-row encoder grid is $\mathbf z_e=((0.1,0.2);(1.8,0.2))$ and the codebook is $\mathbf e_1=(0,0)$, $\mathbf e_2=(2,0)$. Trace the autoencoder of <XRef id="def-autoencoder" /> through the nearest-code index and lookup maps of <XRef id="def-codebook" />. If $\Psi$ sums the two rows and the target is $x=(1,1)$, compute $\hat x$ and squared reconstruction loss.

<SolutionBox>

The first row is closer to $\mathbf e_1$ and the second to $\mathbf e_2$, so the index sequence is $(1,2)$ and
$$
\mathbf z_q=((0,0);(2,0)).
$$
The decoder sum is $\hat x=\Psi(\mathbf z_q)=(2,0)$. Therefore
$$
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{rec}}=\norm{x-\hat x}^2
=\norm{(1,1)-(2,0)}^2=(-1)^2+1^2=2.
$$
This is the full encoder-grid $\to$ indices $\to$ quantized grid $\to$ reconstruction path; the STE changes its backward derivative, not these forward values. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
