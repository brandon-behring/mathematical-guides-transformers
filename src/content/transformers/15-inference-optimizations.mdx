# Inference Optimizations

**Where we are.** Training optimized a loss; inference optimizes a different quantity — the time to emit one token on fixed hardware. This chapter extracts the algebraic core common to every production serving technique: each one moves a single term in one cost equation. We fix that equation first (the roofline model and its memory-bound regime), then derive the mathematics behind five techniques in turn — the **KV-cache** (why autoregressive decoding would otherwise recompute, and how caching collapses per-step compute from quadratic to linear), **quantization** (the affine map from floats to integers and its round-off bound), **FlashAttention** (the online-softmax recurrence that computes attention *exactly* without ever materializing the $n\times n$ score matrix), **speculative decoding** (the draft-then-verify acceptance law and its expected speedup), and **mixture-of-experts** (top-$k$ routing and the conditional-compute FLOP argument). Throughout, $\softmax$, scaled dot-product attention, and the standing dimensions $n,d,d_k$ are as fixed in earlier chapters. We give definitions and inline proofs for the quantitative claims and reserve remarks for the qualitative engineering facts.

## The cost-per-token equation

A device finishes an operation no faster than the slower of two clocks: the rate at which it can do arithmetic, and the rate at which it can stream the operands in from memory. A single decode step performs some number of floating-point operations and reads some number of bytes; its time is governed by whichever of the two is the binding constraint. This is the roofline picture, and it is the entire conceptual frame for the chapter.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Inference cost-per-token equation" id="def-cost-eq">
For a device with peak arithmetic rate $F$ (operations per second) and memory bandwidth $\mathcal B$ (bytes per second), a decode step that performs $\Phi$ floating-point operations and reads $\beta$ bytes from memory takes time at least
$$
T_{\text{token}} \defeq \max\!\left(\frac{\Phi}{F},\ \frac{\beta}{\mathcal B}\right).
$$
The step is **compute-bound** when $\Phi/F \ge \beta/\mathcal B$ and **memory-bound** otherwise. The two are equal at the **arithmetic intensity**
$$
I \defeq \frac{\Phi}{\beta} \;=\; \frac{F}{\mathcal B} \;=\; I^*,
$$
the **ridge point** $I^*$ of the device.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Ridge point">$I^* = F/\mathcal B$. Above it (high arithmetic per byte) the device saturates at $F$; below it, at $\mathcal B\cdot I$.</MarginNote>

The ridge point is a property of the hardware alone; a workload is fast or slow according to which side of $I^*$ its intensity falls. The next proposition makes the memory-bound half quantitative, because that is the half almost every decode step lives in.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="The memory-bound ceiling" id="prop-memory-bound">
A workload with arithmetic intensity $I < I^*$ has time per token $T_{\text{token}} \ge \beta/\mathcal B$ and achieved throughput at most $\mathcal B\cdot I$ floating-point operations per second, regardless of how large $F$ is.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
When $I < I^*$, divide both sides by $F$: $\Phi/(\beta F) < 1/\mathcal B$, i.e. $\Phi/F < \beta/\mathcal B$. So the second argument of the $\max$ in <XRef id="def-cost-eq" /> dominates and $T_{\text{token}} = \beta/\mathcal B$. Achieved throughput is the operations done divided by the time taken,
$$
\frac{\Phi}{T_{\text{token}}} = \frac{\Phi}{\beta/\mathcal B} = \mathcal B\cdot\frac{\Phi}{\beta} = \mathcal B\cdot I,
$$
which is independent of $F$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="One question">For any kernel, compute $I=\Phi/\beta$ once and compare to $I^*$. Below the ridge, time is $\beta/\mathcal B$ and the FLOP rate is whatever falls out.</MarginNote>

The proposition isolates the lever. Below the ridge, the only way to go faster is to read fewer bytes (smaller $\beta$) or to do more arithmetic per byte read (larger $I$). Every technique in the rest of the chapter is one of those two moves. The qualitative split between the two inference phases explains why decode is the phase that matters here.

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Prefill is compute-bound; decode is memory-bound" id="rem-prefill-decode">
Prefill processes the whole prompt at once: the dominant products have shape $\R^{n\times d}\cdot\R^{d\times d}$ with $n$ in the thousands, so a weight read of $O(d^2)$ bytes is amortized across $O(n)$ rows: the weight term alone gives $I\approx n$, and activation reads and writes cap the total near $d/b$ once $n \gtrsim d$ — comfortably above the ridge either way, hence compute-bound. Decode generates one token at a time: each step has a single new query against the cached keys and values, so the matrix product degenerates to a matrix–vector product, the weight read is amortized across one row, and $I$ collapses to $\sim 1$. "Inference is memory-bound" is shorthand for "the decode phase is memory-bound."
</Theorem>

## The KV-cache

Autoregressive decoding emits token $t$ from a query at position $t$ attending to the keys and values of all positions $\le t$. Those keys and values are deterministic functions of tokens already fixed, so recomputing them at every step would redo work that cannot change. Caching them is the obvious fix; its payoff is precisely a change of asymptotic order in the per-step compute, which we now prove.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="KV-cache" id="def-kv-cache">
During decoding, the **KV-cache** stores, for each layer, the key and value vectors of every generated position. With $h_k$ distinct key heads and $h_v$ distinct value heads, after $n$ tokens one layer holds the distinct-head tensors flattened as $\mathbf K_{\text{cache}}\in\R^{n\times h_kd_k}$ and $\mathbf V_{\text{cache}}\in\R^{n\times h_vd_v}$. Their combined row width is $d_{\text{cache}}=h_kd_k+h_vd_v$ from <XRef id="prop-kv-cache" />. Step $n+1$ computes only the new key and value rows, appends them, and reuses all earlier rows unchanged. That the reuse is *exact* — cached and uncached decoding emit identical distributions — is the prefix-reuse proposition <XRef id="prop-prefix-reuse" />, a consequence of causality alone.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Cache size">Per layer: $n\,d_{\text{cache}}=n(h_kd_k+h_vd_v)$ elements — linear in context length. Standard MHA with $hd_k=hd_v=d$ gives $2nd$.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="KV-cache memory" id="prop-kv-memory">
For a model with $N$ layers, let layer $\ell$ have cache width $d_{\text{cache},\ell}$ and store each element in $b$ bytes. The KV-cache for one request at context length $n$ occupies
$$
M_{\text{KV}}=n b\sum_{\ell=1}^{N}d_{\text{cache},\ell}.
$$
If every layer has the same $d_{\text{cache}}=h_kd_k+h_vd_v$, this becomes $Nnbd_{\text{cache}}$. For standard MHA with $h_k=h_v=h$ and $hd_k=hd_v=d$ in every layer, it further specializes to $2Nndb$; heterogeneous GQA and MQA layers use their own smaller widths from <XRef id="prop-kv-cache" /> inside the sum.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Layer $\ell$ stores $n d_{\text{cache},\ell}$ key/value scalars (<XRef id="def-kv-cache" />), hence $nbd_{\text{cache},\ell}$ bytes. The layers store disjoint caches, so summing gives $nb\sum_\ell d_{\text{cache},\ell}$. Equal widths factor out as $Nnbd_{\text{cache}}$. Under the all-MHA specialization, every $d_{\text{cache},\ell}=hd_k+hd_v=2d$, giving $2Nndb$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Count the redo">An uncached step $t$ re-forms all $\Theta(t^2)$ prefix scores; a cached step forms only the $\Theta(t)$ scores of the one new query. Summing over the sequence is what turns that per-step gap into a factor of $n$.</MarginNote>

The cache trades memory for compute. The trade is favorable because it changes the order of the per-step work, not merely its constant — and the cumulative effect over a full generation is one order of $n$.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Caching makes per-step attention compute linear" id="prop-decode-cost">
Fix one attention layer. At decode step $t$, computing the attention output for the new position against all $t$ positions costs $\Theta(t\,d + d^2)$ arithmetic with a KV-cache (the $d^2$ for projecting the new token), versus $\Theta(t\,d^2 + t^2 d)$ without one (recomputing every key and value from scratch; the quadratic term dominates once $t \ge d$). Consequently, generating a sequence of length $n$ costs $\Theta(n^2 d + n\,d^2)$ with the cache and $\Theta(n^2 d^2 + n^3 d)$ without it.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Cached step.** The keys and values for positions $1,\dots,t-1$ are already stored. The step computes the new query, keys, values, and output projection ($\Theta(d^2)$, independent of $t$ and absorbed below). Across the $h$ query heads, the new query then forms $t$ key inner products of width $d_k$ and $t$-row value combinations of width $d_v$, for
$$
\Theta\!\bigl(t\,h(d_k+d_v)\bigr)=\Theta(t\,d)
$$
under the standard-width relations $hd_k=\Theta(d)$ and $hd_v=\Theta(d)$. GQA and MQA reduce the number of *distinct cached* K/V heads, but every query head still performs its own score and value aggregation.

**Uncached step.** Without a cache, step $t$ must regenerate the keys and values for *all* $t$ positions and then attend. Regenerating $t$ keys and values is $\Theta(t\,d^2)$, and the score matrix for the prefix of length $t$ is $\Theta(t^2 d)$ inner products; together $\Theta(t\,d^2 + t^2 d)$, the score term dominating once $t \ge d$.

**Whole sequence.** Summing the per-step costs over $t=1,\dots,n$,
$$
\text{cached: }\ \sum_{t=1}^{n}\Theta(t\,d + d^2)=\Theta(n^2 d + n\,d^2),
\qquad
\text{uncached: }\ \sum_{t=1}^{n}\Theta(t\,d^2 + t^2 d)=\Theta(n^2 d^2 + n^3 d),
$$
using $\sum_{t=1}^n t=\Theta(n^2)$ and $\sum_{t=1}^n t^2=\Theta(n^3)$. Comparing matching terms, regeneration $t\,d^2$ replaces the cached step's $d^2$, and the full prefix score matrix $t^2d$ replaces the new-query work $td$: the cache removes a factor $\Theta(t)$ from either source of repeated work at step $t$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Why cache">Per step: $O(t\,d^2 + t^2 d)\to O(t\,d + d^2)$. Over a generation: $O(n^2 d^2 + n^3 d)\to O(n^2 d + n\,d^2)$. The cache spends $O(nd)$ memory per layer to buy back one factor of $n$ in time.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="The cache is the byte budget, and paging packs it" id="rem-kv-bytes">
By <XRef id="prop-kv-memory" /> the cache grows linearly in context length and in batch size, so at long context (or large batch) it overtakes the weight stream as the dominant term in $\beta$ of <XRef id="def-cost-eq" />, making it the binding read each decode step. Two qualitative engineering moves follow. *Paged attention* stores the cache in fixed-size blocks with a per-request page table instead of one contiguous reservation per request, so internal fragmentation is bounded by one block rather than by the maximum context length; this raises the number of concurrent requests that fit, which raises batch and thus arithmetic intensity, but it does not change the per-request decode time. *KV quantization* lowers $b$ (e.g. INT8 halves it), shrinking $M_{\text{KV}}$ proportionally at some cost in decode quality. Both are tunings of terms in the same equation. A third response changes the term's *shape* rather than its constants: the $nb\sum_\ell d_{\text{cache},\ell}$ bytes (or $Nnbd_{\text{cache}}$ for equal-width layers) are the price of a **lossless** prefix memory, and the recurrent route replaces them with a fixed $O(d_s d)$ state per layer — constant in $n$ — at the cost quantified by the recall bound <XRef id="prop-state-recall" /> and priced for hybrid stacks by <XRef id="prop-decode-state" />.
</Theorem>

## Quantization

The other term in $\beta$ is the weight stream. Storing each weight in fewer bits reads fewer bytes and — in the memory-bound regime of <XRef id="prop-memory-bound" /> — raises throughput in direct proportion. The map from a floating-point tensor to a low-bit integer tensor is affine, and its fidelity is captured by a single round-off bound.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Affine quantization" id="def-affine-quant">
Fix a bit width yielding integer levels $\{0,1,\dots,2^{B}-1\}$ for $B$-bit quantization. Given a weight block with range $[w_{\min}, w_{\max}]$, the **scale** and **zero-point** are
$$
s \defeq \frac{w_{\max}-w_{\min}}{2^{B}-1}, \qquad z \defeq \operatorname{round}\!\left(-\frac{w_{\min}}{s}\right).
$$
The **quantize** map $Q$ and **dequantize** map $D$ are
$$
Q(w) \defeq \operatorname{clamp}\!\big(\operatorname{round}(w/s)+z,\ 0,\ 2^{B}-1\big),
\qquad
D(q) \defeq s\,(q - z).
$$
The stored tensor is $Q(w)$ in $B$ bits; computation uses the reconstruction $\widehat w \defeq D(Q(w))$.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Step size">The reconstruction grid has spacing $s$. INT8 over a fixed range has $s$ sixteen-fold smaller than INT4 ($(2^8{-}1)/(2^4{-}1) = 17 \approx 2^4$) — one byte versus half a byte.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Round-off error bound" id="prop-quant-error">
For any weight $w\in[w_{\min},w_{\max}]$ whose quantized value is not clamped, the reconstruction $\widehat w = D(Q(w))$ satisfies
$$
\big|\,w - \widehat w\,\big| \;\le\; \frac{s}{2} \;=\; \frac{w_{\max}-w_{\min}}{2\,(2^{B}-1)}.
$$
Each additional bit of precision halves the worst-case error.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Write $r \defeq w/s + z$, so by <XRef id="def-affine-quant" /> the unclamped quantized value is $Q(w)=\operatorname{round}(r)$. Rounding to the nearest integer obeys $|\operatorname{round}(r)-r|\le \tfrac12$. Dequantizing,
$$
\widehat w = s\big(\operatorname{round}(r)-z\big),\qquad
w = s\big(r - z\big),
$$
so $w-\widehat w = s\big(r-\operatorname{round}(r)\big)$ and $|w-\widehat w| = s\,|r-\operatorname{round}(r)| \le s/2$. Substituting $s=(w_{\max}-w_{\min})/(2^{B}-1)$ gives the stated form. Replacing $B$ by $B+1$ multiplies $2^{B}-1$ by roughly $2$, halving the bound. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Bandwidth ÷ precision">In the memory-bound regime, throughput $\propto 1/b$. INT8 ($b{=}1$) roughly doubles tokens/sec over FP16 ($b{=}2$); INT4 quadruples it — until the workload crosses the ridge.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Activations are harder than weights" id="rem-activation-quant">
The bound above is for *weights*, which are fixed after training, so quantization is a one-time, reversible reparameterization: if quality regresses, one dequantizes. *Activation* quantization quantizes the running tensors at every layer, injecting fresh round-off noise at each step, and activation distributions carry per-batch outliers with a much wider effective range — inflating $s$ and hence the error bound of <XRef id="prop-quant-error" /> for the same bit width. This is why low-bit weight quantization is routine while low-bit activation quantization needs outlier-aware schemes; the formal object is the same affine map, but applied to a worse-conditioned range.
</Theorem>

## FlashAttention

Standard attention forms the full $n\times n$ score matrix, applies a row-wise $\softmax$, and multiplies by the values — writing and re-reading $\Theta(n^2)$ numbers from memory. FlashAttention <Cite key="dao2022flashattention" /> computes the *same function* while streaming the keys and values in blocks and never storing the score matrix. The mathematical content is a recurrence that updates a running maximum and a running normalizer so that the softmax is assembled exactly, one block at a time.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Online-softmax recurrence" id="def-online-softmax">
Let a query have scores $s_1,\dots,s_n$ (one per key) and let $\mathbf v_1,\dots,\mathbf v_n$ be the value rows. Process the keys left to right, maintaining a running maximum $m$, a running normalizer $\ell$, and a running output accumulator $\mathbf o$, initialized $m_0 = -\infty$, $\ell_0 = 0$, $\mathbf o_0 = \mathbf 0$. On reading score $s_j$ with value $\mathbf v_j$,
$$
\begin{aligned}
m_j &= \max(m_{j-1},\, s_j), \\
\ell_j &= \ell_{j-1}\,e^{\,m_{j-1}-m_j} + e^{\,s_j-m_j}, \\
\mathbf o_j &= \mathbf o_{j-1}\,e^{\,m_{j-1}-m_j} + e^{\,s_j-m_j}\,\mathbf v_j.
\end{aligned}
$$
The block output after all $n$ keys is $\mathbf o_n/\ell_n$.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Rescale factor">When the max grows from $m_{j-1}$ to $m_j$, every quantity carried so far is multiplied by $e^{m_{j-1}-m_j}\le 1$ — the correction that keeps the shifted exponentials consistent.</MarginNote>

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before the proof: translation invariance of softmax (proved earlier) lets you subtract *any* constant from all scores. The recurrence subtracts the running max $m_j$ rather than the global max. What must the rescale factor $e^{m_{j-1}-m_j}$ repair when $m_j>m_{j-1}$?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="FlashAttention computes exact attention" id="thm-online-softmax-exact">
With $m_n = \max_{j} s_j$, the online recurrence of <XRef id="def-online-softmax" /> yields, for every query,
$$
\ell_n = \sum_{j=1}^{n} e^{\,s_j - m_n}, \qquad
\frac{\mathbf o_n}{\ell_n} = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{e^{\,s_j}}{\sum_{k} e^{\,s_k}}\,\mathbf v_j
= \big[\softmax(\mathbf s)\,\mathbf V\big],
$$
i.e. it equals scaled dot-product attention (<XRef id="def-softmax" />) exactly. The HBM traffic is $\Theta(n^2 d^2 / M)$ bytes for on-chip memory of size $M$, against $\Theta(n^2 + nd)$ for the materialized kernel, with no change to the arithmetic or the output.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
We show by induction the invariant
$$
\ell_j = \sum_{i=1}^{j} e^{\,s_i - m_j}, \qquad \mathbf o_j = \sum_{i=1}^{j} e^{\,s_i - m_j}\,\mathbf v_i,
\tag{$\star$}
$$
with $m_j=\max_{i\le j}s_i$. The claim is then the case $j=n$, after dividing.

**Base case.** For $j=1$: $m_1=s_1$, and the updates give $\ell_1 = 0\cdot e^{-\infty-s_1}+e^{s_1-s_1}=1=\sum_{i\le 1}e^{s_i-m_1}$, and likewise $\mathbf o_1 = e^{s_1-m_1}\mathbf v_1$. So $(\star)$ holds.

**Inductive step.** Assume $(\star)$ at $j-1$. By the max update, $m_j=\max(m_{j-1},s_j)$, so $e^{m_{j-1}-m_j}$ is exactly the factor that re-centers every term written with offset $m_{j-1}$ to offset $m_j$, since $e^{s_i-m_{j-1}}\cdot e^{m_{j-1}-m_j}=e^{s_i-m_j}$. Therefore
$$
\ell_j = \underbrace{\ell_{j-1}\,e^{m_{j-1}-m_j}}_{\sum_{i\le j-1} e^{s_i-m_j}} + \underbrace{e^{s_j-m_j}}_{i=j\text{ term}} = \sum_{i=1}^{j} e^{s_i-m_j},
$$
and the identical algebra on the value-weighted accumulator gives $\mathbf o_j=\sum_{i\le j}e^{s_i-m_j}\mathbf v_i$. This is $(\star)$ at $j$.

**Conclusion.** At $j=n$, $m_n=\max_i s_i$ and $(\star)$ reads $\ell_n=\sum_i e^{s_i-m_n}$, $\mathbf o_n=\sum_i e^{s_i-m_n}\mathbf v_i$. Dividing, the common factor $e^{-m_n}$ cancels (translation invariance, <XRef id="prop-softmax-translation-inv" />):
$$
\frac{\mathbf o_n}{\ell_n} = \frac{\sum_i e^{s_i-m_n}\mathbf v_i}{\sum_i e^{s_i-m_n}} = \frac{\sum_i e^{s_i}\mathbf v_i}{\sum_i e^{s_i}} = \softmax(\mathbf s)\,\mathbf V.
$$
The output is the materialized-kernel output. For the IO count, tiling the queries into blocks of $M/d$ rows reads all $n$ keys and values once per query block; there are $\Theta(nd/M)$ such blocks, each reading $\Theta(nd)$ bytes, for $\Theta(n^2 d^2/M)$ total — and the $n\times n$ matrix is never written. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Exact, not approximate">FlashAttention changes the *memory access pattern*, not the function. Same FLOPs ($O(n^2d)$), same output — only the $O(n^2)$ HBM writes disappear.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="The big-O is invariant across versions" id="rem-flash-versions">
Successive FlashAttention variants reorganize the *parallelism* of the same recurrence — for instance distributing work along the sequence dimension rather than only across heads, raising the fraction of the device kept busy. These change the constant factor in $\Theta(n^2 d^2/M)$, not the bound, and they leave <XRef id="thm-online-softmax-exact" /> untouched: the tile-plus-online-softmax computation is exact by construction in every version. The technique's gain is realized only where attention is memory-bound (large $n$); at very short sequences the $n\times n$ matrix already fits on chip and there is nothing to save.
</Theorem>

## Speculative decoding

The previous techniques shrank $\beta$. Speculative decoding <Cite key="leviathan2023speculative" /> attacks the per-token cost from the other side: a cheap draft model proposes several tokens, and one expensive forward pass of the target model verifies them all at once. Because a memory-bound target reads its weights once whether it scores one position or several, verifying $K{+}1$ candidates costs essentially the same as emitting one token — so every accepted draft is nearly free. The payoff is governed by how many drafts survive verification, which is a short expectation.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Speculative decoding" id="def-spec-decode">
A small **draft model** generates $K$ candidate tokens autoregressively. The **target model** performs one parallel forward pass over the prefix and the $K$ candidates, producing its distribution at each position. Candidates are checked left to right under a rejection-sampling rule that accepts each with probability matching the target distribution; on the first rejection the target's own resampled token is used at that position, and the remaining drafts are discarded. The accepted prefix seeds the next cycle.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Per cycle">One target pass (cost $1$) plus $K$ draft passes (cost $Kc$, where $c$ is the draft/target per-token cost ratio), in exchange for an expected $\E[T_K]$ accepted tokens.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Expected tokens and speedup per cycle" id="prop-spec-speedup">
Suppose each candidate is accepted independently with the same probability $\alpha\in[0,1]$. Then the expected number of tokens produced per cycle (the accepted drafts plus one guaranteed target token) is
$$
\E[T_K] = \frac{1-\alpha^{K+1}}{1-\alpha} \qquad (\alpha<1),
$$
and the expected speedup over one-token-at-a-time target decoding is
$$
\text{speedup} = \frac{\E[T_K]}{1 + Kc},
$$
where $c$ is the per-token cost of the draft relative to one target forward pass.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Expected tokens.** Accepting independently with probability $\alpha$, the first rejection occurs after exactly $j$ acceptances ($0\le j\le K-1$) with probability $\alpha^j(1-\alpha)$, and the cycle then outputs $j{+}1$ tokens (the $j$ drafts plus the target's resample). If all $K$ are accepted — probability $\alpha^K$ — the cycle outputs $K{+}1$ tokens (the $K$ drafts plus the target's free next-token sample). Hence
$$
\E[T_K] = \sum_{j=0}^{K-1}(j+1)\,\alpha^j(1-\alpha) \;+\; (K+1)\,\alpha^K.
$$
A cleaner route to the closed form: since each of the $K$ drafts is accepted iff all earlier ones were, the $i$-th draft contributes an accepted token with probability $\alpha^{i}$, and the one target token is always produced. Summing the indicator expectations,
$$
\E[T_K] = 1 + \sum_{i=1}^{K}\alpha^{i} = \sum_{i=0}^{K}\alpha^{i} = \frac{1-\alpha^{K+1}}{1-\alpha},
$$
the last step the finite geometric series.

**Speedup.** Each cycle costs one target pass ($1$ unit) plus $K$ draft passes ($Kc$ units) and yields $\E[T_K]$ tokens, so its tokens-per-unit-cost is $\E[T_K]/(1+Kc)$. Plain target decoding yields one token per target pass, i.e. one token per unit cost. The ratio of the two rates is the displayed speedup. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Saturation in K">$\E[T_K]\to 1/(1-\alpha)$ as $K\to\infty$: accepted tokens cap at $1/(1-\alpha)$, so beyond a few drafts the numerator stalls while $Kc$ keeps growing — there is an interior-optimal $K$.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Why a memory-bound target makes it pay" id="rem-spec-regime">
The speedup exceeds one when $\E[T_K] > 1 + Kc$, which for a cheap draft ($c\to 0$) holds for any $\alpha>0$ since $\E[T_K]\ge 1+\alpha$. The hidden premise is that one target pass over $K{+}1$ candidates costs about the same as one target pass over a single token. That is exactly the memory-bound regime of <XRef id="prop-memory-bound" />: the target reads its full weight set once per pass regardless of how many positions it scores, so the verification of many candidates is amortized into a single weight read. In a compute-bound regime (large batches, prefill) the verification pass costs proportionally more arithmetic and the free-lunch argument fails.
</Theorem>

## Mixture-of-experts

A dense feed-forward layer applies the same parameters to every token. A mixture-of-experts layer <Cite key="shazeer2017moe" /> holds many expert sub-networks but routes each token to only a few. Three quantities must not be collapsed: the bytes needed to keep all experts **resident**, the arithmetic of the expert calls actually **activated**, and the weight **traffic** generated by the union of experts used by one served batch. The roofline equation prices compute and traffic; residency is a capacity constraint.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Top-k mixture-of-experts layer" id="def-moe">
A **top-$k$ mixture-of-experts** layer has $E$ expert networks $\{\mathrm{FFN}_1,\dots,\mathrm{FFN}_E\}$ of identical shape and a router $\mathbf W_r\in\R^{d\times E}$. For token $\mathbf x_t\in\R^{d}$ the router scores $\mathbf s_t = \mathbf W_r^\top\mathbf x_t$, selects the index set $\mathcal S_t$ of the $k$ largest scores, and outputs
$$
\mathrm{MoE}(\mathbf x_t) = \sum_{i\in\mathcal S_t} \softmax(\mathbf s_t)_i\,\mathrm{FFN}_i(\mathbf x_t).
$$
For a served batch of $T$ token positions, define the **active-expert union**
$$
\mathcal A_B\defeq\bigcup_{t=1}^{T}\mathcal S_t,
\qquad
A_B\defeq|\mathcal A_B|,
\qquad
k\le A_B\le\min(E,Tk).
$$
Experts outside $\mathcal S_t$ are not evaluated for token $t$; experts outside $\mathcal A_B$ are not used anywhere in that batch.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Three quantities">Resident: $E\mu$. Activated: $Tk\Phi$. Ideal batch traffic: $A_B\mu$. Only the last two enter the basic roofline time.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="MoE resident capacity, activated compute, and traffic" id="prop-moe-flops">
Let one expert's parameters occupy $\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ bytes and one expert call cost $\Phi_{\text{FFN}}$ FLOPs. For the $T$-token batch of <XRef id="def-moe" />, ignoring the lower-order router and activation traffic:
1. **resident capacity** for the unsharded layer is $E\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ bytes;
2. **activated compute** is $Tk\Phi_{\text{FFN}}$ FLOPs;
3. if every active expert's weights are streamed once and reused across all routed tokens, the ideal expert-weight **traffic** is $A_B\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ bytes. Re-reads can only increase this term.

The corresponding expert-kernel roofline bound and ideal arithmetic intensity are
$$
T_{\text{MoE}}\ge
\max\!\left(\frac{Tk\Phi_{\text{FFN}}}{F},
\frac{A_B\mu_{\text{FFN}}}{\mathcal B}\right),
\qquad
I_{\text{MoE}}=\frac{Tk\Phi_{\text{FFN}}}{A_B\mu_{\text{FFN}}}.
$$
Relative to one dense expert applied to the same $T$ tokens with one weight read, $I_{\text{MoE}}/I_{\text{dense}}=k/A_B$. The familiar $k/E$ ratio is only the limiting case $A_B=E$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Resident capacity.** There are $E$ parameter-disjoint experts of $\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ bytes each, so holding the unsharded layer requires $E\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ bytes whether or not a particular batch visits all of them.

**Activated compute.** Every one of the $T$ tokens calls exactly $k$ experts, producing $Tk$ calls at $\Phi_{\text{FFN}}$ FLOPs each and hence $Tk\Phi_{\text{FFN}}$ FLOPs.

**Traffic.** The batch uses exactly the experts in $\mathcal A_B$. Under the stated once-streamed idealization, each of those $A_B$ weight sets contributes $\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ bytes once, for $A_B\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ bytes. A schedule or cache too small to retain an active expert may fetch it again, so this is the ideal working-set traffic, not a claim that physical traffic can never be larger.

Substitution of the activated FLOPs and ideal traffic into <XRef id="def-cost-eq" /> gives the bound and their quotient gives $I_{\text{MoE}}$. A dense expert applied to $T$ tokens performs $T\Phi_{\text{FFN}}$ FLOPs for one $\mu_{\text{FFN}}$-byte weight stream, so its ideal intensity is $T\Phi_{\text{FFN}}/\mu_{\text{FFN}}$; division leaves $k/A_B$. Setting $A_B=E$ yields $k/E$. For a single token, $T=1$ and $A_B=k$, so the ideal intensity equals the dense expert's: conditional compute reduces the number of expert calls relative to evaluating all $E$, but does not by itself make that one routed token stream all $E$ experts. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Reuse decides traffic">If all tokens repeat the same $k$ experts, $A_B=k$ and ideal intensity matches dense. If routing covers all experts, $A_B=E$ and the ratio becomes $k/E$. Residency remains $E\mu$ in both cases.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Expert parallelism shards the byte term" id="rem-expert-parallel">
Production *expert parallelism* partitions the $E$ experts across devices and dispatches tokens to their owners. If device $p$ holds $E_p$ experts and its active subset has size $A_{B,p}$, its resident capacity is $E_p\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ and ideal local weight traffic is $A_{B,p}\mu_{\text{FFN}}$. Across a disjoint partition, $\sum_pE_p=E$ and $\sum_pA_{B,p}=A_B$: sharding redistributes the resident and active working sets rather than turning total $A_B\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ traffic into $E\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ or eliminating it. The new cost is dynamic all-to-all communication of routed activations and outputs. Thus expert parallelism primarily solves per-device capacity and placement; its latency benefit depends on routing balance, local reuse, and communication.
</Theorem>

## Exercises

Attempt each before expanding its solution.

**Exercise 15.1 (Derive).** From <XRef id="def-cost-eq" />, show that for a fixed kernel the boundary between compute- and memory-bound is exactly the device ridge point $I^* = F/\mathcal B$, and that below it throughput is capped at $\mathcal B\cdot I$ independent of $F$.

<SolutionBox>

A kernel is compute-bound when $\Phi/F \ge \beta/\mathcal B$, i.e. $I \ge I^* = F/\mathcal B$; memory-bound is $I < I^*$, so the boundary is $I = I^*$. Below it, $T_{\text{token}} = \beta/\mathcal B$ and throughput is $\Phi/T_{\text{token}} = \mathcal B\,I$, which contains no $F$: raising the FLOP rate cannot help a memory-bound kernel. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 15.2 (Compute).** A standard-MHA model has $N=80$ layers, $d=8192$ with $hd_k=hd_v=d$, and stores its KV-cache in $b=2$ bytes. First obtain $d_{\text{cache}}$ from <XRef id="prop-kv-cache" />; then use <XRef id="prop-kv-memory" /> to give $M_{\text{KV}}$ per request at $n=8192$ and state the factor by which it grows if $n$ doubles.

<SolutionBox>

Standard MHA has $d_{\text{cache}}=hd_k+hd_v=2d=16{,}384$. Thus $M_{\text{KV}}=Nnbd_{\text{cache}}=80\cdot8192\cdot2\cdot16{,}384=21{,}474{,}836{,}480$ bytes $\approx21.5$ GB ($20$ GiB) per request, the same as the specialized formula $2Nndb$. The expression is linear in $n$, so doubling the context doubles the cache. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 15.3 (Prove).** Using <XRef id="prop-decode-cost" />, derive the full per-layer generation costs $\Theta(n^2d+nd^2)$ with a KV-cache and $\Theta(n^2d^2+n^3d)$ without one, naming the two summation identities. Under what relation between $n$ and $d$ do these simplify to $\Theta(n^2d)$ and $\Theta(n^3d)$?

<SolutionBox>

A cached step $t$ does $\Theta(td+d^2)$ work: one new query attends to $t$ keys/values and the new token is projected. An uncached step does $\Theta(td^2+t^2d)$: it regenerates all prefix K/V projections and recomputes the prefix score matrix. Using $\sum_{t=1}^nt=\Theta(n^2)$ and $\sum_{t=1}^nt^2=\Theta(n^3)$ gives
$$
\Theta(n^2d+nd^2)
\quad\text{and}\quad
\Theta(n^2d^2+n^3d),
$$
respectively. When $n\ge d$, $n^2d$ dominates $nd^2$ and $n^3d$ dominates $n^2d^2$, yielding the shorter formulas. Without that regime assumption, dropping the projection terms would be invalid. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 15.4 (Prove).** For the online-softmax recurrence of <XRef id="def-online-softmax" />, prove by induction that $\ell_j=\sum_{i\le j}e^{s_i-m_j}$, and identify precisely where translation invariance of softmax is invoked.

<SolutionBox>

Induct on $j$. Base: $m_1 = s_1$, $\ell_1 = e^{s_1 - m_1} = 1$. Step: with $m_j = \max(m_{j-1}, s_j)$, the rescale $e^{m_{j-1}-m_j}$ re-centers each stored term, $e^{s_i - m_{j-1}}e^{m_{j-1}-m_j} = e^{s_i - m_j}$, so $\ell_j = \ell_{j-1}e^{m_{j-1}-m_j} + e^{s_j - m_j} = \sum_{i\le j} e^{s_i - m_j}$. The final division $\mathbf o_n/\ell_n$ cancels the common $e^{-m_n}$ — exactly softmax translation invariance (<XRef id="prop-softmax-translation-inv" />). $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 15.5 (Compute).** A target/draft pair has $c=0.1$ and runs at $K=4$. Using <XRef id="prop-spec-speedup" />, compute the expected speedup at $\alpha=0.8$ and at $\alpha=0.4$, and state the limit of $\E[T_K]$ as $K\to\infty$ for $\alpha=0.8$.

<SolutionBox>

With $c=0.1$, $K=4$, the denominator is $1 + Kc = 1.4$. At $\alpha=0.8$: $\E[T_K] = (1 - 0.8^5)/0.2 \approx 3.36$, speedup $\approx 2.40$. At $\alpha=0.4$: $\E[T_K] = (1 - 0.4^5)/0.6 \approx 1.65$, speedup $\approx 1.18$. As $K\to\infty$ with $\alpha=0.8$, $\E[T_K]\to 1/(1-\alpha) = 5$ while draft cost $Kc$ keeps growing, so an interior $K$ is optimal. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 15.6 (Derive).** A top-$2$ MoE has $E=64$ experts. For a batch of $T=32$ token positions, compute resident capacity, activated FLOPs, ideal weight traffic, and the intensity ratio to a dense expert in two cases: (a) every token selects the same two experts; (b) the active union covers all $64$ experts. Write the answers in terms of $\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ and $\Phi_{\text{FFN}}$, then state what expert parallelism changes.

<SolutionBox>

In both cases resident capacity is $E\mu_{\text{FFN}}=64\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ and activated compute is $Tk\Phi_{\text{FFN}}=64\Phi_{\text{FFN}}$. **(a)** Repeated routing gives $A_B=2$, so ideal traffic is $2\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ and $I_{\text{MoE}}/I_{\text{dense}}=k/A_B=1$. **(b)** Full coverage gives $A_B=64$, so ideal traffic is $64\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ and the intensity ratio is $2/64=1/32$. Expert parallelism partitions the $64\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ resident capacity and the active working set across devices; it leaves the global $64\Phi_{\text{FFN}}$ activated compute and, under the same once-read idealization, the global $A_B\mu_{\text{FFN}}$ expert-weight traffic unchanged, while adding routed-activation all-to-all communication. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
