# Encoder Readouts, Contrastive Alignment, and Detection

**Where we are.** Earlier chapters built the encoder as a sequence-to-sequence map whose every layer is bidirectional and whose attention rows are convex averages of value vectors (<XRef id="prop-row-stochastic-convex" />). This chapter turns that map into a *detector*: a function from a whole document to a single class label or anomaly score. Three pieces do the work, and each reuses machinery we already proved. First, a **pooling** rule collapses the encoder's contextual states into one document vector $\mathbf r$, on which a linear head and a softmax produce class probabilities — the same softmax and cross-entropy from the notation chapter, now over labels instead of vocabulary. Second, a **contrastive objective** trains $\mathbf r$ without labels by making it identify its true partner among a batch of candidates; we prove this is exactly a temperature-scaled softmax-plus-cross-entropy, so it inherits every property of <XRef id="def-softmax" /> and <XRef id="prop-ce-kl" />. Third, the geometry of the learned embedding gives a **score function** for out-of-distribution detection, whose threshold-free quality is captured by a single ranking probability. We write $\mathbf r$ for a pooled representation, $\tau$ for a temperature, and $s(\cdot)$ for a score; everything else is fixed in the notation chapter.

## The pooled representation

The starting picture is the simplest possible detector. An encoder reads a sequence and emits one contextual vector per position; to decide *one* thing about the *whole* sequence we must compress those vectors into one. The compression is a fixed, parameter-light rule — read off a designated position, or average — and the only learned part of the decision is a linear map from that summary to class logits.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Pooled document representation" id="def-tf10-pooled-rep">
Let an encoder $Q:\R^{n\times d}\to\R^{n\times d}$ map an input sequence to contextual states $\mathbf H = Q(\mathbf X)$ with rows $\mathbf h_1,\dots,\mathbf h_n\in\R^{d}$. A **pooling** rule is a map $\operatorname{pool}:\R^{n\times d}\to\R^{d}$ producing a single **document representation** $\mathbf r \defeq \operatorname{pool}(\mathbf H)$. Two standard choices are
$$
\mathbf r_{\textsf{CLS}} = \mathbf h_{1}
\quad(\text{a designated first position}),
\qquad
\mathbf r_{\textsf{mean}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mathbf h_i
\quad(\text{the average of all positions}).
$$
In the first, a learned vector $\mathbf h_{\textsf{CLS}}$ is prepended to the input before the encoder runs, and $\mathbf r$ is read off that position's final state.<Sidenote>The designated-position convention is the one popularized for encoder classifiers <Cite key="devlin2019bert" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Pooling">Mean pooling is a row-stochastic average ($A_{ij}=1/n$), so $\mathbf r_{\textsf{mean}}$ lies in the convex hull of the token states — <XRef id="prop-row-stochastic-convex" /> again. The designated-position rule is *not* an average; the learned slot can carry information outside that hull.</MarginNote>

The next picture is the decision itself. The pooled vector is a point in $\R^{d}$; a linear map sends it to one logit per class, and the softmax of those logits is the predicted label distribution. Nothing here is new — it is the vocabulary softmax of the notation chapter with the vocabulary replaced by the label set.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Encoder-only classifier" id="def-tf10-encoder-classifier">
An **encoder-only classifier** over $C$ classes is the composition
$$
\mathbf r = \operatorname{pool}\!\big(Q(\mathbf X)\big)\in\R^{d},
\qquad
\mathbf o = \mathbf r\,\mathbf W_{\textsf{cls}} + \mathbf b \in\R^{C},
\qquad
\hat{\mathbf y} = \softmax(\mathbf o),
$$
with a **classifier head** $\mathbf W_{\textsf{cls}}\in\R^{d\times C}$, bias $\mathbf b\in\R^{C}$, and $Q$ a bidirectional encoder. Training minimizes the cross-entropy $H(\mathbf e_y,\hat{\mathbf y}) = -\log \hat y_{y}$ (<XRef id="def-cross-entropy" />) of the true label $y$ under $\hat{\mathbf y}$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Bidirectionality is the design choice, not the formalism" id="rem-tf10-bidirectional">
A detector reads a document that already exists, so there is no reason to forbid a position from attending to its right context: the causal mask of the generation setting is simply omitted, and the encoder is bidirectional. Formally this changes nothing in <XRef id="def-tf10-encoder-classifier" /> — the same pooling and head apply — but it is what lets a single position aggregate evidence from the entire sequence, which is exactly what a document-level decision needs.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="cross-ref" label="The encoder pattern">This is the mixer-agnostic encoder pattern of <XRef id="def-tf8-patterns" />: unconstrained (bidirectional) mixing, product a representation — instantiable by attention, a bidirectional recurrence, or a bidirectional scan alike.</MarginNote>

## The contrastive objective

Labeled detection data is often scarce, but *pairs* are cheap: any document and a second view of it — paired text under a second encoder, an augmentation, or, the canonical case, **the same content in another modality** (an image and its caption) — form a positive pair, and every other document in the batch is a negative. The picture to hold is a retrieval game. Lay $B$ representations on one side and their $B$ partners on the other; the objective asks each representation to pick its true partner out of the $B$ candidates by similarity. The crucial observation, which we will prove, is that "pick the true partner by similarity" is *literally* a softmax classification with the similarities as logits — so contrastive learning is the cross-entropy of the notation chapter wearing different clothes.

We first fix the similarity and its scale.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Cosine similarity with temperature" id="def-tf10-cosine-temp">
For embeddings $\mathbf u,\mathbf v\in\R^{d}$, the **cosine similarity** is $\operatorname{sim}(\mathbf u,\mathbf v) \defeq \inner{\mathbf u}{\mathbf v}/(\norm{\mathbf u}\,\norm{\mathbf v})$. When the embeddings are $L^2$-normalized ($\norm{\mathbf u}=\norm{\mathbf v}=1$) this is the plain inner product $\inner{\mathbf u}{\mathbf v}\in[-1,1]$. A **temperature** $\tau>0$ rescales it to the **similarity logit** $\inner{\mathbf u}{\mathbf v}/\tau$.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Temperature">Small $\tau$ sharpens the candidate softmax toward the single nearest partner; large $\tau$ flattens it toward uniform — exactly the temperature limits of <XRef id="rem-softmax-temperature" />. Practice learns $\log(1/\tau)$ as a parameter.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Symmetric contrastive objective (InfoNCE)" id="def-tf10-contrastive">
Let a batch of $B$ matched pairs have $L^2$-normalized embeddings stacked rowwise as $\mathbf U,\mathbf V\in\R^{B\times d}$, with row $i$ of each the two views of example $i$. Form the **similarity matrix**
$$
\mathbf M = \frac{1}{\tau}\,\mathbf U\mathbf V^{\top}\in\R^{B\times B},
\qquad
M_{ij} = \frac{\inner{\mathbf u_i}{\mathbf v_j}}{\tau}.
$$
The **row** and **column** losses are the cross-entropies of the row- and column-wise softmaxes against the diagonal target,
$$
\mathcal L_{\textsf{row}} = -\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B}\log\frac{e^{M_{ii}}}{\sum_{j=1}^{B} e^{M_{ij}}},
\qquad
\mathcal L_{\textsf{col}} = -\frac{1}{B}\sum_{j=1}^{B}\log\frac{e^{M_{jj}}}{\sum_{i=1}^{B} e^{M_{ij}}},
$$
and the **symmetric contrastive objective** is $\mathcal L_{\textsf{con}} \defeq \tfrac12(\mathcal L_{\textsf{row}} + \mathcal L_{\textsf{col}})$.<Sidenote>The in-batch softmax form is InfoNCE <Cite key="oord2018cpc" />; the symmetric dual-encoder version is the CLIP objective <Cite key="radford2021clip" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Contrastive loss is cross-entropy over pair indices" id="prop-tf10-contrastive-ce">
Each row of $\mathbf M$ is a vector of logits over the $B$ candidate $\mathbf v$-embeddings, and row-wise softmax produces the categorical distribution $p(j\mid i) = \softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j$. The $i$-th summand of $\mathcal L_{\textsf{row}}$ is the cross-entropy $H(\mathbf e_i, p(\cdot\mid i)) = -\log p(i\mid i)$ of the one-hot target $\mathbf e_i$ — "the correct partner is index $i$." The same holds column-wise with the two views exchanged. Hence $\mathcal L_{\textsf{con}}$ is an average of standard cross-entropy losses, with the temperature-scaled cosine similarities as logits.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Fix row $i$. By the definition of softmax (<XRef id="def-softmax" />) applied to the logit vector $\mathbf M_{i,:}$,
$$
p(j\mid i) \defeq \softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j = \frac{e^{M_{ij}}}{\sum_{j'=1}^{B} e^{M_{ij'}}},
$$
which is positive and sums to one over $j$, hence a categorical distribution over the $B$ candidate $\mathbf v$-embeddings. The contrastive target names the matching index, i.e. the one-hot $\mathbf e_i$. Cross-entropy against a one-hot target reduces to the negative log-probability of its support (<XRef id="def-cross-entropy" />):
$$
H(\mathbf e_i, p(\cdot\mid i)) = -\sum_{j} [\mathbf e_i]_j \log p(j\mid i) = -\log p(i\mid i) = -\log\frac{e^{M_{ii}}}{\sum_{j} e^{M_{ij}}},
$$
which is exactly the $i$-th summand of $\mathcal L_{\textsf{row}}$. Averaging over $i$ gives $\mathcal L_{\textsf{row}}$ as a mean cross-entropy. The transpose argument — read $\mathbf M$ columnwise, target $j$ on column $j$ — gives $\mathcal L_{\textsf{col}}$ identically, and $\mathcal L_{\textsf{con}}$ is their average. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

Because the objective is cross-entropy with softmax logits, every property of that machinery transfers verbatim. Translation invariance of the softmax (<XRef id="prop-softmax-translation-inv" />) says adding a constant to a whole row of $\mathbf M$ leaves $\mathcal L_{\textsf{row}}$ unchanged, so the row loss sees only the row gaps $M_{ii}-M_{ij}$. Transposing gives the corresponding statement for $\mathcal L_{\textsf{col}}$ and column gaps. The full symmetric objective contains both families; it is not invariant to an arbitrary row shift, because that same shift changes the column softmaxes (nor to an arbitrary column shift, by symmetry). The softmax Jacobian (<XRef id="prop-softmax-jacobian" />) governs the gradient: each directional loss pushes its matched logit up and every mismatched logit down, with the push on a negative proportional to the probability mass it currently steals — hard negatives receive the largest gradient.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before reading the next proof: an unconstrained logit gap can diverge, but two unit vectors have inner product only in $[-1,1]$. What does that bounded range do to the zero-loss limit at fixed temperature?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Unconstrained margins versus normalized contrastive geometry" id="prop-tf10-contrastive-alignment">
Assume $B\ge2$ and fix $\tau>0$.
1. Treating a row's logits as unconstrained, its loss $\ell_i=-\log p(i\mid i)$ has infimum $0$, approached if and only if $M_{ii}-\max_{j\ne i}M_{ij}\to+\infty$.
2. For $L^2$-normalized embeddings, every logit gap is at most $2/\tau$, so
$$
\ell_i \ge \log\!\bigl(1+(B-1)e^{-2/\tau}\bigr)>0.
$$
The normalized symmetric objective is continuous on a compact product of spheres and therefore attains a strictly positive minimum. Holding the remaining logits fixed, each directional loss is strictly decreasing in its matched logit and strictly increasing in every mismatched logit. Thus **alignment** and **separation** describe its local optimization pressures; the global optimum is a coupled spherical-code problem, not an equivalent unbounded-margin limit.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Write
$$
\ell_i=\log\!\left(1+\sum_{j\ne i}e^{M_{ij}-M_{ii}}\right).
$$
For unconstrained logits, $\ell_i\to0$ exactly when every exponential tends to zero, equivalently when the minimum row gap $M_{ii}-\max_{j\ne i}M_{ij}$ diverges.

For normalized embeddings, $M_{ii},M_{ij}\in[-1/\tau,1/\tau]$, hence $M_{ij}-M_{ii}\ge-2/\tau$. Every one of the $B-1$ negative terms is therefore at least $e^{-2/\tau}$, giving the displayed positive lower bound. The feasible set $(S^{d-1})^{2B}$ is compact and the symmetric loss is continuous, so the extreme-value theorem supplies an attained minimum. Finally,
$$
\frac{\partial\ell_i}{\partial M_{ii}}=p(i\mid i)-1<0,
\qquad
\frac{\partial\ell_i}{\partial M_{ij}}=p(j\mid i)>0\quad(j\ne i),
$$
which proves the directional alignment/separation pressure. Because changing one unit vector moves several entries of $\mathbf M$ at once, those coordinatewise preferences do not characterize the coupled global minimizer. The factor $1/\tau$ only converts a cosine gap $\delta$ into logit gap $\delta/\tau$; smaller $\tau$ makes a fixed feasible gap more decisive. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="What contrastive geometry does — and does not — imply" id="rem-tf10-separation-geometry">
The proved geometric pressure is **instance-level**: matched views are favored over the other examples in the batch. It does not imply that semantic classes form linearly separable clusters. Indeed, an in-batch negative can share the anchor's class, and the objective still treats that particular pair as mismatched. Empirically, suitable augmentations, data, and optimization often produce features on which linear heads and distance-based OOD scores work well, but that class geometry is a measured representation-learning outcome rather than a consequence of <XRef id="prop-tf10-contrastive-alignment" />. The score definitions below therefore apply to any embedding; whether a contrastively trained embedding makes them discriminative must be validated on held-out data.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Contrastive alignment is the canonical cross-modal objective" id="rem-tf10-crossmodal-canonical">
Take the two views of <XRef id="def-tf10-contrastive" /> to be **two modalities**: $\mathbf U$ the pooled embeddings of images (a Vision Transformer, <XRef id="rem-tf7-vit" />) and $\mathbf V$ the pooled embeddings of their captions (a text encoder), each formed through the embedding interface (<XRef id="def-tf1-embedding-interface" />) and an encoder, each pooled to $\R^{d}$ by <XRef id="def-tf10-pooled-rep" />. Then the symmetric InfoNCE of <XRef id="prop-tf10-contrastive-ce" /> is **exactly** the CLIP image–text objective <Cite key="radford2021clip" />. Its alignment/separation gradients (<XRef id="prop-tf10-contrastive-alignment" />) train a **shared embedding space** in which matched image–caption pairs are favored over mismatches; the familiar picture of a dog neighboring the string "a dog" is the empirical geometry that successful optimization produces, not a theorem about every minimizer. This geometry directly supports zero-shot classification (<XRef id="def-tf18-zeroshot" />): score an image against text-prompt embeddings in the same retrieval game. Generative vision-language models take a separate route: a connector (<XRef id="def-tf15-connector" />) is learned to map vision features into a language model's space; contrastive pretraining may supply the vision tower, but its objective does not itself supply that projector. The contrastive head, the autoregressive head (<XRef id="def-tf9-autoregressive-lm" />), and cross-attention (<XRef id="def-tf7-cross-attention" />) are distinct interfaces around the modality-agnostic core; this chapter's objective is the cross-modal contrastive one.
</Theorem>

## Embedding geometry for out-of-distribution detection

The final picture leaves classification entirely. Suppose we have an embedding $\mathbf r = \operatorname{pool}(Q(\mathbf X))$ and a sample of *inlier* (in-distribution) embeddings. If the representation places anomalies in regions that are sparse under the inlier distribution, distance or fitted-density geometry can expose them; neither contrastive training nor encoder architecture guarantees that separation. Conditional on a chosen embedding, detection is a one-dimensional thresholding problem on a **score** that measures outlyingness, and its usefulness is an empirical property of the resulting score distributions.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Embedding-geometry OOD score" id="def-tf10-ood-score">
Let $\{\mathbf r^{(1)},\dots,\mathbf r^{(m)}\}\subset\R^{d}$ be inlier embeddings with mean $\boldsymbol\mu$ and (full-rank) covariance $\boldsymbol\Sigma$. An **out-of-distribution score** $s:\R^{d}\to\R$ assigns each embedding $\mathbf r$ a value increasing in its outlyingness; an input is flagged anomalous when $s(\mathbf r)$ exceeds a threshold. Three standard score functions are
$$
s_{\textsf{NN}}(\mathbf r) = \min_{k}\norm{\mathbf r - \mathbf r^{(k)}}
\quad(\text{nearest-inlier distance}),
\qquad
s_{\textsf{M}}(\mathbf r) = \big((\mathbf r-\boldsymbol\mu)\,\boldsymbol\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf r-\boldsymbol\mu)^{\top}\big)^{1/2}
\quad(\text{Mahalanobis distance}),
$$
$$
s_{\textsf{nll}}(\mathbf r) = -\log \hat p(\mathbf r)
\quad(\text{negative log-density under a model } \hat p \text{ fit to the inliers}).
$$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Mahalanobis = whitened distance">$s_{\textsf{M}}$ is the Euclidean distance after whitening by $\boldsymbol\Sigma^{-1/2}$: it is the Euclidean score in coordinates where the inlier cloud is isotropic, so it accounts for the cluster's shape, not just its center.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="The three scores are one idea" id="rem-tf10-scores-unified">
All three scores answer "how unlike the inliers is this point?" and are monotone reparametrizations of one another under matching modeling assumptions. If the inliers are fit by a single Gaussian $\mathcal N(\boldsymbol\mu,\boldsymbol\Sigma)$, then the negative log-density is $s_{\textsf{nll}}(\mathbf r) = \tfrac12 s_{\textsf{M}}(\mathbf r)^2 + \text{const}$, so thresholding $s_{\textsf{nll}}$ and thresholding $s_{\textsf{M}}$ produce identical decisions. The nearest-inlier score $s_{\textsf{NN}}$ is the nonparametric counterpart: it makes no distributional assumption and reduces to the same "distance from the support" intuition when the support is a single cluster. The contrastive geometry of <XRef id="rem-tf10-separation-geometry" /> is what makes any of them work — it is what concentrates the inliers so that "far from them" is meaningful.
</Theorem>

A score function must be judged independently of any particular threshold, because the deployment threshold trades false alarms against misses and is chosen later. The threshold-free summary is the probability that the score ranks a true anomaly above a true inlier — and this probability has a closed name.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="AUROC as a ranking probability" id="def-tf10-auroc">
Let $S^{+} = s(\mathbf r)$ for a random anomaly ("positive") and $S^{-} = s(\mathbf r)$ for an independent random inlier ("negative"). The **AUROC** (area under the receiver-operating-characteristic curve) of the score $s$ is *defined here* as the ranking probability<Sidenote>That this ranking probability equals the literal area under the ROC curve is the Hanley–McNeil identity <Cite key="hanley1982meaning" />; we only ever use the ranking form.</Sidenote>
$$
\operatorname{AUROC}(s) \defeq \Pr[\,S^{+} > S^{-}\,] + \tfrac12\Pr[\,S^{+} = S^{-}\,].
$$
It lies in $[0,1]$; $\operatorname{AUROC} = 1$ means the score ranks every anomaly above every inlier, and $\operatorname{AUROC} = \tfrac12$ is chance.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="AUROC equals the rank statistic" id="prop-tf10-auroc-rank">
For a finite test set of $n_{+}$ anomalies with scores $\{a_p\}$ and $n_{-}$ inliers with scores $\{b_q\}$, the empirical AUROC equals the normalized count of correctly ordered cross pairs,
$$
\widehat{\operatorname{AUROC}}
= \frac{1}{n_{+}n_{-}}\sum_{p=1}^{n_{+}}\sum_{q=1}^{n_{-}}
\Big(\mathbf 1\{a_p > b_q\} + \tfrac12\,\mathbf 1\{a_p = b_q\}\Big),
$$
the Mann–Whitney statistic. It is invariant under any strictly increasing reparametrization of the score and depends on no threshold.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Estimate the probabilities in <XRef id="def-tf10-auroc" /> by their empirical frequencies over the $n_{+}n_{-}$ independent cross pairs $(a_p,b_q)$. The event $S^{+}>S^{-}$ has empirical frequency $\frac{1}{n_+n_-}\sum_{p,q}\mathbf 1\{a_p>b_q\}$, and the tie event contributes $\frac{1}{n_+n_-}\sum_{p,q}\mathbf 1\{a_p=b_q\}$ with weight $\tfrac12$ exactly as in the definition; summing gives the displayed statistic. For the invariance claim, let $\phi$ be strictly increasing. Then $a_p>b_q \iff \phi(a_p)>\phi(b_q)$ and $a_p=b_q\iff\phi(a_p)=\phi(b_q)$, so every indicator in the double sum is unchanged and so is $\widehat{\operatorname{AUROC}}$. No comparison references a cutoff, so the statistic is threshold-free. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Threshold-free ranking is not a deployment guarantee" id="rem-tf10-auroc-caveat">
AUROC measures only the *ordering* a score induces, averaged over all thresholds; a deployed detector commits to one threshold and therefore to a specific false-positive/false-negative tradeoff, which a single ranking number does not pin down. Two further gaps are routine in detection reports. An average AUROC over evaluation slices can hide a catastrophic worst slice — the relevant quantity for a deployment is often $\min$ over slices, not the mean. And a high AUROC on a held-out distribution close to training is weaker evidence than the same number under a genuine distribution shift (a new content domain, or anomalies from a generator unseen in training). The honest reading of a strong benchmark AUROC is "the score ranks well under the tested shifts," not "the detector is robust."
</Theorem>

## Exercises

Attempt each before expanding its solution.

**Exercise 10.1 (Prove).** Show that mean pooling $\mathbf r_{\textsf{mean}} = \tfrac1n\sum_i \mathbf h_i$ is the action of a row-stochastic matrix on $\mathbf H$, and conclude $\mathbf r_{\textsf{mean}}$ lies in the convex hull of the contextual states $\{\mathbf h_i\}$. In what sense does the prepended designated-position rule of <XRef id="def-tf10-pooled-rep" /> escape confinement — being itself one of the states, $\mathbf r_{\textsf{CLS}}$ trivially lies in the full hull, so what hull need it *not* lie in?

<SolutionBox>

Mean pooling is $\mathbf r_{\textsf{mean}} = \mathbf a\mathbf H$ with $\mathbf a = \tfrac1n\mathbf 1^\top$, a $1\times n$ row-stochastic vector, so $\mathbf r_{\textsf{mean}} = \sum_j \tfrac1n\mathbf h_j$ lies in the convex hull of the token states (<XRef id="prop-row-stochastic-convex" />). The prepended designated-position representation $\mathbf r_{\textsf{CLS}}$ need not lie in the hull of the *averaged token states* $\{\mathbf h_j\}_{j\ge 2}$, because $\mathbf h_{\textsf{CLS}}$ is a learned input vector, not one of the $\mathbf h_j$ averaged: the encoder writes into that slot through attention and the residual path, so it need not be any convex mixture of the token states. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 10.2 (Derive).** Starting from <XRef id="prop-tf10-contrastive-ce" />, write the gradient of the per-row loss $-\log p(i\mid i)$ with respect to the logit row $\mathbf M_{i,:}$ using the softmax Jacobian (<XRef id="prop-softmax-jacobian" />). Show the matched logit $M_{ii}$ is pushed up and each mismatched $M_{ij}$ is pushed down in proportion to $p(j\mid i)$, and explain why this makes hard negatives dominate the update.

<SolutionBox>

With $p_j = \softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j$ and $L_i = -\log p_i$, the loss is the cross-entropy of $\mathbf e_i$ against $\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})$, so by the softmax Jacobian (<XRef id="prop-softmax-jacobian" />), $\partial L_i/\partial M_{ij} = p_j - \delta_{ij}$: the matched logit $M_{ii}$ has gradient $p_i - 1 < 0$ (descent raises it), each mismatched $M_{ij}$ has gradient $p_j > 0$ (descent lowers it). A hard negative — large current similarity, hence large $p_j$ — receives the largest downward push, so the update is dominated by the negatives the model currently confuses with the true partner. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 10.3 (Prove).** Using translation invariance of the softmax (<XRef id="prop-softmax-translation-inv" />), show that adding any constant $c$ to an entire row of the similarity matrix $\mathbf M$ leaves $\mathcal L_{\textsf{row}}$ unchanged, and conclude that the *row* loss depends on the similarities only through the row gaps $M_{ii} - M_{ij}$. Why does the symmetric $\mathcal L_{\textsf{con}}$ additionally depend on the column gaps $M_{jj} - M_{ij}$?

<SolutionBox>

Adding $c$ to row $i$ replaces $\mathbf M_{i,:}$ by $\mathbf M_{i,:} + c\mathbf 1$; by translation invariance (<XRef id="prop-softmax-translation-inv" />) the softmax row is unchanged, so $p(i\mid i)$ and $\mathcal L_{\textsf{row}}$ are unchanged. Equivalently $p(i\mid i) = \big(1 + \sum_{j\ne i} e^{M_{ij}-M_{ii}}\big)^{-1}$ depends on the logits only through the row gaps $M_{ii}-M_{ij}$. The symmetric $\mathcal L_{\textsf{con}}$ also averages $\mathcal L_{\textsf{col}}$, whose column softmaxes are *not* invariant to a row shift; by the transposed argument it contributes the column gaps $M_{jj}-M_{ij}$. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 10.4 (Compute).** Inliers are fit by $\mathcal N(\boldsymbol\mu,\boldsymbol\Sigma)$. Show $s_{\textsf{nll}}(\mathbf r) = \tfrac12 s_{\textsf{M}}(\mathbf r)^2 + \text{const}$, and conclude that thresholding the negative log-density and thresholding the Mahalanobis distance give identical decisions (hence identical AUROC by <XRef id="prop-tf10-auroc-rank" />).

<SolutionBox>

For $\mathbf r\sim\mathcal N(\boldsymbol\mu,\boldsymbol\Sigma)$, $-\log\hat p(\mathbf r) = \tfrac12(\mathbf r-\boldsymbol\mu)\boldsymbol\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf r-\boldsymbol\mu)^\top + \tfrac{d}{2}\log(2\pi) + \tfrac12\log|\boldsymbol\Sigma| = \tfrac12 s_{\textsf{M}}(\mathbf r)^2 + \text{const}$, where $s_{\textsf{M}}^2$ is the squared Mahalanobis distance. Since $u\mapsto\tfrac12 u^2 + \text{const}$ is strictly increasing on $u\ge0$, the neg-log-density is a strictly increasing reparametrization of $s_{\textsf{M}}$, so thresholding either gives identical accept/reject sets and identical AUROC. $\qquad\blacksquare$

**Exercise 10.5 (Construct).** Exhibit scores for $4$ anomalies and $4$ inliers with empirical $\widehat{\operatorname{AUROC}} = 1$, then shift one inlier's score so that $\widehat{\operatorname{AUROC}} = \tfrac34$ while the *mean* anomaly score still exceeds the mean inlier score. Explain what this shows about reporting a mean score gap versus AUROC.

<SolutionBox>

Take 4 anomaly scores $\{5,6,7,8\}$ (mean $6.5$) and 4 inlier scores $\{1,2,3,4\}$ (mean $2.5$): every one of the $16$ cross-pairs has anomaly $>$ inlier, so $\widehat{\operatorname{AUROC}} = 16/16 = 1$, with mean gap $+4$. Now shift one inlier's score, $4 \to 12$: inliers $\{1,2,3,12\}$ (mean $4.5$). The mean gap stays positive ($6.5 - 4.5 = +2.0$), but ranking degrades: the inliers at $1,2,3$ lose to all $4$ anomalies ($12$ correctly-ranked pairs) while the shifted inlier at $12$ beats all $4$ anomalies ($4$ misranked pairs), so $\widehat{\operatorname{AUROC}} = 12/16 = 0.75 < 1$. A single badly-placed inlier flips many pairwise comparisons while barely moving the mean — the mean gap is a first-moment summary, AUROC is the pairwise ranking. Report AUROC (or a worst-slice metric), not a mean-score difference. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
