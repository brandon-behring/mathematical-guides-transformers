**Where we are.** Recurrent encoder–decoders introduced content addressing (<XRef id="def-additive-attention" />) as a way to read whichever source states mattered at the current step. The transformer keeps that score–normalize–average operation and removes the recurrent controller around it. This chapter builds the resulting attention map from three pieces: dot products compare queries with keys, row-wise softmax turns those scores into weights, and the weights average value rows. We then add the causal mask needed for generation and show why scaling each dot product by the square root of its width prevents dimension alone from driving softmax toward saturation.

## Notation

Vector conventions, the standing dimensions ($n$, $d$, $d_k$), $\defeq$, the inner product, and the softmax are all fixed in the Notation chapter; we invoke them by name. This chapter introduces the attention operator $\operatorname{Attn}(\mathbf Q,\mathbf K,\mathbf V)$, the causal mask $\mathbf M$, and the per-head value dimension $d_v$.

## Softmax, recalled

Softmax turns a vector of scores into a probability distribution: larger scores get exponentially more weight, and the weights sum to one. Its definition and its two working properties are fixed once in the Notation chapter — the definition (<XRef id="def-softmax" />), translation invariance (<XRef id="prop-softmax-translation-inv" />), and the Jacobian (<XRef id="prop-softmax-jacobian" />). Everything this chapter proves rides on the first two: the definition makes each attention weight row a probability vector, and translation invariance is what makes the numerically stable max-subtracted implementation exact.

## Scaled dot-product attention

Structurally, attention is **Nadaraya–Watson kernel regression with a learned kernel**: each output is a kernel-weighted average of the value vectors, where the weight value $j$ receives is proportional to $\exp(\inner{\mathbf q_i}{\mathbf k_j}/\sqrt{d_k})$. The correspondence is exact — *kernel weights* ↦ *a softmax row*, *kernel-regression estimate* ↦ *the attention output* — and it breaks in exactly one place: the kernel is **learned**, so it need not be symmetric or positive-definite, and the classical kernel-smoothing guarantees do not transfer. We make the construction precise.

<Theorem kind="remark" name="The addressing lineage" id="rem-addressing-lineage">
Score–normalize–average is older than the transformer: additive attention (<XRef id="def-additive-attention" />) already computed $\softmax$ weights over content and took the convex combination — inside a recurrent encoder–decoder, with the score $\mathbf v_a^\top\tanh(\mathbf W_a\mathbf s + \mathbf U_a\mathbf h)$ computed by a small network. Scaled dot-product attention makes two substitutions: the additive score becomes an inner product (one matmul, no extra nonlinearity), and the recurrent controller that manufactured queries and keys is deleted — learned projections of the sequence itself produce them. The kernel-regression reading above covers both: they are the same estimator under different kernels.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Scaled dot-product attention" id="def-attention">
Given queries $\mathbf Q\in\R^{n\times d_k}$, keys $\mathbf K\in\R^{n\times d_k}$, and values $\mathbf V\in\R^{n\times d_v}$,
$$
\operatorname{Attn}(\mathbf Q,\mathbf K,\mathbf V) \defeq \softmax\!\Big(\tfrac{\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\Big)\,\mathbf V,
$$
with softmax applied row-wise to the $n\times n$ logit matrix.
</Theorem>

### A complete two-token trace

Take two-dimensional queries and keys with
$$
\mathbf Q=\mathbf K=
\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf V=
\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0&4\end{pmatrix},
\qquad d_k=2.
$$
The scaled logit matrix is
$$
\mathbf L=\frac{\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top}{\sqrt2}
=\begin{pmatrix}1/\sqrt2&0\\0&1/\sqrt2\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Set $a\defeq e^{1/\sqrt2}$, $\alpha\defeq a/(a+1)\approx0.6698$, and $\beta\defeq1/(a+1)\approx0.3302$. Applying softmax row by row gives
$$
\mathbf A=\softmax(\mathbf L)
=\begin{pmatrix}\alpha&\beta\\\beta&\alpha\end{pmatrix}
\approx
\begin{pmatrix}0.6698&0.3302\\0.3302&0.6698\end{pmatrix}.
$$
The attention output is therefore
$$
\mathbf A\mathbf V
=\begin{pmatrix}2\alpha&4\beta\\2\beta&4\alpha\end{pmatrix}
\approx
\begin{pmatrix}1.3395&1.3210\\0.6605&2.6790\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Each row is visibly a weighted average of the two value rows. With a causal mask, the first query may read only the first key while the second may still read both:
$$
\mathbf A_{\mathrm{causal}}
=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\\beta&\alpha\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf A_{\mathrm{causal}}\mathbf V
=\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\2\beta&4\alpha\end{pmatrix}
\approx
\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0.6605&2.6790\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Adding the same constant to every entry of either logit row would leave that row's weights unchanged by softmax translation invariance (<XRef id="prop-softmax-translation-inv" />).

Because each softmax row is nonnegative and sums to one (<XRef id="def-softmax" />), the weight matrix is row-stochastic, so output row $i$ is a **convex combination** of the value rows (<XRef id="prop-row-stochastic-convex" />) — the kernel-regression reading made literal.

For language modeling, attention must additionally respect time: position $i$ may consult only positions $j \le i$. This is imposed not by changing the operator but by adding a fixed mask to the logits before the softmax.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Causal (masked) attention" id="def-causal-mask">
For $C>0$, define the finite mask $\mathbf M^{(C)}\in\R^{n\times n}$ by
$$
M^{(C)}_{ij} \defeq \begin{cases} 0 & j \le i,\\ -C & j > i.\end{cases}
$$
For finite $\mathbf Q,\mathbf K,\mathbf V$, **causal attention** is the entrywise limit
$$
\operatorname{Attn}^{\text{causal}}(\mathbf Q,\mathbf K,\mathbf V)
\defeq \lim_{C\to\infty}
\softmax\!\Big(\tfrac{\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}} + \mathbf M^{(C)}\Big)\,\mathbf V.
$$
Equivalently, use the extended-real mask $\mathbf M\in(\R\cup\{-\infty\})^{n\times n}$ with entries $0$ for $j\le i$ and $-\infty$ for $j>i$, interpreting $e^{-\infty}=0$. Every row has at least the finite diagonal logit $j=i$, so its limiting denominator is positive and the formula is well-defined. Each resulting row remains a probability distribution, now supported on $\{j:j\le i\}$: row $i$ attends only to positions up to $i$.
</Theorem>

## Why scale the dot products before softmax?

Each logit $\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}$ sums $d_k$ independent product terms, so its spread grows with $d_k$ and pushes softmax toward saturation. The scaling is chosen to *exactly* cancel that growth.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">
Before reading: $\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}=\sum_m q_m k_m$ adds $d_k$ independent zero-mean unit-variance terms. What should its variance scale like in $d_k$?
</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="Variance preservation" id="thm-variance-preservation">
Let the $2d_k$ scalar components $q_1,\dots,q_{d_k},k_1,\dots,k_{d_k}$ be **mutually independent** with $\E[q_m]=\E[k_m]=0$ and $\Var(q_m)=\Var(k_m)=1$. Then $\E[\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}]=0$, $\Var(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k})=d_k$, and $\Var\!\big(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}/\sqrt{d_k}\big)=1$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Three steps, each naming the rule it uses.

**Mean.** By linearity and independence,
$$
\E\Big[\textstyle\sum_{m} q_m k_m\Big] = \sum_m \E[q_m]\,\E[k_m] = 0
\qquad\text{(linearity; } q_m\perp k_m\text{)}.
$$

**Variance.** Since the mean is $0$, $\Var(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}) = \E[(\sum_m q_m k_m)^2]$. For $m\neq n$ mutual independence factorizes the cross term into $\E[q_m]\E[k_m]\E[q_n]\E[k_n]=0$ — this is where independence *across* indices is used, not just $q_m \perp k_m$ — leaving
$$
\sum_m \E[q_m^2]\,\E[k_m^2] = \sum_{m=1}^{d_k} 1\cdot 1 = d_k
\qquad\text{(independence; } \Var=\E[\,\cdot\,^2]\text{ at mean }0).
$$

**Scaling.** $\Var$ is $2$-homogeneous, so dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ divides the variance by $d_k$:
$$
\Var\!\Big(\tfrac{\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\Big) = \tfrac{1}{d_k}\,\Var(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}) = \tfrac{d_k}{d_k} = 1. \qquad\blacksquare
$$
</Theorem>

So $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ is the unique positive scale that holds logit variance at $1$ as $d_k$ grows. It removes this dimension-driven pressure toward softmax saturation; it does not guarantee that learned logits can never saturate <Cite key="vaswani2017attention" />.

## Exercises

Attempt each before expanding its solution.

**Exercise 4.1 (Prove).** The weight matrix $\softmax(\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top/\sqrt{d_k})$ is row-stochastic (<XRef id="def-row-stochastic" />); conclude each attention output is a convex combination of the value rows.

<SolutionBox exercise="4.1">

Fix a query row $i$ and write the logits $\ell_{ij} = \inner{\mathbf q_i}{\mathbf k_j}/\sqrt{d_k}$. The attention weight $w_{ij} = e^{\ell_{ij}}/\sum_{j'} e^{\ell_{ij'}}$ is **positive** (an exponential over a positive sum) and the row **sums to one** ($\sum_j w_{ij} = \sum_j e^{\ell_{ij}} / \sum_{j'} e^{\ell_{ij'}} = 1$); so every row is a probability vector, i.e. the weight matrix is row-stochastic. The output row is $\sum_j w_{ij}\,\mathbf v_j$ with $w_{ij}\ge 0$ and $\sum_j w_{ij}=1$, i.e. a **convex combination** of the value rows. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 4.2 (Derive).** Without the $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling, give $\Var(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k})$. State a sufficient logit-gap condition under which a softmax row approaches one-hot as $d_k\to\infty$, relate it to gradient saturation, and contrast the case of tied maxima.

<SolutionBox exercise="4.2">

By the variance-preservation theorem (unscaled case), $\Var(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}) = d_k$. Variance growth alone does **not** deterministically imply a one-hot row. For a sequence of logit rows $\boldsymbol\ell^{(d_k)}$, a sufficient condition is a unique maximizing index $i^\ast$ whose gaps diverge:
$$
\ell^{(d_k)}_{i^\ast}-\ell^{(d_k)}_j\longrightarrow+\infty
\qquad\text{for every }j\ne i^\ast.
$$
Dividing numerator and denominator by $e^{\ell_{i^\ast}}$ then gives $\softmax(\boldsymbol\ell^{(d_k)})\to\mathbf e_{i^\ast}$. At that limit the Jacobian $\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf p)-\mathbf p\mathbf p^\top$ tends to $\mathbf0$, so gradients saturate (<XRef id="prop-softmax-jacobian" />). If instead $r\ge2$ entries remain tied for the maximum while their gaps to all other entries diverge, the limit is uniform on that tied set, not one-hot; the Jacobian retains a nonzero rank-$(r-1)$ block within the tied face. Scaling by $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ holds the variance at $1$ and removes dimension-driven spread, but it does not by itself rule out either learned saturation or ties. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 4.3 (Compute).** Let $d_k=1$ and
$$
\mathbf Q=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf K=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf V=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&3\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Compute the dense attention logits, row-wise softmax weights, and output. Then apply the causal mask and compute the masked output.

<SolutionBox exercise="4.3">

Because $d_k=1$, scaling changes nothing. The dense logit matrix is
$$
\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top
=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\0&0\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Set $p\defeq1/(1+e)\approx0.2689$ and $q\defeq e/(1+e)\approx0.7311$. Row-wise softmax gives
$$
\mathbf A
=\begin{pmatrix}p&q\\1/2&1/2\end{pmatrix}
\approx
\begin{pmatrix}0.2689&0.7311\\0.5&0.5\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Multiplying by the values,
$$
\mathbf A\mathbf V
=\begin{pmatrix}p&3q\\1/2&3/2\end{pmatrix}
\approx
\begin{pmatrix}0.2689&2.1932\\0.5&1.5\end{pmatrix}.
$$
The causal mask deletes the first row's future logit but leaves the second row unchanged, so
$$
\mathbf A_{\mathrm{causal}}
=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\1/2&1/2\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf A_{\mathrm{causal}}\mathbf V
=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\1/2&3/2\end{pmatrix}.
$$
This traces the complete score–normalize–average computation in both visibility regimes. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
