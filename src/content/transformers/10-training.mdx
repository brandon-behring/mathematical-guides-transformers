# Training a Transformer

**Where we are.** Earlier chapters built the transformer as a static map from a token sequence to a distribution over the next token. This chapter makes that map *learn*: we fix the scalar objective it descends — the cross-entropy / negative-log-likelihood loss — show it is exactly KL divergence to the data up to a parameter-free constant, derive the single subtraction gradient ($\mathbf q$ minus the target) that drives every update, and then treat the four adjustments that make the descent behave — teacher forcing (and the exposure bias it incurs), label smoothing (and its effect on the target distribution), the warmup / inverse-square-root learning-rate schedule, and weight initialization. We write $p_\theta$ for the model distribution with parameters $\theta$, $p_{\text{data}}$ for the data distribution, $T$ for sequence length (the same quantity as the standing $n$; we write $T$ where the literature's $1/T$ averaging is idiomatic), and $\mathbf W_{\text{out}}\in\R^{d\times\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}$ for the output projection from hidden states to vocabulary logits; the softmax, cross-entropy $H(\mathbf p,\mathbf q)$, and KL divergence $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbf p\,\|\,\mathbf q)$ are all fixed in the Notation chapter.

## Autoregressive language modeling

A language model assigns a probability to a whole sequence, but it never has to compute one directly: the chain rule of probability factors any joint distribution into a product of next-token conditionals, and an autoregressive model parameterizes exactly those conditionals. The factorization is exact, not an approximation — there is no independence assumption hidden in it. Nor is it the transformer's: the same chain rule first served the recurrent encoder–decoder (<XRef id="def-seq2seq" />), and it is architecture-blind.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Autoregressive language model" id="def-autoregressive-lm">
An **autoregressive language model**<Sidenote>The decoder-only formulation underlying the GPT family <Cite key="radford2019language" />.</Sidenote> factorizes the joint probability of a token sequence $\mathbf x = (x_1,\dots,x_T)$ into a product of conditionals,
$$
p_\theta(\mathbf x) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}), \qquad x_{<t} \defeq (x_1,\dots,x_{t-1}),
$$
where each conditional is produced by a transformer with parameters $\theta$. At position $t$ the model maps its hidden state $\mathbf h_t\in\R^{d}$ through the output projection and a softmax:
$$
p_\theta(x_t = k \mid x_{<t}) = \softmax(\mathbf h_t \mathbf W_{\text{out}})_k, \qquad \mathbf W_{\text{out}}\in\R^{d\times\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}.
$$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Chain rule">Predict each token from all previous tokens. The factorization is exact — no independence assumption is introduced.</MarginNote>

## The cross-entropy loss

Training minimizes one scalar per sequence: the average negative log-probability the model assigns to the tokens that actually occurred. This is the cross-entropy between a one-hot target and the model's predicted distribution at each position, averaged over the sequence — the maximum-likelihood objective written per token.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Cross-entropy loss for language modeling" id="def-ce-loss">
For a training sequence $\mathbf x = (x_1,\dots,x_T)$, the **cross-entropy loss** is the average negative log-likelihood
$$
\mathcal L(\theta) \defeq -\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}).
$$
At each position $t$ this is the cross-entropy between the one-hot target $\mathbf e_{x_t}$ and the model's prediction $\mathbf q_t = \softmax(\mathbf h_t \mathbf W_{\text{out}})$, since $H(\mathbf e_{x_t}, \mathbf q_t) = -\log q_{t,x_t}$.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="NLL objective">$\mathcal L = -\frac{1}{T}\sum_t \log p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t})$: the data's negative log-likelihood under the model.</MarginNote>

The objective is not arbitrary. The cross-entropy decomposition proved in the Notation chapter splits any cross-entropy into the target's own entropy plus the KL divergence to the model, and only the second piece depends on $\theta$. Applied across the data distribution, this fixes what training optimizes.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Cross-entropy loss minimizes KL divergence" id="prop-ce-kl-training">
Over the data distribution $p_{\text{data}}$, the expected loss satisfies
$$
\E_{\mathbf x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\mathcal L(\theta)] = \tfrac{1}{T}\Big(H(p_{\text{data}}) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \,\|\, p_\theta)\Big),
$$
where $H$ and $D_{\mathrm{KL}}$ are taken over length-$T$ sequences (the loss carries a $1/T$ average). Since $H(p_{\text{data}})$ does not depend on $\theta$, minimizing the cross-entropy loss over the parameters is equivalent to minimizing $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \,\|\, p_\theta)$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
This is the sequence-level instance of the cross-entropy decomposition (<XRef id="prop-ce-kl" />). Each per-token loss $-\log p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t})$ is the cross-entropy $H(\mathbf e_{x_t}, \mathbf q_t)$ (<XRef id="def-ce-loss" />). Taking the expectation over $p_{\text{data}}$, summing over positions (the chain rule factorizes sequence-level cross-entropy into the per-position conditionals), and carrying the loss's $1/T$ average, the decomposition gives
$$
\E[\mathcal L] = \tfrac{1}{T}\Big(H(p_{\text{data}}) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \,\|\, p_\theta)\Big),
$$
in which the entropy term is a property of the data alone (<XRef id="prop-ce-kl" />). Its gradient in $\theta$ is therefore zero, so $\nabla_\theta \E[\mathcal L] = \nabla_\theta D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}\,\|\,p_\theta)$: the two objectives share the same minimizer. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

The descent is driven entirely by one gradient, and it has a strikingly simple closed form at the logit level. Because the loss factors through softmax, the softmax Jacobian from the Notation chapter does the work, and a fortunate cancellation collapses the whole chain to a single subtraction.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before reading the proof: the loss is $-\log q_{x_t}$, a function of one coordinate of $\mathbf q$. The softmax Jacobian (Notation chapter) has the form $q_i(\delta_{ij}-q_j)$. Which factor do you expect to cancel?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Gradient of cross-entropy with respect to logits" id="prop-ce-logit-gradient">
Let $\mathbf z_t \defeq \mathbf h_t \mathbf W_{\text{out}}$ and $\mathbf q_t = \softmax(\mathbf z_t)$. For a one-hot target $\mathbf e_{x_t}$, the gradient of the per-token cross-entropy with respect to the logits is
$$
\frac{\partial H(\mathbf e_{x_t}, \mathbf q_t)}{\partial z_{t,k}} = q_{t,k} - [\mathbf e_{x_t}]_k, \qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad \nabla_{\mathbf z_t} \mathcal L_t = \mathbf q_t - \mathbf e_{x_t}.
$$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Write $\mathcal L_t = -\log q_{t,x_t}$ and differentiate, each step naming its rule.

**Chain rule into the softmax.** Only the $x_t$-th coordinate of $\mathbf q_t$ enters $\mathcal L_t$, so by the chain rule
$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal L_t}{\partial z_{t,k}} = -\frac{1}{q_{t,x_t}}\,\frac{\partial q_{t,x_t}}{\partial z_{t,k}}.
$$

**Softmax Jacobian.** The Jacobian entry from the Notation chapter (<XRef id="prop-softmax-jacobian" />) is $\partial q_{t,i}/\partial z_{t,k} = q_{t,i}(\delta_{ik} - q_{t,k})$. With $i = x_t$,
$$
\frac{\partial q_{t,x_t}}{\partial z_{t,k}} = q_{t,x_t}\big([\mathbf e_{x_t}]_k - q_{t,k}\big),
$$
using $\delta_{x_t k} = [\mathbf e_{x_t}]_k$.

**Cancellation.** Substituting, the factor $q_{t,x_t}$ cancels:
$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal L_t}{\partial z_{t,k}} = -\frac{1}{q_{t,x_t}}\,q_{t,x_t}\big([\mathbf e_{x_t}]_k - q_{t,k}\big) = q_{t,k} - [\mathbf e_{x_t}]_k. \qquad\blacksquare
$$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="The training signal">$\nabla_{\mathbf z}\mathcal L = \mathbf q - \mathbf y$: push the correct logit up by $1-q_{x_t}$, push each wrong logit down by its own probability.</MarginNote>

The form $\mathbf q_t - \mathbf e_{x_t}$ is distribution matching read off directly: the update pushes the correct-class logit up by an amount proportional to $1 - q_{t,x_t}$ and pushes every incorrect logit down in proportion to the probability it currently holds. The gradient vanishes exactly when $\mathbf q_t = \mathbf e_{x_t}$, i.e. when the model already places all mass on the right token. This same expression returns below: replacing the one-hot target $\mathbf e_{x_t}$ by any target distribution $\mathbf y$ leaves the gradient $\mathbf q_t - \mathbf y$ — the identical mechanism with a different target.

Nothing in the objective above named what the sequence *is*. Conditioning on an image is the same loss with one extra symbol in the conditioning set.

<Theorem kind="example" name="Image captioning is conditional autoregressive modeling" id="ex-captioning">
A captioning model factorizes the caption $\mathbf y = (y_1,\dots,y_{T})$ conditioned on image features $\mathbf c = \operatorname{Enc}_{\text{img}}(\mathbf I)$ — a Vision Transformer output, or projected patch tokens prepended to the sequence (<XRef id="rem-multimodal-crossattn" />, <XRef id="rem-unified-sequence" />):
$$
p_\theta(\mathbf y \mid \mathbf I) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_\theta(y_t \mid y_{<t},\,\mathbf c).
$$
The loss is the cross-entropy of <XRef id="def-ce-loss" /> verbatim, with the conditioning set enlarged from $y_{<t}$ to $(y_{<t},\mathbf c)$:
$$
\mathcal L(\theta) = -\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(y_t \mid y_{<t},\,\mathbf c).
$$
The KL identity (<XRef id="prop-ce-kl-training" />), the logit gradient $\mathbf q_t - \mathbf e_{y_t}$ (<XRef id="prop-ce-logit-gradient" />), teacher forcing (<XRef id="def-teacher-forcing" />), and label smoothing (<XRef id="prop-label-smoothing-decomposition" />) all transfer with no change — only the conditioning variable is new. The image reaches the model through the embedding interface (prepended visual tokens) or through cross-attention (<XRef id="rem-multimodal-crossattn" />), both already defined. Read in either direction, this same conditional factorization is what lets one unified model both caption images and generate them (<XRef id="thm-any-to-any" />).<Sidenote>Conditional captioning as visual instruction tuning <Cite key="liu2023llava" />; cross-attended conditioning <Cite key="alayrac2022flamingo" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Same loss, bigger context">Captioning is next-token cross-entropy with $\mathbf c$ added to the conditioning set; the $\mathbf q - \mathbf e_{y_t}$ logit gradient is unchanged.</MarginNote>

## Teacher forcing

The loss of <XRef id="def-ce-loss" /> conditions every position on the ground-truth prefix $x_{<t}$ — by construction, not as an approximation. The contrast is with *generation*, where each conditional is fed the model's own earlier outputs one step at a time. Conditioning training on the ground truth is called **teacher forcing**, and it is what lets all $T$ positions be scored in one parallel pass.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Teacher forcing" id="def-teacher-forcing">
During training, **teacher forcing** supplies the ground-truth token $x_t$ as the input at position $t+1$, rather than the model's own prediction $\hat x_t = \arg\max_k p_\theta(k\mid x_{<t})$. Because the inputs no longer depend on the outputs, all $T$ predictions are computed in one forward pass with causal masking.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Why it parallelizes">Conditioning on gold tokens makes every position's input known in advance, so the $T$ losses share one masked forward pass.</MarginNote>

<MarginNote variant="cross-ref" label="Two licenses">One pass yielding all $T$ conditionals is the causality theorem <XRef id="thm-one-pass" /> — every causal mixer has it; that the pass is also *fast* is the $\Theta(1)$-depth column of <XRef id="prop-sequential-depth" />, which recurrences lack.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Exposure bias">
At inference there are no gold tokens: the model must generate autoregressively, feeding each prediction back as the next input. If it errs at position $t$, every later prediction conditions on a prefix the model never saw during training — a train–test distribution mismatch known as **exposure bias**, and one that compounds with sequence length. Standard mitigations include scheduled sampling, which gradually substitutes model predictions for gold tokens during training, and sequence-level objectives that score the rollout directly rather than each position in isolation.
</Theorem>

## Label smoothing

The one-hot target asks the model for an impossible optimum: by <XRef id="prop-ce-logit-gradient" /> the loss keeps falling as $q_{x_t}\to 1$, which requires the correct logit to run off to $+\infty$. Label smoothing tempers this by moving a small share of the target mass off the correct class and spreading it uniformly, so the target the model chases is itself a proper distribution with finite optimal logits.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Label smoothing" id="def-label-smoothing">
**Label smoothing**<Sidenote>Introduced for image classification <Cite key="szegedy2016rethinking" />; its calibration effect analyzed by <Cite key="muller2019labelsmoothing" />.</Sidenote> replaces the hard one-hot target $\mathbf y = \mathbf e_{x_t}$ with a mixture of the one-hot vector and the uniform distribution,
$$
\tilde{\mathbf y} \defeq (1-\alpha)\,\mathbf e_{x_t} + \frac{\alpha}{\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}\,\mathbf 1, \qquad \alpha\in[0,1),
$$
where $\alpha$ is the smoothing parameter (typically $\alpha = 0.1$). The correct class then carries target weight $1 - \alpha + \alpha/\lvert\mathcal V\rvert$ instead of $1$, and every other class carries $\alpha/\lvert\mathcal V\rvert$ instead of $0$.
</Theorem>

The picture to hold is two bar charts over the vocabulary: the one-hot target is a single spike of height $1$ on the correct class; the smoothed target lowers that spike to $1-\alpha+\alpha/\lvert\mathcal V\rvert$ and lifts every other bar from $0$ to the floor $\alpha/\lvert\mathcal V\rvert$, redistributing a total mass $\alpha$. The loss against this smoothed target splits into two recognizable pieces.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Label-smoothing decomposition" id="prop-label-smoothing-decomposition">
With $\mathbf u = \mathbf 1/\lvert\mathcal V\rvert$ the uniform distribution, the cross-entropy against the label-smoothed target decomposes as
$$
H(\tilde{\mathbf y}, \mathbf q) = (1-\alpha)\,H(\mathbf e_{x_t}, \mathbf q) + \alpha\,H(\mathbf u, \mathbf q).
$$
The first term is the ordinary cross-entropy; the second, $\alpha\,H(\mathbf u,\mathbf q) = \alpha\big(H(\mathbf u) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbf u\,\|\,\mathbf q)\big)$, is a uniform-target penalty minimized when $\mathbf q = \mathbf u$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Cross-entropy is linear in its first argument, so expand $\tilde{\mathbf y}$ termwise, each step naming its rule.

**Linearity in the target.** By the definition of cross-entropy,
$$
H(\tilde{\mathbf y}, \mathbf q) = -\sum_k \tilde y_k \log q_k = -\sum_k\Big[(1-\alpha)[\mathbf e_{x_t}]_k + \tfrac{\alpha}{\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}\Big]\log q_k.
$$

**Split the sum.** Separate the two contributions and pull out the constants:
$$
H(\tilde{\mathbf y}, \mathbf q) = -(1-\alpha)\log q_{x_t} \;-\; \frac{\alpha}{\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}\sum_k \log q_k.
$$

**Reassemble as cross-entropies.** The first term is $(1-\alpha)\,H(\mathbf e_{x_t},\mathbf q)$ since $H(\mathbf e_{x_t},\mathbf q) = -\log q_{x_t}$; the second is $\alpha\,H(\mathbf u,\mathbf q)$ since $H(\mathbf u,\mathbf q) = -\sum_k \tfrac{1}{\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}\log q_k$. Hence
$$
H(\tilde{\mathbf y}, \mathbf q) = (1-\alpha)\,H(\mathbf e_{x_t}, \mathbf q) + \alpha\,H(\mathbf u, \mathbf q).
$$
Finally $H(\mathbf u,\mathbf q) = H(\mathbf u) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbf u\,\|\,\mathbf q)$ by the cross-entropy decomposition (<XRef id="prop-ce-kl" />), and the divergence term is minimized at $\mathbf q = \mathbf u$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Smoothed loss">$H(\tilde{\mathbf y},\mathbf q) = (1-\alpha)H(\mathbf e_{x_t},\mathbf q) + \alpha H(\mathbf u,\mathbf q)$: standard CE plus a uniform-KL penalty.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Calibration view">
The decomposition explains label smoothing through the logit gradient (<XRef id="prop-ce-logit-gradient" />). Swapping the one-hot target for $\tilde{\mathbf y}$ turns the logit gradient from $\mathbf q - \mathbf e_{x_t}$ into $\mathbf q - \tilde{\mathbf y}$: the correct class receives a smaller upward push and the incorrect classes less aggressive downward pushes. This caps the logit gaps the model can open — the same effect as adding a penalty that pulls $\mathbf q$ toward uniform — which is why label smoothing improves probability calibration and often generalization even when top-1 accuracy barely moves <Cite key="muller2019labelsmoothing" />.
</Theorem>

## Learning-rate schedule

The optimizer needs a step size that is small while its own statistics are unreliable, then large enough to make progress, then decaying for convergence. The original transformer encodes exactly this shape in a closed-form schedule: a linear ramp up to a peak at step $t_{\text{warmup}}$, followed by an inverse-square-root decay.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Warmup with inverse-square-root decay" id="def-lr-schedule">
The original transformer <Cite key="vaswani2017attention" /> sets the learning rate at training step $t$ to
$$
\operatorname{lr}(t) = d^{-1/2}\cdot \min\!\big(t^{-1/2},\; t\cdot t_{\text{warmup}}^{-3/2}\big).
$$
For $t \le t_{\text{warmup}}$ the second argument is smaller, so $\operatorname{lr}$ rises linearly in $t$; for $t > t_{\text{warmup}}$ the first argument is smaller, so $\operatorname{lr}$ decays as $t^{-1/2}$. The two pieces meet at the peak $t = t_{\text{warmup}}$, where $\operatorname{lr} = d^{-1/2}\,t_{\text{warmup}}^{-1/2}$.
</Theorem>

The curve to picture is a sharp triangle on the left — the linear warmup climbing to its peak at $t_{\text{warmup}}$ — joined to a long, slowly falling $t^{-1/2}$ tail on the right. The warmup branch exists because the adaptive optimizer is least trustworthy at the start.

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="The schedule">Linear ramp for $t\le t_{\text{warmup}}$, then $t^{-1/2}$ decay; peak at $d^{-1/2}t_{\text{warmup}}^{-1/2}$.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Warmup rationale" id="prop-warmup-rationale">
The Adam optimizer<Sidenote>Adaptive moment estimation; the de facto transformer optimizer <Cite key="kingma2015adam" />.</Sidenote> forms bias-corrected first- and second-moment estimates $\hat m_t = m_t/(1-\beta_1^{t})$ and $\hat v_t = v_t/(1-\beta_2^{t})$, where $m_t$ and $v_t$ are exponential moving averages of the gradients and squared gradients ($\beta_1\approx 0.9$, $\beta_2\approx 0.999$), and applies the update ($\epsilon$ a small constant guarding the division)
$$
\Delta\theta_t \propto \frac{\operatorname{lr}(t)\,\hat m_t}{\sqrt{\hat v_t} + \epsilon}.
$$
Early in training $\hat m_t$ and $\hat v_t$ are averaged over very few gradients, so the ratio $\hat m_t/(\sqrt{\hat v_t}+\epsilon)$ can swing sharply between steps; activation and gradient scales also shift fast over the first updates, especially in deep stacks. Holding $\operatorname{lr}(t)$ small while these estimates settle bounds the size of those poorly calibrated early updates and the divergence they would otherwise cause.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Why warmup">Few gradients early ⇒ noisy $\hat v_t$ ⇒ erratic update scale. A small early $\operatorname{lr}$ keeps steps bounded until the moment estimates settle.</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Modern variants">
Production language models usually replace the inverse-square-root tail with a cosine decay from a peak to a small floor (commonly a tenfold drop), keep a warmup of many thousands of steps, and decouple weight decay from the adaptive step. The warmup *rationale* (<XRef id="prop-warmup-rationale" />) — small steps while the optimizer's moment estimates and the network's activation scales stabilize — is unchanged; only the shape of the decay differs.
</Theorem>

## Weight initialization

Warmup controls the optimizer at $t=0$; initialization controls the *network* at $t=0$. The aim is the same variance-preservation principle that motivated the $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ attention scaling: choose the initial weight scale so that activations and gradients neither shrink nor blow up as they pass through depth.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Xavier initialization" id="def-xavier-init">
For a weight matrix $\mathbf W\in\R^{d_{\text{in}}\times d_{\text{out}}}$, **Xavier (Glorot) initialization**<Sidenote>Derived to preserve activation and gradient variance across layers <Cite key="glorot2010understanding" />.</Sidenote> draws each entry independently as
$$
w_{ij} \sim \mathcal N\!\Big(0,\; \frac{2}{d_{\text{in}} + d_{\text{out}}}\Big).
$$
This holds the variance of activations and gradients roughly constant across layers when the activation is approximately linear near the origin.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Scaled residual initialization">
A residual block *adds* each sublayer's output to the residual stream, so with $N$ such blocks the stream's variance would grow as $O(N)$ if every sublayer initialized at unit scale. Scaling the variance of the output projections (the $\mathbf W^O$ of attention and the second feed-forward matrix) by $1/(2N)$,
$$
w_{ij} \sim \mathcal N\!\Big(0,\; \frac{2}{(d_{\text{in}} + d_{\text{out}})\cdot 2N}\Big),
$$
holds the accumulated residual-stream variance at order one across depth. This depth-scaled initialization is the GPT-2 training recipe <Cite key="radford2019language" />; the original transformer paper prescribes no residual-depth scaling.
</Theorem>

These five choices are interlocking, not independent. The loss (<XRef id="prop-ce-kl-training" />) aligns the model with the data distribution; teacher forcing (<XRef id="def-teacher-forcing" />) makes that loss computable in parallel at the cost of exposure bias; label smoothing (<XRef id="prop-label-smoothing-decomposition" />) keeps the targets — and therefore the optimal logits — finite; warmup (<XRef id="prop-warmup-rationale" />) protects the optimizer while its statistics are unreliable; and scaled initialization (<XRef id="def-xavier-init" />) keeps the forward and backward signals at order one through depth. Removing any one degrades or destroys training: the trained transformer is a single coupled system, its architecture and its optimization co-designed.

## Exercises

Attempt each before expanding its solution.

**Exercise 10.1 (Prove).** Show that minimizing the cross-entropy loss $H(p_{\text{data}}, p_\theta)$ over $\theta$ is equivalent to minimizing $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}\,\|\,p_\theta)$, and identify the term that is constant in $\theta$.

<SolutionBox>

$H(p_{\text{data}}, p_\theta) = -\sum_x p_{\text{data}}(x)\log p_\theta(x)$. Add and subtract $\sum_x p_{\text{data}}(x)\log p_{\text{data}}(x)$:
$$
H(p_{\text{data}}, p_\theta) = H(p_{\text{data}}) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}\,\|\,p_\theta).
$$
The entropy $H(p_{\text{data}})$ is fixed by the data, so $\nabla_\theta H(p_{\text{data}}, p_\theta) = \nabla_\theta D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}\,\|\,p_\theta)$: the two objectives share the same minimizer. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 10.2 (Derive).** Starting from $\mathcal L_t = -\log q_{t,x_t}$ and the softmax Jacobian, derive the logit gradient $\nabla_{\mathbf z_t}\mathcal L_t = \mathbf q_t - \mathbf e_{x_t}$, and state how it changes for a general target distribution $\mathbf y$.

<SolutionBox>

With $\mathcal L_t = -\log q_{t,x_t}$, only the $x_t$-th coordinate of $\mathbf q_t$ enters, so $\partial\mathcal L_t/\partial z_{t,k} = -q_{t,x_t}^{-1}\,\partial q_{t,x_t}/\partial z_{t,k}$. The softmax Jacobian gives $\partial q_{t,x_t}/\partial z_{t,k} = q_{t,x_t}([\mathbf e_{x_t}]_k - q_{t,k})$; the factor $q_{t,x_t}$ cancels, leaving $\partial\mathcal L_t/\partial z_{t,k} = q_{t,k} - [\mathbf e_{x_t}]_k$, i.e. $\nabla_{\mathbf z_t}\mathcal L_t = \mathbf q_t - \mathbf e_{x_t}$. For a general target $\mathbf y$ the same per-coordinate derivative weighted by $y_k$ gives $\mathbf q_t - \mathbf y$. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 10.3 (Derive).** Using linearity of cross-entropy in its first argument, prove the label-smoothing decomposition $H(\tilde{\mathbf y},\mathbf q) = (1-\alpha)H(\mathbf e_{x_t},\mathbf q) + \alpha H(\mathbf u,\mathbf q)$, and explain why the second term is a confidence penalty.

<SolutionBox>

Cross-entropy is linear in its first argument. With $\tilde{\mathbf y} = (1-\alpha)\mathbf e_{x_t} + \tfrac{\alpha}{\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}\mathbf 1$,
$$
H(\tilde{\mathbf y},\mathbf q) = -(1-\alpha)\log q_{x_t} - \tfrac{\alpha}{\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}\sum_k\log q_k = (1-\alpha)H(\mathbf e_{x_t},\mathbf q) + \alpha H(\mathbf u,\mathbf q),
$$
with $\mathbf u = \mathbf 1/\lvert\mathcal V\rvert$. Since $H(\mathbf u,\mathbf q) = H(\mathbf u) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbf u\,\|\,\mathbf q)$, the second term is a KL penalty pulling $\mathbf q$ toward uniform — a confidence penalty, minimized at $\mathbf q = \mathbf u$. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 10.4 (Compute).** For $\lvert\mathcal V\rvert = 5$, target token $x_t = 4$, and logits $\mathbf z = (2.0, 1.0, 0.5, 3.0, -1.0)$, compute the softmax $\mathbf q$, the standard cross-entropy $-\log q_4$, and the label-smoothed loss at $\alpha = 0.1$; confirm the smoothed loss exceeds the standard one.

<SolutionBox>

With $\mathbf z = (2,1,0.5,3,-1)$, the exponentials $(7.389,2.718,1.649,20.086,0.368)$ sum to $\approx 32.21$, so $\mathbf q \approx (0.229,0.084,0.051,0.624,0.011)$. Standard CE: $-\log q_4 \approx 0.472$. The per-class $-\log q_k \approx (1.472,2.472,2.972,0.472,4.472)$ (computed from the exact softmax, not the rounded $\mathbf q$) have mean $\approx 2.372$, so the smoothed loss ($\alpha=0.1$) is $H(\tilde{\mathbf y},\mathbf q) \approx 0.9\cdot0.472 + 0.1\cdot2.372 \approx 0.662 > 0.472$: even a correct prediction is penalized for being peaked. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 10.5 (Construct).** Exhibit a one-token-vocabulary-of-three example with $\mathbf q = (0.80, 0.10, 0.10)$ and correct class $1$, give the standard logit gradient $\mathbf q - \mathbf e_1$ and the smoothed gradient $\mathbf q - \tilde{\mathbf y}$ at $\alpha = 0.1$, and verify the smoothed update is less aggressive on every coordinate.

<SolutionBox>

With $\mathbf q = (0.80,0.10,0.10)$ and correct class $1$, the standard gradient is $\mathbf q - \mathbf e_1 = (-0.20,0.10,0.10)$. For $\lvert\mathcal V\rvert=3$, $\alpha=0.1$, $\tilde{\mathbf y} = (0.9333,0.0333,0.0333)$, so $\mathbf q - \tilde{\mathbf y} \approx (-0.1333,0.0667,0.0667)$. Every coordinate is smaller in magnitude ($0.133 < 0.20$; $0.067 < 0.10$): the correct logit is pushed up less and the incorrect logits down less — how label smoothing curbs overconfidence. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
