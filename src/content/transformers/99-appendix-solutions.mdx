# Appendix: Solutions

Solutions to the chapter exercises, deferred here so each exercise stays retrieval practice.

## Notation and Prerequisites

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 1 (translation invariance ⇒ J·1 = 0)" id="soln-tf0-ex1">
For any $c\in\R$, multiplying the numerator and denominator of $\softmax(\mathbf z)_i$ by $e^{c}$ leaves each entry unchanged, so $\softmax(\mathbf z + c\mathbf 1) = \softmax(\mathbf z)$. The Jacobian then annihilates $\mathbf 1$ directly: $\mathbf J\mathbf 1 = \operatorname{diag}(\mathbf p)\mathbf 1 - \mathbf p(\mathbf p^\top\mathbf 1) = \mathbf p - \mathbf p = \mathbf 0$, since $\mathbf p^\top\mathbf 1 = 1$. (Equivalently, differentiating the invariance in $c$ at $c=0$ gives the directional derivative $\mathbf J\mathbf 1 = \mathbf 0$.) $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 2 (rank of the softmax Jacobian)" id="soln-tf0-ex2">
Suppose $\mathbf J\mathbf x = \mathbf 0$, i.e. $\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf p)\mathbf x = \mathbf p(\mathbf p^\top\mathbf x)$. Componentwise, $p_i x_i = p_i(\mathbf p^\top\mathbf x)$ for every $i$. Since every $p_i > 0$, divide by $p_i$ to get $x_i = \mathbf p^\top\mathbf x$ — the same constant for all $i$. Hence $\mathbf x = c\mathbf 1$, so the null space is $\operatorname{span}\{\mathbf 1\}$, of dimension $1$. By rank–nullity, $\operatorname{rank}\mathbf J = K - 1$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 3 (stable softmax + invariance check)" id="soln-tf0-ex3">
Subtract $\max_i z_i = 2$: $\mathbf z - 2\mathbf 1 = (0, -2, -3)$. Exponentiate to $(1,\ e^{-2},\ e^{-3}) \approx (1,\ 0.135,\ 0.0498)$, with sum $\approx 1.185$, so
$$
\softmax(\mathbf z) \approx (0.844,\ 0.114,\ 0.042).
$$
Adding $5\mathbf 1$ gives $(7,5,4)$; every weight is multiplied by $e^{5}$, which cancels in the normalization, returning the identical vector (Proposition 1). $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 4 (cross-entropy = entropy + KL)" id="soln-tf0-ex4">
Add and subtract $\sum_k p_k \log p_k$:
$$
H(\mathbf p,\mathbf q) = -\sum_k p_k\log q_k = -\sum_k p_k\log p_k + \sum_k p_k\log\frac{p_k}{q_k} = H(\mathbf p) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbf p\,\|\,\mathbf q).
$$
Because $H(\mathbf p)$ depends only on the (fixed) data distribution, $\nabla_{\boldsymbol\theta} H(\mathbf p,\mathbf q) = \nabla_{\boldsymbol\theta} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbf p\,\|\,\mathbf q)$: the two objectives share the same gradient and minimizer in the model parameters. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

## The Attention Mechanism

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 1 (row-stochastic ⇒ convex combination)" id="soln-tf-ex1">
Fix a query row $i$ and write the logits $\ell_{ij} = \inner{\mathbf q_i}{\mathbf k_j}/\sqrt{d_k}$. The attention weight $w_{ij} = e^{\ell_{ij}}/\sum_{j'} e^{\ell_{ij'}}$ is **positive** (an exponential over a positive sum) and the row **sums to one** ($\sum_j w_{ij} = \sum_j e^{\ell_{ij}} / \sum_{j'} e^{\ell_{ij'}} = 1$); so the row is a probability vector — row-stochastic. The output row is $\sum_j w_{ij}\,\mathbf v_j$ with $w_{ij}\ge 0$ and $\sum_j w_{ij}=1$, i.e. a **convex combination** of the value rows. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 2 (unscaled variance and saturation)" id="soln-tf-ex2">
By the variance-preservation theorem (unscaled case), $\Var(\inner{\mathbf q}{\mathbf k}) = d_k$. As $d_k\to\infty$ the logit spread grows without bound, so within a softmax row the largest logit dominates and the row concentrates toward a **one-hot** vector. At a near-one-hot $\mathbf p$, the softmax Jacobian $\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf p) - \mathbf p\mathbf p^\top$ has entries $p_i(1-p_i)\to 0$, so gradients through the softmax **vanish** (gradient saturation). Dividing logits by $\sqrt{d_k}$ holds the variance at $1$ and prevents this. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 3 (compute + translation invariance)" id="soln-tf-ex3">
With $\mathbf z=(2,1,0)$ the unnormalized weights are $(e^2, e^1, e^0) \approx (7.389,\, 2.718,\, 1.000)$, summing to $\approx 11.107$, so
$$
\softmax(\mathbf z) \approx (0.665,\; 0.245,\; 0.090).
$$
For $\mathbf z + 5\mathbf 1 = (7,6,5)$ every weight is multiplied by $e^5$, which cancels in the normalization, giving the identical vector — exactly Proposition 1. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

## Input Representations

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 1 (PE(0) and PE(1) for d=4)" id="soln-tf1-ex1">
With $d = 4$ the frequencies are $\omega_0 = 10000^{-0/4} = 1$ and $\omega_1 = 10000^{-2/4} = 0.01$. By Definition 3, $\operatorname{PE}(t) = \big(\sin(\omega_0 t),\, \cos(\omega_0 t),\, \sin(\omega_1 t),\, \cos(\omega_1 t)\big)$, so
$$
\operatorname{PE}(0) = (0,\, 1,\, 0,\, 1), \qquad
\operatorname{PE}(1) \approx (0.8415,\, 0.5403,\, 0.0100,\, 0.9999).
$$
They differ already in the fast pair ($\sin 1 \approx 0.8415 \ne 0$), so $\operatorname{PE}(0) \ne \operatorname{PE}(1)$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 2 (verify the rotation block)" id="soln-tf1-ex2">
For offset $k = 1$ and pair $i = 0$ ($\omega_0 = 1$), the rotation is $\mathbf R_0(1) = \begin{pmatrix} \cos 1 & \sin 1 \\ -\sin 1 & \cos 1 \end{pmatrix}$. The first pair of $\operatorname{PE}(0)$ is $(0, 1)^\top$, and
$$
\mathbf R_0(1)\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \sin 1 \\ \cos 1 \end{pmatrix} \approx \begin{pmatrix} 0.8415 \\ 0.5403 \end{pmatrix},
$$
exactly the first pair of $\operatorname{PE}(1)$. This verifies $\operatorname{PE}(t+k) = \mathbf R_i(k)\,\operatorname{PE}(t)$ on the block. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 3 (PE inner product depends only on the offset)" id="soln-tf1-ex3">
For pair $i$, the contribution to the inner product is
$$
\sin(\omega_i t)\sin(\omega_i(t+k)) + \cos(\omega_i t)\cos(\omega_i(t+k)) = \cos(\omega_i k),
$$
using $\cos(A - B) = \cos A\cos B + \sin A\sin B$. Summing over the $d/2$ pairs, $\operatorname{PE}(t)\cdot\operatorname{PE}(t+k) = \sum_{i=0}^{d/2-1}\cos(\omega_i k)$, which depends only on the offset $k$, not on $t$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 4 (a lookup table has no shift law)" id="soln-tf1-ex4">
Suppose a fixed map sent each token's embedding to the next, $\mathbf E[x+1,:] = g(\mathbf E[x,:])$ for one fixed $g$. The rows of $\mathbf E$ are free parameters, so choose $x, x'$ with $\mathbf E[x,:] = \mathbf E[x',:]$ yet $\mathbf E[x+1,:] \ne \mathbf E[x'+1,:]$; then $g$ must send one input to two outputs — a contradiction. The index $x{+}1$ is an arbitrary symbol, not a geometric increment. What makes $\operatorname{PE}$'s shift law possible is that position enters through angles $\omega_i t$, where adding $k$ is the same rotation $\mathbf R_i(k)$ for every $t$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 5 (RoPE composition)" id="soln-tf1-ex5">
Each diagonal block of $\mathbf\Theta(\cdot)$ is $\mathbf R(\theta_i\,\cdot)$. By Definition 5, $\mathbf R(\theta_i s)\mathbf R(\theta_i t) = \mathbf R(\theta_i(s+t))$ and $\mathbf R(\theta_i t)^\top = \mathbf R(-\theta_i t)$; blockwise this gives $\mathbf\Theta(s)\mathbf\Theta(t) = \mathbf\Theta(s+t)$ and $\mathbf\Theta(t)^\top = \mathbf\Theta(-t)$. Then $f(\mathbf q,m)^\top f(\mathbf k,n) = \mathbf q^\top\mathbf\Theta(m)^\top\mathbf\Theta(n)\mathbf k = \mathbf q^\top\mathbf\Theta(n-m)\mathbf k$, which is Theorem 3. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

## Multi-Head Attention

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 1 (parameter count is 4d² and h-independent)" id="soln-tf3-ex1">
Take $d_k = d_v = d/h$. Each head holds $\mathbf W_i^Q,\mathbf W_i^K,\mathbf W_i^V$, each $d\cdot(d/h)$ entries, so one head has $3d^2/h$ parameters. Summing over $h$ heads the $h$ cancels: $h\cdot 3d^2/h = 3d^2$. The output projection $\mathbf W^O\in\R^{d\times d}$ adds $d^2$. Total $4d^2$, independent of $h$: each new head is correspondingly narrower ($d_k = d/h$), so per-head cost falls exactly as fast as head count rises. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 2 (per-head logit rank ≤ d_k)" id="soln-tf3-ex2">
For one head $\mathbf Q_i,\mathbf K_i\in\R^{n\times d_k}$, so $\mathbf L_i = \mathbf Q_i\mathbf K_i^\top\in\R^{n\times n}$. By the rank-of-a-product inequality, $\operatorname{rank}(\mathbf L_i)\le\min(\operatorname{rank}\mathbf Q_i,\operatorname{rank}\mathbf K_i)\le d_k$. When $n > d_k$ this is below the maximal rank $n$, so a single head cannot realize an arbitrary full-rank interaction. Concatenating $h$ heads supplies $h$ independent rank-$\le d_k$ patterns, which $\mathbf W^O$ mixes and later layers compose. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 3 (KV-cache size by variant)" id="soln-tf3-ex3">
The cache stores one key and one value matrix per distinct K/V projection, each $n\times d_k$, i.e. $2n\,d_k$ entries per distinct projection: MHA $2n\,h\,d_k$, GQA $2n\,g\,d_k$, MQA $2n\,d_k$. As fractions of MHA these are $1$, $g/h$, $1/h$. **MQA** saves the cache by the full factor $h$; GQA interpolates. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 4 (shape trace, d=512, h=8)" id="soln-tf3-ex4">
With $d=512$, $h=8$, head width $64$. From $(B,T,512)$: Q/K/V projection $\to (B,T,512)$; per-head split (reshape $512 = 8\times64$, move head axis) $\to (B,8,T,64)$; scores $\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top/\sqrt{64}\to(B,8,T,T)$, softmax weights $(B,8,T,T)$, times values $\to (B,8,T,64)$; concatenate $\to (B,T,512)$; output projection $\to (B,T,512)$. The layer is shape-preserving. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 5 (GQA parameter count interpolates)" id="soln-tf3-ex5">
Query and output projections are unchanged across variants, each $d^2$. Only K,V shrink with grouping: $g$ groups give $2(g/h)d^2$. Hence $P_{\text{GQA}}(g) = d^2 + 2\tfrac{g}{h}d^2 + d^2 = 2d^2 + 2\tfrac{g}{h}d^2$. At $g=h$ this is $4d^2$ (MHA); at $g=1$ it is $2d^2 + 2d^2/h$ (MQA); affine and increasing in $g$, it interpolates monotonically. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

## The Transformer Block

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 1 (residual gradient identity + all-skip term)" id="soln-tf4-ex1">
Each residual layer $\mathbf x_{i+1} = \mathbf x_i + f_i(\mathbf x_i)$ has Jacobian $\mathbf I + \partial f_i/\partial\mathbf x_i$ (sum rule). By the chain rule $\partial\mathcal L/\partial\mathbf x_\ell = (\partial\mathcal L/\partial\mathbf x_L)\prod_{i=\ell}^{L-1}(\mathbf I + \mathbf J_i)$ with $\mathbf J_i = \partial f_i/\partial\mathbf x_i$. Expanding the product by distributivity gives a sum over subsets $S\subseteq\{\ell,\dots,L-1\}$ of ordered products $\prod_{i\in S}\mathbf J_i$; the empty subset contributes the empty product $\mathbf I$ — the all-skip term, containing no sublayer Jacobian. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 2 (post-norm vs pre-norm layer Jacobian)" id="soln-tf4-ex2">
For post-norm $\mathbf y = \operatorname{LN}(\mathbf x + f(\mathbf x))$, with $\mathbf u = \mathbf x + f(\mathbf x)$, the chain rule gives $\partial\mathbf y/\partial\mathbf x = \mathbf J_{\operatorname{LN}}(\mathbf u)(\mathbf I + \partial f/\partial\mathbf x)$: the residual $\mathbf I$ sits inside the bracket, multiplied by $\mathbf J_{\operatorname{LN}}$ — no unmultiplied $\mathbf I$ survives. For pre-norm $\mathbf y = \mathbf x + f(\operatorname{LN}(\mathbf x))$, the sum rule gives $\partial\mathbf y/\partial\mathbf x = \mathbf I + (\partial f/\partial\mathbf x)\mathbf J_{\operatorname{LN}}(\mathbf x)$, in which the leading $\mathbf I$ stands alone. Pre-norm leaves the unmultiplied identity in the gradient; post-norm does not. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 3 (LN shift-invariance ⇒ J·1 = 0)" id="soln-tf4-ex3">
Shifting $\mathbf x\mapsto\mathbf x + c\mathbf 1$ shifts the feature mean to $\mu_{\mathbf x} + c$ and leaves the variance unchanged, so the centered vector $\mathbf x - \mu_{\mathbf x}\mathbf 1$ and the denominator are unchanged: $\operatorname{LN}(\mathbf x + c\mathbf 1) = \operatorname{LN}(\mathbf x)$ for all $c$. Differentiating in $c$ at $c=0$ gives the directional derivative along $\mathbf 1$: $(\partial\operatorname{LN}/\partial\mathbf x)\mathbf 1 = \mathbf 0$ — the Jacobian annihilates $\mathbf 1$, the same null-space structure as the softmax Jacobian. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 4 (compute layer norm)" id="soln-tf4-ex4">
With $\mathbf x = (4,0,-2,2)$: $\mu_{\mathbf x} = 1$, centered $(3,-1,-3,1)$, $\sigma_{\mathbf x}^2 = \tfrac14(9+1+9+1) = 5$. With $\boldsymbol\gamma=\mathbf 1,\boldsymbol\beta=\mathbf 0,\epsilon=0$,
$$
\operatorname{LN}(\mathbf x) = \tfrac{(3,-1,-3,1)}{\sqrt5} \approx (1.342,\,-0.447,\,-1.342,\,0.447),
$$
with feature mean $0$ and feature variance $1$, as required. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 5 (the all-skip term need not dominate)" id="soln-tf4-ex5">
Take $f(\mathbf x) = \mathbf x\mathbf A$ with $\mathbf A = 9\mathbf I$, so the layer Jacobian is $\mathbf I + 9\mathbf I = 10\mathbf I$, spectral norm $10$: the all-skip term is the single $\mathbf I$, but the full bracket has norm $10$, so the skip route is a $10\%$ share, not dominant. Conversely $\mathbf A = -\tfrac{9}{10}\mathbf I$ gives bracket $\tfrac{1}{10}\mathbf I$. Either way, <XRef id="thm-tf4-gradient-highway" /> guarantees the identity route exists; it does not bound its relative magnitude. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

## Architecture Composition

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 1 (cross-attention output is a convex combination)" id="soln-tf5-ex1">
Cross-attention is $\operatorname{Attn}(\mathbf Q,\mathbf K,\mathbf V)$ with $\mathbf Q = \mathbf X_{\text{dec}}\mathbf W^Q$, $\mathbf K = \mathbf H_{\text{enc}}\mathbf W^K$, $\mathbf V = \mathbf H_{\text{enc}}\mathbf W^V$. Each row of $\softmax(\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top/\sqrt{d_k})$ is row-stochastic, so decoder output row $i$ is $\sum_j w_{ij}\mathbf v_j$, a convex combination of the encoder value rows (<XRef id="prop-row-stochastic-convex" />). It therefore lies in the convex hull of the encoder values: the decoder selects and averages encoded source content, it cannot extrapolate beyond it. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 2 (architectures as two binary switches)" id="soln-tf5-ex2">
The switches are (a) self-attention causal vs bidirectional and (b) cross-attention present vs absent (an encoder exists exactly when (b) is present). **Encoder-only:** bidirectional, no cross-attention (BERT). **Encoder-decoder:** bidirectional encoder + causal decoder with cross-attention pulling $\mathbf K,\mathbf V$ from $\mathbf H_{\text{enc}}$ (original transformer, T5). **Decoder-only:** causal, no cross-attention; conditioning is in-context via the prompt (GPT, LLaMA). The fourth setting (bidirectional + cross-attention) is non-standard, since bidirectional self-attention is incompatible with the autoregressive generation cross-attention conditioning serves. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 3 (attention/FFN time crossover)" id="soln-tf5-ex3">
Per layer, attention is $O(n^2 d)$ and the FFN is $O(n\,d\,d_{\text{ff}})$. Setting $n^2 d = n\,d\,d_{\text{ff}}$ and cancelling $nd$ gives $n = d_{\text{ff}}$; with $d=1024$, $d_{\text{ff}}=4096$, the crossover is $n \approx 4096$. FlashAttention does not move it — it computes the same $O(n^2 d)$ operator, tiling to cut memory traffic and constants, not the asymptotic. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 4 (12·N·d² block scaling)" id="soln-tf5-ex4">
Per block, MHA contributes $4d^2$ and the FFN $2 d\,d_{\text{ff}}$ (layer-norm scale/shift are $O(d)$, lower order), so one block is $4d^2 + 2 d\,d_{\text{ff}}$. With $d_{\text{ff}} = 4d$ this is $4d^2 + 8d^2 = 12d^2$, and $N$ blocks give $\approx 12 N d^2$. The term $|\mathcal V|\,d$ is separate: it governs the embedding (and tied output projection), scaling with vocabulary rather than depth. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 5 (permutation equivariance forces the restriction)" id="soln-tf5-ex5">
Let $\mathbf P$ be a permutation matrix. Self-attention applies the same position-independent linear maps to every row, so permuting inputs by $\mathbf P$ permutes $\mathbf Q,\mathbf K,\mathbf V$ identically, sends the logits to $\mathbf P(\mathbf Q\mathbf K^\top)\mathbf P^\top$, and (row-wise softmax + per-row FFN) sends the output to $\mathbf P f(\mathbf X)$: the network is permutation-equivariant. A uniform limit of equivariant maps is equivariant, so without positional encodings the reachable class is exactly the permutation-equivariant continuous maps. A position-sensitive target — e.g. "return the first token," $f(\mathbf X) = \mathbf x_1$ broadcast — has $f(\mathbf P\mathbf X)\ne\mathbf P f(\mathbf X)$ and is unreachable; positional encodings break the symmetry and restore it. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

## Training a Transformer

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 1 (CE minimization = KL minimization)" id="soln-tf6-ex1">
$H(p_{\text{data}}, p_\theta) = -\sum_x p_{\text{data}}(x)\log p_\theta(x)$. Add and subtract $\sum_x p_{\text{data}}(x)\log p_{\text{data}}(x)$:
$$
H(p_{\text{data}}, p_\theta) = H(p_{\text{data}}) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}\,\|\,p_\theta).
$$
The entropy $H(p_{\text{data}})$ is fixed by the data, so $\nabla_\theta H(p_{\text{data}}, p_\theta) = \nabla_\theta D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}\,\|\,p_\theta)$: the two objectives share the same minimizer. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 2 (softmax + CE logit gradient)" id="soln-tf6-ex2">
With $\mathcal L_t = -\log q_{t,x_t}$, only the $x_t$-th coordinate of $\mathbf q_t$ enters, so $\partial\mathcal L_t/\partial z_{t,k} = -q_{t,x_t}^{-1}\,\partial q_{t,x_t}/\partial z_{t,k}$. The softmax Jacobian gives $\partial q_{t,x_t}/\partial z_{t,k} = q_{t,x_t}([\mathbf e_{x_t}]_k - q_{t,k})$; the factor $q_{t,x_t}$ cancels, leaving $\partial\mathcal L_t/\partial z_{t,k} = q_{t,k} - [\mathbf e_{x_t}]_k$, i.e. $\nabla_{\mathbf z_t}\mathcal L_t = \mathbf q_t - \mathbf e_{x_t}$. For a general target $\mathbf y$ the same per-coordinate derivative weighted by $y_k$ gives $\mathbf q_t - \mathbf y$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 3 (label-smoothing decomposition)" id="soln-tf6-ex3">
Cross-entropy is linear in its first argument. With $\tilde{\mathbf y} = (1-\alpha)\mathbf e_{x_t} + \tfrac{\alpha}{\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}\mathbf 1$,
$$
H(\tilde{\mathbf y},\mathbf q) = -(1-\alpha)\log q_{x_t} - \tfrac{\alpha}{\lvert\mathcal V\rvert}\sum_k\log q_k = (1-\alpha)H(\mathbf e_{x_t},\mathbf q) + \alpha H(\mathbf u,\mathbf q),
$$
with $\mathbf u = \mathbf 1/\lvert\mathcal V\rvert$. Since $H(\mathbf u,\mathbf q) = H(\mathbf u) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbf u\,\|\,\mathbf q)$, the second term is a KL penalty pulling $\mathbf q$ toward uniform — a confidence penalty, minimized at $\mathbf q = \mathbf u$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 4 (CE vs label-smoothed loss)" id="soln-tf6-ex4">
With $\mathbf z = (2,1,0.5,3,-1)$, the exponentials $(7.389,2.718,1.649,20.086,0.368)$ sum to $\approx 32.21$, so $\mathbf q \approx (0.229,0.084,0.051,0.624,0.011)$. Standard CE: $-\log q_4 \approx 0.471$. The per-class $-\log q_k \approx (1.474,2.477,2.976,0.471,4.510)$ have mean $\approx 2.382$, so the smoothed loss ($\alpha=0.1$) is $H(\tilde{\mathbf y},\mathbf q) \approx 0.9\cdot0.471 + 0.1\cdot2.382 \approx 0.662 > 0.471$: even a correct prediction is penalized for being peaked. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 5 (smoothed gradient is less aggressive)" id="soln-tf6-ex5">
With $\mathbf q = (0.80,0.10,0.10)$ and correct class $1$, the standard gradient is $\mathbf q - \mathbf e_1 = (-0.20,0.10,0.10)$. For $\lvert\mathcal V\rvert=3$, $\alpha=0.1$, $\tilde{\mathbf y} = (0.9333,0.0333,0.0333)$, so $\mathbf q - \tilde{\mathbf y} \approx (-0.1333,0.0667,0.0667)$. Every coordinate is smaller in magnitude ($0.133 < 0.20$; $0.067 < 0.10$): the correct logit is pushed up less and the incorrect logits down less — how label smoothing curbs overconfidence. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

## Encoders for Detection

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 1 (mean pooling is a convex combination)" id="soln-tf7-ex1">
Mean pooling is $\mathbf r_{\text{mean}} = \mathbf a\mathbf H$ with $\mathbf a = \tfrac1n\mathbf 1^\top$, a $1\times n$ row-stochastic vector, so $\mathbf r_{\text{mean}} = \sum_j \tfrac1n\mathbf h_j$ lies in the convex hull of the token states (<XRef id="prop-row-stochastic-convex" />). The prepended designated-position representation $\mathbf r_{\text{CLS}}$ escapes the hull because $\mathbf h_{\text{CLS}}$ is a learned input vector, not one of the $\mathbf h_j$ averaged: the encoder writes into that slot through attention and the residual path, so it need not be any convex mixture of the token states. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 2 (gradient of the contrastive row loss)" id="soln-tf7-ex2">
With $p_j = \softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})_j$ and $L_i = -\log p_i$, the loss is the cross-entropy of $\mathbf e_i$ against $\softmax(\mathbf M_{i,:})$, so by the softmax Jacobian (<XRef id="prop-softmax-jacobian" />), $\partial L_i/\partial M_{ij} = p_j - \delta_{ij}$: the matched logit $M_{ii}$ has gradient $p_i - 1 < 0$ (descent raises it), each mismatched $M_{ij}$ has gradient $p_j > 0$ (descent lowers it). A hard negative — large current similarity, hence large $p_j$ — receives the largest downward push, so the update is dominated by the negatives the model currently confuses with the true partner. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 3 (row-constant invariance ⇒ gaps only)" id="soln-tf7-ex3">
Adding $c$ to row $i$ replaces $\mathbf M_{i,:}$ by $\mathbf M_{i,:} + c\mathbf 1$; by translation invariance (<XRef id="prop-softmax-translation-inv" />) the softmax row is unchanged, so $p(i\mid i)$ and the loss are unchanged. Equivalently $p(i\mid i) = \big(1 + \sum_{j\ne i} e^{M_{ij}-M_{ii}}\big)^{-1}$ depends on the logits only through the gaps $M_{ii}-M_{ij}$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 4 (Gaussian neg-log-density = ½ Mahalanobis²)" id="soln-tf7-ex4">
For $\mathbf r\sim\mathcal N(\boldsymbol\mu,\boldsymbol\Sigma)$, $-\log\hat p(\mathbf r) = \tfrac12(\mathbf r-\boldsymbol\mu)\boldsymbol\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf r-\boldsymbol\mu)^\top + \tfrac{d}{2}\log(2\pi) + \tfrac12\log|\boldsymbol\Sigma| = \tfrac12 s_{\text{M}}(\mathbf r)^2 + \text{const}$, where $s_{\text{M}}^2$ is the squared Mahalanobis distance. Since $u\mapsto\tfrac12 u^2 + \text{const}$ is strictly increasing on $u\ge0$, the neg-log-density is a strictly increasing reparametrization of $s_{\text{M}}$, so thresholding either gives identical accept/reject sets and identical AUROC. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 5 (mean gap can survive while AUROC degrades)" id="soln-tf7-ex5">
Take 4 anomaly scores $\{5,6,7,8\}$ (mean $6.5$) and 4 inlier scores $\{1,2,3,4\}$ (mean $2.5$): every one of the $16$ cross-pairs has anomaly $>$ inlier, so $\widehat{\operatorname{AUROC}} = 16/16 = 1$, with mean gap $+4$. Now replace one inlier by a mild outlier: inliers $\{1,1,1,12\}$ (mean $3.75$). The mean gap stays positive ($6.5 - 3.75 = +2.75$), but ranking degrades: the three inliers at $1$ lose to all $4$ anomalies ($12$ correctly-ranked pairs) while the outlier at $12$ beats all $4$ anomalies ($4$ misranked pairs), so $\widehat{\operatorname{AUROC}} = 12/16 = 0.75 < 1$. A single badly-placed inlier flips many pairwise comparisons while barely moving the mean — the mean gap is a first-moment summary, AUROC is the pairwise ranking. Report AUROC (or a worst-slice metric), not a mean-score difference. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

## Practical Training Optimizations

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 1 (FP32+Adam baseline for 13B)" id="soln-tf8-ex1">
The master-copy block is $16N$ bytes: $4$ (FP32 params) $+ 4$ (FP32 grads) $+ 8$ (two FP32 Adam moments). For $N = 13\times10^9$, $16N = 208$ GB, which overflows an $80$ GB accelerator by $2.6\times$ — before any activations. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 2 (mixed-precision footprint for 7B)" id="soln-tf8-ex2">
The mixed-precision block is $20N$: a $6N$ parameter term ($4N$ FP32 master $+ 2N$ bf16 working), a $2N$ bf16 gradient term, and an $8N$ FP32 Adam term. For $N = 7\times10^9$, $20N = 140$ GB $= 1.75\times$ an $80$ GB accelerator. The naive "16-bit halves $16N$" guess fails because the FP32 master copy adds $4N$ on top of the working copy and the FP32 Adam state ($8N$, the largest term) is precision-pinned. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 3 (sqrt(L) is the checkpointing optimum)" id="soln-tf8-ex3">
Minimize $g(K) = K + L/K$. By AM–GM, $K + L/K \ge 2\sqrt{L}$, equality iff $K = \sqrt{L}$ (or $g'(K) = 1 - L/K^2 = 0$, $g'' > 0$). For $L=100$, $K=10$: store $10$ checkpoints instead of $100$, a $10\times$ activation-memory reduction, for one extra forward pass on backward ($\approx +1/3$ step time). $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 4 (LoRA trainable count for 13B)" id="soln-tf8-ex4">
With $K = 4\cdot40 = 160$ adapted matrices, $r=8$, $d=5120$, the trainable count is $2Krd = 2\cdot160\cdot8\cdot5120 \approx 13$ M $= 0.1\%$ of $13$ B. Adam state shrinks by the same factor $2r/d = 1/320$: the frozen $\mathbf W_0$ produce no gradient and thus no moments, so gradients and optimizer state live only on the adapters. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 5 (ZeRO-3 vs DDP for 70B on 64 workers)" id="soln-tf8-ex5">
The unsharded $(4+2+8)N = 14N$ block for $N=70$ B is $980$ GB. ZeRO-3/FSDP partitions all three terms by $P=64$: $\approx 15.3$ GB per worker — fits, with communication $\approx 3N b_p$ per step. DDP replicates the full $980$ GB per worker (infeasible on $80$ GB), communicating $2N b_g$ per step. ZeRO-3 trades $\sim1.5\times$ communication for full $1/P$ memory partitioning. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

## Inference Optimizations

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 1 (ridge point separates the regimes)" id="soln-tf9-ex1">
A kernel is compute-bound when $\Phi/F \ge \beta/\mathcal B$, i.e. $I \ge I^* = F/\mathcal B$; memory-bound is $I < I^*$, so the boundary is $I = I^*$. Below it, $T_{\text{token}} = \beta/\mathcal B$ and throughput is $\Phi/T_{\text{token}} = \mathcal B\,I$, which contains no $F$: raising the FLOP rate cannot help a memory-bound kernel. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 2 (KV-cache size)" id="soln-tf9-ex2">
By $M_{\text{KV}} = 2Lndb$ with $L=80$, $d=8192$, $b=2$, $n=8192$: $M_{\text{KV}} = 2\cdot80\cdot8192\cdot8192\cdot2 \approx 17.2$ GB per request. Linear in $n$, so doubling the context doubles the cache. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 3 (caching gives one factor of n)" id="soln-tf9-ex3">
A cached step $t$ does $\Theta(td)$ work (one new query against $t$ keys/values); uncached, $\Theta(t^2 d)$. Summing, $\sum_{t=1}^n \Theta(td) = \Theta(n^2 d)$ and $\sum_{t=1}^n \Theta(t^2 d) = \Theta(n^3 d)$, using $\sum t = \Theta(n^2)$, $\sum t^2 = \Theta(n^3)$. The cache removes one factor of $n$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 4 (online-softmax normalizer)" id="soln-tf9-ex4">
Induct on $j$. Base: $m_1 = s_1$, $\ell_1 = e^{s_1 - m_1} = 1$. Step: with $m_j = \max(m_{j-1}, s_j)$, the rescale $e^{m_{j-1}-m_j}$ re-centers each stored term, $e^{s_i - m_{j-1}}e^{m_{j-1}-m_j} = e^{s_i - m_j}$, so $\ell_j = \ell_{j-1}e^{m_{j-1}-m_j} + e^{s_j - m_j} = \sum_{i\le j} e^{s_i - m_j}$. The final division $\mathbf o_n/\ell_n$ cancels the common $e^{-m_n}$ — exactly softmax translation invariance (<XRef id="prop-softmax-translation-inv" />). $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof" name="Solution — Exercise 5 (speculative speedup)" id="soln-tf9-ex5">
With $c=0.1$, $K=4$, the denominator is $1 + Kc = 1.4$. At $\alpha=0.8$: $\mathbb E[T_K] = (1 - 0.8^5)/0.2 \approx 3.36$, speedup $\approx 2.40$. At $\alpha=0.4$: $\mathbb E[T_K] = (1 - 0.4^5)/0.6 \approx 1.66$, speedup $\approx 1.19$. As $K\to\infty$ with $\alpha=0.8$, $\mathbb E[T_K]\to 1/(1-\alpha) = 5$ while draft cost $Kc$ keeps growing, so an interior $K$ is optimal. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>
