# Multi-Head Attention

**Where we are.** The Attention chapter built a single scaled dot-product head: one triple of projections — $\mathbf W^Q, \mathbf W^K$ mapping $\R^{d}\to\R^{d_k}$ and $\mathbf W^V$ mapping $\R^{d}\to\R^{d_v}$ — producing one $n\times n$ weight matrix. One head can express only one pattern of token-to-token relationships at a time. Multi-head attention runs $h$ heads in parallel, each in its own $d_k$-dimensional subspace, then concatenates and remixes their outputs. This chapter fixes the construction (the attention head, then multi-head attention), proves the parameter count is independent of $h$ (<XRef id="prop-mha-params" />), establishes the per-head rank bound that makes a single head limited and many heads complementary (<XRef id="thm-low-rank" />), and derives two distinct routes to a smaller decode cache: MQA/GQA share materialized key/value heads, while MLA persists a joint latent channel (<XRef id="prop-kv-cache" />).

## The multi-head construction

A head is just the single-head attention of the previous chapter, but reading the input through its own narrow projection: instead of attending in the full $d$-dimensional space, head $i$ first compresses each token to a $d_k$-dimensional view, then runs scaled dot-product attention there.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Attention head" id="def-attention-head">
The $i$-th **attention head** ($i = 1,\dots,h$) carries its own learned projections $\mathbf W_i^Q, \mathbf W_i^K \in \R^{d\times d_k}$ and $\mathbf W_i^V \in \R^{d\times d_v}$. Given input $\mathbf X\in\R^{n\times d}$,
$$
\operatorname{head}_i \defeq \operatorname{Attn}(\mathbf X\mathbf W_i^Q,\ \mathbf X\mathbf W_i^K,\ \mathbf X\mathbf W_i^V) \in \R^{n\times d_v},
$$
where $\operatorname{Attn}$ is scaled dot-product attention (<XRef id="def-attention" />).
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Head splitting">Each head sees $\mathbf X$ through a rank-$\le d_k$ projection, so it attends in a $d_k$-dimensional subspace.</MarginNote>

Multi-head attention is then the concatenation of all heads' outputs followed by one shared linear map back to model width. The concatenation lays the $h$ output blocks side by side within each row; the output projection lets the layer mix across heads, so the heads are not forced to occupy disjoint output coordinates.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Multi-head attention" id="def-mha">
**Multi-head attention** concatenates the $h$ heads row-wise and applies a final linear projection:<Sidenote>The split–concatenate–project construction, with $h\mid d$ and $d_k=d_v=d/h$, is the original design of <Cite key="vaswani2017attention" />.</Sidenote>
$$
\operatorname{MHA}(\mathbf X) \defeq \operatorname{Concat}(\operatorname{head}_1,\dots,\operatorname{head}_h)\,\mathbf W^O \in \R^{n\times d},
$$
where $\operatorname{Concat}$ stacks the head outputs along the feature axis (giving an $n\times h\,d_v$ matrix whose $i$-th row is $([\operatorname{head}_1]_{i,:},\dots,[\operatorname{head}_h]_{i,:})$) and $\mathbf W^O\in\R^{h\,d_v\times d}$ is the **output projection matrix**. With the standard choice $h\mid d$ and $d_k = d_v = d/h$, the concatenation has width $h\cdot d_v = d$, so $\mathbf W^O\in\R^{d\times d}$.
</Theorem>

Schematically: the input $\mathbf X$ fans out to $h$ heads; each head runs attention in its $d_k$-dimensional subspace and returns an $n\times d_v$ block; the blocks concatenate into an $n\times h\,d_v$ matrix; and $\mathbf W^O$ maps that back to $n\times d$. The split into subspaces is what makes the heads independent; the output projection is what lets them recombine.

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Split / attend / concat / project">Four steps: project to $h$ subspaces, attend per head, concatenate, then mix with $\mathbf W^O$.</MarginNote>

## Parameter count and rank structure

When $h\mid d$, the standard choice $d_k = d_v = d/h$ is not arbitrary: it is exactly the width that holds the layer's parameter count fixed as $h$ varies, so adding heads buys more independent patterns at no extra cost.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="MHA parameter count" id="prop-mha-params">
Assume $h\mid d$. With $d_k = d_v = d/h$, the multi-head attention layer has $4d^2$ learnable parameters (excluding biases), independent of $h$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Count the four matrix families, each step naming what it sums.

**Per head.** Each head holds $\mathbf W_i^Q,\mathbf W_i^K\in\R^{d\times d_k}$ and $\mathbf W_i^V\in\R^{d\times d_v}$. With $d_k = d_v = d/h$, each matrix has $d\cdot(d/h)$ entries, so one head has $3\,d\cdot(d/h) = 3d^2/h$ parameters.

**Across heads.** Summing over the $h$ heads, the $h$ cancels: $h\cdot 3d^2/h = 3d^2$.

**Output projection.** $\mathbf W^O\in\R^{d\times d}$ adds $d^2$.

**Total.** $3d^2 + d^2 = 4d^2$, with no residual dependence on $h$ — more heads simply means narrower heads. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Why d_k = d/h">Total parameters $=4d^2$ and total FLOPs match a single $d$-wide head; e.g. Llama-3 uses $d=4096$, $h=32$, $d_k=128$.</MarginNote>

We now translate each head's pre-softmax logits into a single linear-algebra object — a product of two thin matrices — whose rank is bounded by their shared inner dimension $d_k$. The correspondence is exact: *head's logit matrix* ↦ *a rank-$\le d_k$ Gram-style product*, and the bound follows from the standard rank-of-a-product inequality. Where the analogy breaks: a true Gram matrix $\mathbf W\mathbf W^\top$ is symmetric positive semidefinite, while $\mathbf W_i^Q(\mathbf W_i^K)^\top$ has two distinct learned factors and is in general neither.

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="Low-rank factorization" id="thm-low-rank">
The $n\times n$ logit matrix of head $i$ factors through the $d_k$-dimensional subspace, so its rank is at most $\min(d_k, n)$. Explicitly,
$$
\mathbf X\mathbf W_i^Q(\mathbf W_i^K)^\top\mathbf X^\top
= \mathbf X\,\underbrace{\mathbf W_i^Q(\mathbf W_i^K)^\top}_{\in\,\R^{d\times d},\ \operatorname{rank}\le d_k}\,\mathbf X^\top,
$$
and therefore $\operatorname{rank}\!\big(\text{logit matrix}_i\big)\le \min(d_k, n)$.<Sidenote>The rank bound and its expressiveness consequences are studied in <Cite key="bhojanapalli2020low" />.</Sidenote>
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Two steps, each naming the rank fact it uses.

**Inner product is rank-$\le d_k$.** The matrix $\mathbf W_i^Q(\mathbf W_i^K)^\top$ is the product of $\mathbf W_i^Q\in\R^{d\times d_k}$ with $(\mathbf W_i^K)^\top\in\R^{d_k\times d}$. By the rank-of-a-product inequality, $\operatorname{rank}(\mathbf A\mathbf B)\le\min(\operatorname{rank}\mathbf A,\operatorname{rank}\mathbf B)$, and each factor has rank at most $d_k$ (its smaller dimension), so
$$
\operatorname{rank}\!\big(\mathbf W_i^Q(\mathbf W_i^K)^\top\big)\le \min\!\big(\operatorname{rank}\mathbf W_i^Q,\ \operatorname{rank}\mathbf W_i^K\big)\le d_k.
$$

**Pre/post-multiplication cannot raise rank.** Left- and right-multiplying by $\mathbf X$ and $\mathbf X^\top$ only contract column and row spaces, so $\operatorname{rank}(\mathbf X\mathbf C\mathbf X^\top)\le\operatorname{rank}\mathbf C\le d_k$. Combined with the trivial bound $\operatorname{rank}\le n$ for an $n\times n$ matrix, the logit matrix of head $i$ has rank at most $\min(d_k, n)$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Rank trade-off">One head with $d_k=d$: rank up to $\min(d,n)$, one pattern. Many heads with $d_k=1$: rank-1 each, very limited. Practice keeps $d_k\ge 32$.</MarginNote>

The bound is a statement about *one head's logits before softmax*, and it explains both why a single head is limited and why several heads are complementary. A single wide head ($d_k = d$) can realize logit matrices of rank up to $\min(d, n)$ — any logit matrix only when $n \le d$ — and in any case only one pattern; whereas $h$ heads of rank $\le d_k$ give $h$ *independently parameterized* low-rank patterns — one head may track syntactic adjacency, another semantic similarity — which $\mathbf W^O$ then mixes.

<Theorem kind="remark" name="What the rank bound does not say" id="rem-low-rank">
<XRef id="thm-low-rank" /> bounds each head's logit rank by $d_k$; it does **not** assert that the full module has logit rank $h\,d_k$. The heads are concatenated *after* their separate softmax nonlinearities and then mixed by $\mathbf W^O$, so there is no single $n\times n$ logit matrix for the module to take the rank of. The "effective rank $h\,d_k$" reading is an expressiveness heuristic, not a corollary of the displayed factorization.
</Theorem>

## Head sharing and latent compression

During autoregressive decoding the keys and values of every past token are retained in a **key/value cache** so they are not recomputed at each step. In standard multi-head attention each head keeps its own cache, so cache memory grows linearly in $h$ — and at long context lengths it can exceed the model weights themselves. Multi-Query and Grouped-Query Attention cut this cost by sharing the key and value projections across heads while keeping a separate query projection per head.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Multi-Query Attention (MQA)" id="def-mqa">
In **MQA** all $h$ heads share a single key projection $\mathbf W^K\in\R^{d\times d_k}$ and a single value projection $\mathbf W^V\in\R^{d\times d_v}$; only the query projections $\mathbf W_i^Q$ differ per head:<Sidenote>Introduced as a decoding-bandwidth optimization in <Cite key="shazeer2019mqa" />.</Sidenote>
$$
\operatorname{head}_i^{\text{MQA}} \defeq \operatorname{Attn}(\mathbf X\mathbf W_i^Q,\ \mathbf X\mathbf W^K,\ \mathbf X\mathbf W^V).
$$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Grouped-Query Attention (GQA)" id="def-gqa">
**GQA** partitions the $h$ heads into $g$ groups ($1\le g\le h$); heads within a group share one key projection and one value projection.<Sidenote>The interpolating family between MHA and MQA <Cite key="ainslie2023gqa" />.</Sidenote> MHA is the case $g = h$ (no sharing) and MQA is the case $g = 1$ (full sharing).
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="definition" name="MLA content compression" id="def-mla">
Let $\mathbf x_t\in\R^d$ be the row entering an attention layer. The **content-compression core of multi-head latent attention (MLA)** first maps it to a joint key/value latent
$$
\mathbf c_t^{KV}\defeq \mathbf x_t\mathbf W^{DKV}\in\R^{d_c},
\qquad \mathbf W^{DKV}\in\R^{d\times d_c},
$$
then up-projects that latent to concatenated content keys and values,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf k_t^C&\defeq\mathbf c_t^{KV}\mathbf W^{UK}\in\R^{h d_k}, &
\mathbf W^{UK}&\in\R^{d_c\times h d_k},\\
\mathbf v_t^C&\defeq\mathbf c_t^{KV}\mathbf W^{UV}\in\R^{h d_v}, &
\mathbf W^{UV}&\in\R^{d_c\times h d_v}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The $h$ width-$d_k$ and width-$d_v$ slices supply the attention heads. At decode time the content cache persists $\mathbf c_t^{KV}$ and reconstructs—or algebraically consumes—the head content derived from it.<Sidenote>DeepSeek-V2 introduced this latent KV-compression architecture <Cite key="deepseek2024v2" />.</Sidenote> Unlike GQA, this definition does not reduce the number of materialized attention heads: it moves the persisted content representation upstream to a shared latent channel. The latent width $d_c$ is therefore a separate design axis, not a fractional number of KV groups. This is deliberately a **content-cache** definition: a full implementation may persist positional or other auxiliary state in addition to $\mathbf c_t^{KV}$, and that width must be counted separately. The definition accounts for stored scalars, not the architecture-specific arithmetic used to reconstruct or absorb the up-projections.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before the next proof: in MHA the cache stores $\mathbf K_i\in\R^{n\times d_k}$ and $\mathbf V_i\in\R^{n\times d_v}$ for each of the $h$ heads. If $g$ groups share K and V, how many distinct such matrices remain?</MarginNote>

The cache cost follows by counting what is actually persisted. MHA, GQA, and MQA retain materialized key/value rows, so head sharing replaces "$h$ copies" by "$g$ copies." MLA instead retains the upstream content latent and rematerializes head slices from it.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="KV-cache width and memory" id="prop-kv-cache">
Define a layer's **per-token cache width** $d_{\text{cache}}$ to be the number of scalar entries it actually persists for one token.

**Materialized-head cache.** If the cache stores $h_k$ distinct key heads of width $d_k$ and $h_v$ distinct value heads of width $d_v$, then
$$
d_{\text{cache}} \defeq h_kd_k+h_vd_v.
$$
When keys and values use the same number of distinct heads,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{MHA: }&n\,h(d_k+d_v),&
\text{GQA}(g):&n\,g(d_k+d_v),&
\text{MQA: }&n(d_k+d_v).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus GQA uses a fraction $g/h$ of the MHA cache and MQA a fraction $1/h$, for fixed head widths. In the common special case $d_k=d_v$, these reduce to $2nhd_k$, $2ngd_k$, and $2nd_k$.

**Joint-latent content cache.** If the implementation of <XRef id="def-mla" /> persists only $\mathbf c_t^{KV}\in\R^{d_c}$, then its content-cache width is $d_{\text{cache}}=d_c$ and its length-$n$ content cache contains $nd_c$ scalars. This formula is conditional on that latent being the complete persisted state: any separately cached positional or auxiliary state must be added to $d_c$.

In either case, a length-$n$ layer cache contains $n\,d_{\text{cache}}$ scalars.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Proof.**

For a materialized cache, each position contributes $d_k$ scalars to every distinct key head and $d_v$ to every distinct value head, hence $h_kd_k+h_vd_v=d_{\text{cache}}$ scalars. MHA has $h_k=h_v=h$, GQA has $h_k=h_v=g$, and MQA has $h_k=h_v=1$, which gives the three displayed formulas after multiplying by $n$. Dividing the GQA and MQA totals by the MHA total cancels $n(d_k+d_v)$ and yields $g/h$ and $1/h$; setting $d_k=d_v$ gives the stated simplifications. For the joint-latent case, the hypothesis says that the only persisted content vector per position is $\mathbf c_t^{KV}\in\R^{d_c}$, so its width is $d_c$ and $n$ positions contain $nd_c$ scalars. Any other persisted vector contributes its own width by concatenation. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="Head-sharing savings">For a materialized-head cache, $d_{\text{cache}}=h_kd_k+h_vd_v$. With equal K/V head counts, $h=32$ to $g=8$ keeps $1/4$ of MHA; MQA keeps $1/32$.</MarginNote>

<MarginNote variant="warning" label="Two axes">GQA moves along the discrete head-multiplicity axis $g$; MLA chooses a latent width $d_c$. Equating $d_c$ with a fractional group count confuses two different factorizations.</MarginNote>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="What sharing cannot fix">MQA/GQA shrink the cache by $g/h$, but it still grows with $n$. Removing the $n$-dependence itself needs a state that does not grow — the recurrent route (<XRef id="thm-linattn-rnn" />; priced for hybrids in <XRef id="prop-decode-state" />).</MarginNote>

Two points sharpen the trade-off. First, the savings are about *bandwidth*, not chiefly parameters. Under the standard projection widths $hd_k=hd_v=d$, the query and output projections are untouched, so the projection-parameter count moves only from $4d^2$ (MHA) to $2d^2 + 2(g/h)\,d^2$, a modest change; the real win is that fewer distinct K/V heads mean fewer cache reads and writes per generated token. Second, this is why GQA is the common middle ground — it retains most of that bandwidth gain while recovering much of MHA's quality, by reading K/V groups rather than collapsing to a single shared head <Cite key="ainslie2023gqa" />.

The three variants differ only in the number of *distinct* key/value heads; the query heads and every other projection are untouched.

| Variant | Query heads | KV heads | KV cache / layer | Quality |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| MHA | $h$ | $h$ | $nh(d_k+d_v)$ | baseline |
| GQA | $h$ | $g$ ($g \mid h$) | $ng(d_k+d_v)$ — $g/h$ of MHA | near-MHA <Cite key="ainslie2023gqa" /> |
| MQA | $h$ | $1$ | $n(d_k+d_v)$ — $1/h$ of MHA | largest drop <Cite key="shazeer2019mqa" /> |

The cache column is exact (<XRef id="prop-kv-cache" />); the quality column is *empirical* — a measured trade-off, not implied by the cache formula.

MLA adds a point on a different axis: under the latent-only hypothesis its content cache is $nd_c$, even though all attention-head content keys and values may be rematerialized. The full model-level byte count follows by substituting the sum of all actually persisted widths into <XRef id="prop-kv-memory" />. DeepSeek-V2 reported a 93.3% KV-cache reduction and raised maximum generation throughput to 5.76 times its baseline for the complete system <Cite key="deepseek2024v2" />; those empirical system figures are not derived from $d_c$ alone.

## Tracing the dimensions

To see the construction concretely, take $n = 3$ tokens, $d = 4$, $h = 2$, so $d_k = d_v = d/h = 2$, with input $\mathbf X\in\R^{3\times 4}$.

- **Project.** Head 1 forms $\mathbf Q_1 = \mathbf X\mathbf W_1^Q$, $\mathbf K_1 = \mathbf X\mathbf W_1^K$, $\mathbf V_1 = \mathbf X\mathbf W_1^V$, each in $\R^{3\times 2}$; head 2 likewise with its own projections.
- **Attend.** $\operatorname{head}_1 = \softmax(\mathbf Q_1\mathbf K_1^\top/\sqrt 2)\,\mathbf V_1\in\R^{3\times 2}$, and $\operatorname{head}_2\in\R^{3\times 2}$ in the same way.
- **Concatenate.** $\operatorname{Concat}(\operatorname{head}_1,\operatorname{head}_2)\in\R^{3\times 4}$, each row being $([\operatorname{head}_1]_{i,:},[\operatorname{head}_2]_{i,:})$.
- **Project out.** $\operatorname{MHA}(\mathbf X) = \operatorname{Concat}(\cdots)\,\mathbf W^O\in\R^{3\times 4}$ with $\mathbf W^O\in\R^{4\times 4}$.

The parameter count checks against <XRef id="prop-mha-params" />: the two heads contribute $2\times(3\ \text{matrices}\times 4\times 2) = 48$ and $\mathbf W^O$ contributes $4\times 4 = 16$, for $64 = 4d^2 = 4\cdot 16$. Each head attends in its own $2$-dimensional view of $\mathbf X$ — one might learn local adjacency, the other long-range similarity — and $\mathbf W^O$ blends the two perspectives into the $d$-dimensional output.

## Exercises

Attempt each before expanding its solution.

**Exercise 5.1 (Prove).** Assuming $h\mid d$ and taking $d_k = d_v = d/h$, show the multi-head attention layer has exactly $4d^2$ parameters (excluding biases) and that this count is independent of $h$; identify which $h$-dependence cancels.

<SolutionBox>

Take $d_k = d_v = d/h$. Each head holds $\mathbf W_i^Q,\mathbf W_i^K,\mathbf W_i^V$, each $d\cdot(d/h)$ entries, so one head has $3d^2/h$ parameters. Summing over $h$ heads the $h$ cancels: $h\cdot 3d^2/h = 3d^2$. The output projection $\mathbf W^O\in\R^{d\times d}$ adds $d^2$. Total $4d^2$, independent of $h$: each new head is correspondingly narrower ($d_k = d/h$), so per-head cost falls exactly as fast as head count rises. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 5.2 (Prove).** Show each head's $n\times n$ logit matrix $\mathbf Q_i\mathbf K_i^\top$ has rank at most $d_k$. Explain what this implies for a single head when $n > d_k$, and why concatenating several heads relaxes the limitation.

<SolutionBox>

For one head $\mathbf Q_i,\mathbf K_i\in\R^{n\times d_k}$, so $\mathbf L_i = \mathbf Q_i\mathbf K_i^\top\in\R^{n\times n}$. By the rank-of-a-product inequality, $\operatorname{rank}(\mathbf L_i)\le\min(\operatorname{rank}\mathbf Q_i,\operatorname{rank}\mathbf K_i)\le d_k$. When $n > d_k$ this is below the maximal rank $n$, so a single head cannot realize an arbitrary full-rank interaction. Concatenating $h$ heads supplies $h$ independent rank-$\le d_k$ patterns, which $\mathbf W^O$ mixes and later layers compose. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 5.3 (Derive).** Use <XRef id="prop-kv-cache" /> to express the per-layer cache size for MHA, GQA($g$), and MQA, and state each as a fraction of the MHA cache. Then suppose the MLA case of <XRef id="def-mla" /> persists only a latent of width $d_c$: give its content-cache size and explain why it is not a specialization of the $g$-group curve.

<SolutionBox>

One position in a materialized-head cache stores $h_kd_k+h_vd_v$ scalars and $n$ positions multiply this by $n$. For equal K/V head counts, MHA has $d_{\text{cache}}=h(d_k+d_v)$, GQA has $g(d_k+d_v)$, and MQA has $d_k+d_v$; their per-layer caches are therefore $nh(d_k+d_v)$, $ng(d_k+d_v)$, and $n(d_k+d_v)$. As fractions of MHA these are $1$, $g/h$, and $1/h$; MQA saves the cache by the full factor $h$. When $d_k=d_v$, the formulas simplify to $2nhd_k$, $2ngd_k$, and $2nd_k$. Under the stated latent-only MLA hypothesis, the content cache is instead $nd_c$. GQA changes how many materialized K/V head rows persist; MLA stores a shared upstream latent and rematerializes head content, so $d_c$ is not a choice of group count $g$. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 5.4 (Compute).** For $d = 512$, $h = 8$ (so $d_k = d_v = 64$), trace the tensor shape of a batched input $(B, T, 512)$ through Q/K/V projection, the per-head split, per-head attention, concatenation, and the output projection, reporting the shape at each step.

<SolutionBox>

With $d=512$, $h=8$, head width $64$. **Exercise 5.5 (Construct).** Under the standard-width assumption $hd_k=hd_v=d$, give the GQA parameter count for $g$ groups as a function of $d$, $h$, and $g$, and verify it interpolates between the MHA value at $g = h$ and the MQA value at $g = 1$.

<SolutionBox>

Because $hd_k=hd_v=d$, query and output projections are unchanged across variants, each $d^2$. Only K,V shrink with grouping: $g$ groups give $gd_kd+gd_vd=2(g/h)d^2$. Hence $P_{\text{GQA}}(g) = d^2 + 2\tfrac{g}{h}d^2 + d^2 = 2d^2 + 2\tfrac{g}{h}d^2$. At $g=h$ this is $4d^2$ (MHA); at $g=1$ it is $2d^2 + 2d^2/h$ (MQA); affine and increasing in $g$, it interpolates monotonically. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
