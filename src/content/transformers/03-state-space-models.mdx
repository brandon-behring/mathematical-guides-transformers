**Where we are.** Nonlinear recurrence carries state efficiently but forces sequential evaluation and transports gradients through data-dependent Jacobian products (<XRef id="thm-gradient-bound" />). This chapter removes the nonlinearity and studies the exactly solvable system that remains. We discretize continuous dynamics exactly (<XRef id="thm-zoh" />), then prove that recurrence, convolution, and parallel scan are three schedules for one map (<XRef id="thm-conv-recurrence" />, <XRef id="prop-scan" />). The spectrum makes memory timescales explicit (<XRef id="prop-gradient-timescale" />). An optional advanced section derives HiPPO from Legendre projection (<XRef id="lem-legendre" />, <XRef id="thm-hippo-legs" />) and exposes its useful matrix structure (<XRef id="prop-legs-nplr" />). We close by proving that a fixed linear time-invariant map cannot select by content (<XRef id="prop-lti-obstruction" />).

## The continuous-time model

Strip the recurrent cell (<XRef id="def-rnn" />) to its skeleton: replace the activation $\varphi$ by the identity, and let time flow continuously rather than in token steps. What is left is a vector of $d_s$ numbers — the **state** — whose instantaneous motion is a fixed linear function of itself plus a fixed linear injection of the input, with the output read off by a fixed linear functional. Every coefficient is frozen: the same dynamics act at every time and on every input. This is the classical state-space model of control theory.<Sidenote>The state-space formulation of filtering and prediction is due to <Cite key="kalman1960filtering" />.</Sidenote>

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Linear time-invariant state-space model" id="def-ssm">
A (single-input, single-output) **linear time-invariant state-space model** with state dimension $d_s$ is the pair of equations
$$
\dot{\statevec}(t) = \statemat\,\statevec(t) + \inputmat\,u(t),
\qquad
y(t) = \outputmat\,\statevec(t) + \feedmat\,u(t),
$$
where $u:\R_{\ge 0}\to\R$ is the input signal, $\statevec(t)\in\R^{d_s}$ is the state, $y(t)\in\R$ is the output, and the parameters are $\statemat\in\R^{d_s\times d_s}$, $\inputmat\in\R^{d_s\times 1}$, $\outputmat\in\R^{1\times d_s}$, $\feedmat\in\R$. **Time-invariant** means the quadruple $(\statemat,\inputmat,\outputmat,\feedmat)$ does not depend on $t$; **linear** means it does not depend on $u$ or $\statevec$.
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Per channel">A width-$d$ model runs $d$ independent copies of this scalar-in, scalar-out system — one per channel of the embedded sequence $\hat{\mathbf X}$.</MarginNote>

<NotationOverride scope="Linear Recurrences and State-Space Models">
The state $\statevec$ is a **column** acted on by left multiplication; $\inputmat$ is a column and $\outputmat$ a row. The symbol $u$ is the scalar input signal, not the raw unit $u_t$ of the embedding interface: for an embedded sequence it is one channel of a row of $\hat{\mathbf X}\in\R^{n\times d}$ (<XRef id="def-embedding-interface" />). Here $\Delta>0$ is the discretization step size.
</NotationOverride>

## Exact discretization: zero-order hold

Tokens do not arrive continuously; they arrive as $n$ samples $u_0, u_1, \dots, u_{n-1}$. The standard bridge holds each sample constant for one interval of length $\Delta$ — a *zero-order hold* — and solves the ODE exactly across that interval. Because the dynamics are linear with constant coefficients, "exactly" is available in closed form: the scalar solution $h(t+\Delta) = e^{a\Delta}h(t) + \frac{e^{a\Delta}-1}{a}\,b\,\bar u$ promotes verbatim to matrices, with the matrix exponential replacing $e^{a\Delta}$.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Solve $\dot h = ah + b\bar u$ with $\bar u$ constant by hand first. Which of your two integration steps needs $a \ne 0$ — and what should replace division by $a$ when $a$ becomes a matrix?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="Zero-order-hold discretization" id="thm-zoh">
Let $\statevec$ solve $\dot{\statevec}(s) = \statemat\statevec(s) + \inputmat u(s)$ on an interval of length $\Delta$ on which the input is held constant, $u \equiv \bar u$. Then the state at the end of the interval is an exact linear function of the state at its start and the held sample:
$$
\statevec(\text{end}) = \discA\,\statevec(\text{start}) + \discB\,\bar u,
\qquad
\discA \defeq e^{\Delta\statemat},
\qquad
\discB \defeq \statemat^{-1}\big(e^{\Delta\statemat} - \mathbf I\big)\inputmat,
$$
the second formula valid when $\statemat$ is invertible (the series form in Step 4 is valid always).
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Take the interval to be $[0,\Delta]$ without loss of generality.

**Step 1 (The matrix exponential).** For $\mathbf M\in\R^{d_s\times d_s}$ define $e^{\mathbf M} \defeq \sum_{k\ge 0}\mathbf M^k/k!$; the series converges absolutely since $\norm{\mathbf M^k/k!}\le\norm{\mathbf M}^k/k!$ in any submultiplicative norm. As a power series in $s$ with infinite radius of convergence, $e^{s\statemat}$ may be differentiated term by term:
$$
\frac{d}{ds}\,e^{s\statemat} = \sum_{k\ge 1}\frac{s^{k-1}\statemat^{k}}{(k-1)!} = \statemat\,e^{s\statemat} = e^{s\statemat}\statemat,
$$
the last equality because every partial sum commutes with $\statemat$. For commuting matrices $\mathbf M\mathbf N = \mathbf N\mathbf M$, the Cauchy product of the two series gives $e^{\mathbf M}e^{\mathbf N} = e^{\mathbf M + \mathbf N}$ (the binomial theorem needs commutativity — the identity fails without it); in particular $e^{s\statemat}$ is invertible with inverse $e^{-s\statemat}$, and $e^{\Delta\statemat}e^{-s\statemat} = e^{(\Delta-s)\statemat}$.

**Step 2 (Integrating factor).** By the product rule and Step 1,
$$
\frac{d}{ds}\Big(e^{-s\statemat}\statevec(s)\Big)
= e^{-s\statemat}\big(\dot{\statevec}(s) - \statemat\statevec(s)\big)
= e^{-s\statemat}\inputmat\,u(s).
$$

**Step 3 (Integrate across the step; hold the input).** Integrating from $0$ to $\Delta$ with $u\equiv\bar u$ and applying the fundamental theorem of calculus,
$$
e^{-\Delta\statemat}\statevec(\Delta) - \statevec(0) = \int_0^{\Delta} e^{-s\statemat}\,ds\;\inputmat\,\bar u.
$$
Multiplying on the left by $e^{\Delta\statemat}$ and substituting $\sigma = \Delta - s$ inside the integral,
$$
\statevec(\Delta) = e^{\Delta\statemat}\statevec(0) + \int_0^{\Delta} e^{\sigma\statemat}\,d\sigma\;\inputmat\,\bar u.
$$

**Step 4 (Term-by-term integration).** On the compact interval $[0,\Delta]$ the exponential series converges uniformly (Weierstrass, with majorant $\Delta^k\norm{\statemat}^k/k!$), so the integral passes inside the sum:
$$
\int_0^{\Delta} e^{\sigma\statemat}\,d\sigma
= \sum_{k\ge 0}\frac{\Delta^{k+1}\statemat^{k}}{(k+1)!}.
$$
When $\statemat$ is invertible the sum telescopes against the exponential: $\statemat^{-1}(e^{\Delta\statemat}-\mathbf I) = \statemat^{-1}\sum_{k\ge 1}\Delta^k\statemat^k/k! = \sum_{k\ge 1}\Delta^k\statemat^{k-1}/k!$, which is the same series after the index shift $k\mapsto k+1$. Hence $\statevec(\Delta) = \discA\statevec(0) + \discB\bar u$ with $\discA, \discB$ as stated. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="ZOH">$\discA = e^{\Delta\statemat}$, $\discB = \statemat^{-1}(e^{\Delta\statemat}-\mathbf I)\inputmat$; for singular $\statemat$, $\discB = \sum_{k\ge0}\frac{\Delta^{k+1}\statemat^k}{(k+1)!}\inputmat$.</MarginNote>

Applying the theorem step after step — hold sample $u_t$ for the $t$-th interval, read the state at its end — produces the **discrete SSM**: with the empty-prefix convention $\statevec_{-1} = \mathbf 0$,
$$
\statevec_t = \discA\,\statevec_{t-1} + \discB\,u_t,
\qquad
y_t = \outputmat\,\statevec_t + \feedmat\,u_t,
\qquad t = 0,1,\dots,n-1.
$$
This is the recurrence of <XRef id="def-rnn" /> with the activation removed and the weights tied to a continuous-time parent through $\Delta$ — a tie we will exploit when we read timescales off eigenvalues. First, the payoff of linearity: two more ways to evaluate the same equations.

## Three faces of one map

Unroll the recurrence by hand for three steps: $\statevec_0 = \discB u_0$, then $\statevec_1 = \discA\discB u_0 + \discB u_1$, then $\statevec_2 = \discA^2\discB u_0 + \discA\discB u_1 + \discB u_2$. Each state is a weighted sum of *all* past inputs, with the weight of an input decaying through powers of $\discA$ as it recedes. The output is therefore a fixed pattern — a **kernel** — slid across the input: a causal convolution whose coefficients enumerate powers of $\discA$.

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="The recurrence is a causal convolution" id="thm-conv-recurrence">
For the discrete SSM with $\statevec_{-1}=\mathbf 0$, define the **impulse-response kernel** $\kappa_j \defeq \outputmat\,\discA^{j}\,\discB \in \R$ for $j \ge 0$. Then for every input sequence and every $t$,
$$
\statevec_t = \sum_{j=0}^{t}\discA^{j}\discB\,u_{t-j},
\qquad
y_t = \feedmat\,u_t + \sum_{j=0}^{t}\kappa_j\,u_{t-j} = \feedmat\,u_t + (\kappa * u)_t .
$$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
It suffices to prove the state formula; applying $\outputmat$ and adding $\feedmat u_t$ gives the output formula by linearity. We induct on $t$.

**Base case ($t=0$).** $\statevec_0 = \discA\statevec_{-1} + \discB u_0 = \discB u_0$, which is the $j=0$ term.

**Inductive step.** Assume the formula at $t-1$. Then
$$
\statevec_t = \discA\,\statevec_{t-1} + \discB u_t
= \sum_{j=0}^{t-1}\discA^{j+1}\discB\,u_{t-1-j} + \discB u_t
= \sum_{i=1}^{t}\discA^{i}\discB\,u_{t-i} + \discB u_t,
$$
reindexing $i = j+1$; the last two terms assemble to $\sum_{i=0}^{t}\discA^i\discB u_{t-i}$. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Sidenote>Materializing $\kappa_0,\dots,\kappa_{n-1}$ once turns training — where the whole input is known — into a single length-$n$ convolution, computable in $O(n\log n)$ by the fast Fourier transform <Cite key="cooley1965fft" />; this is how S4 trains <Cite key="gu2022s4" />.</Sidenote>

The convolution face evaluates all $n$ outputs at once when the whole input is known in advance, and the recurrence face emits one output per step with $O(d_s)$ state when it is not — training and decoding, respectively. The third face is subtler. The recurrence *looks* inherently sequential: $\statevec_t$ needs $\statevec_{t-1}$. But each step acts on the state as an **affine map** $\statevec\mapsto a\statevec + \mathbf b$, and affine maps compose. If composition is associative — it is — the chain of $n$ compositions can be regrouped into a balanced tree of depth $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$.

<Theorem kind="definition" name="Scan operator" id="def-scan-op">
On pairs $(a, \mathbf b)$ with $a\in\R^{d_s\times d_s}$ and $\mathbf b\in\R^{d_s}$ — the pair $(a,\mathbf b)$ encoding the affine map $\statevec\mapsto a\statevec + \mathbf b$, written lowercase to keep generic per-step data distinct from the fixed $\statemat$ — define the **scan operator**
$$
(a_2, \mathbf b_2)\bullet(a_1, \mathbf b_1) \defeq \big(a_2 a_1,\; a_2\mathbf b_1 + \mathbf b_2\big),
$$
the composition "first apply $(a_1,\mathbf b_1)$, then $(a_2,\mathbf b_2)$": indeed $a_2(a_1\statevec + \mathbf b_1) + \mathbf b_2 = a_2a_1\statevec + a_2\mathbf b_1 + \mathbf b_2$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Associativity and the log-depth scan" id="prop-scan">
Let $n\ge1$ and let $p_t \defeq (a_t, \mathbf b_t)$, $t = 0,\dots,n-1$, be **arbitrary** pairs — the matrices $a_t$ need not be equal across steps. Then:

1. $\bullet$ is associative, with identity element $(\mathbf I, \mathbf 0)$.
2. The inclusive prefix composites $P_t \defeq p_t \bullet p_{t-1}\bullet\cdots\bullet p_0$ satisfy $P_t = (a_t a_{t-1}\cdots a_0,\ \statevec_t)$, where $\statevec_t$ is the solution of the recurrence $\statevec_t = a_t\statevec_{t-1} + \mathbf b_t$ with $\statevec_{-1} = \mathbf 0$.
3. All $n$ prefix composites $P_0,\dots,P_{n-1}$ can be computed in $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ parallel rounds, each round performing at most $n$ independent $\bullet$-applications.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (Associativity, by direct computation).** For any three pairs,
$$
\big((a_3,\mathbf b_3)\bullet(a_2,\mathbf b_2)\big)\bullet(a_1,\mathbf b_1)
= (a_3a_2,\ a_3\mathbf b_2 + \mathbf b_3)\bullet(a_1,\mathbf b_1)
= (a_3a_2a_1,\ a_3a_2\mathbf b_1 + a_3\mathbf b_2 + \mathbf b_3),
$$
$$
(a_3,\mathbf b_3)\bullet\big((a_2,\mathbf b_2)\bullet(a_1,\mathbf b_1)\big)
= (a_3,\mathbf b_3)\bullet(a_2a_1,\ a_2\mathbf b_1 + \mathbf b_2)
= (a_3a_2a_1,\ a_3a_2\mathbf b_1 + a_3\mathbf b_2 + \mathbf b_3).
$$
The two agree, using only associativity of matrix multiplication and distributivity — equivalently, $\bullet$ is composition of affine maps, and composition of maps is associative. That $(\mathbf I,\mathbf 0)$ is an identity is immediate. We record what the computation did **not** use: at no point were the three matrices required to be equal. Associativity holds for arbitrary per-step pairs $(a_t, \mathbf b_t)$ — this chapter's time-invariant systems do not need that generality, but Chapter 18's input-dependent systems will inherit this proof verbatim.

**Step 2 (The prefixes realize the recurrence).** Induct on $t$. For $t=0$, $P_0 = (a_0, \mathbf b_0)$ and $\statevec_0 = a_0\mathbf 0 + \mathbf b_0 = \mathbf b_0$. For the step, write $P_{t-1} = (a_{t-1}\cdots a_0,\ \statevec_{t-1})$ by hypothesis; then
$$
P_t = p_t \bullet P_{t-1} = \big(a_t\,(a_{t-1}\cdots a_0),\ a_t\statevec_{t-1} + \mathbf b_t\big) = (a_t\cdots a_0,\ \statevec_t).
$$

**Step 3 (Doubling).** Set $P^{(0)}_t \defeq p_t$, and pad with the identity pair at negative indices. In round $r = 1,\dots,\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$, every position computes in parallel $P^{(r)}_t = P^{(r-1)}_t \bullet P^{(r-1)}_{t-2^{r-1}}$. By induction on $r$, $P^{(r)}_t$ is the composite of $p_s$ over $s\in[\max(t-2^r+1,0),\,t]$: the two operands cover the abutting ranges $[t-2^{r-1}+1,\,t]$ and $[t-2^{r}+1,\,t-2^{r-1}]$ (truncated at $0$), and associativity makes the regrouped product equal the flat composite. After $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ rounds, $2^r \ge n$, so every position holds its full prefix. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<Sidenote>The doubling scheme performs $O(n\log n)$ total $\bullet$-work; work-efficient $O(n)$ scans exist <Cite key="blelloch1990prefix" />, with the depth–size trade mapped by <Cite key="ladner1980parallel" />. The scan is the evaluation mode of the S5 layer <Cite key="smith2023s5" />.</Sidenote>

For the discrete SSM the per-step pair is $p_t = (\discA,\ \discB u_t)$: all first components happen to coincide, and part 2 of the proposition returns exactly the states of <XRef id="thm-conv-recurrence" />.

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="Three regimes">Recurrence: $O(1)$ state per emitted token (decode). Convolution: one FFT pass over a known input (train). Scan: $\lceil\log_2 n\rceil$ parallel rounds (train, without materializing $\kappa$).</MarginNote>

The three faces are one map. We verify this concretely on a system small enough to trace by hand — and large enough to show two different memory timescales at work, a preview of the next section.

<Theorem kind="example" name="Impulse response, computed both ways" id="ex-impulse-trace">
Take $d_s = 2$, $\statemat = \operatorname{diag}(-1, -0.1)$, $\inputmat = (1, 1)^\top$, $\outputmat = (1, 1)$, $\feedmat = 0$, and $\Delta = 0.5$; we carry full precision and display four decimals. Because $\statemat$ is diagonal, <XRef id="thm-zoh" /> acts per coordinate:
$$
\discA = \operatorname{diag}\big(e^{-0.5},\,e^{-0.05}\big) = \operatorname{diag}(0.6065,\ 0.9512),
\qquad
\discB = \begin{pmatrix}1 - e^{-0.5}\\ 10\,(1 - e^{-0.05})\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0.3935\\ 0.4877\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Feed the impulse $u = (1, 0, 0)$.

**Face one: the recurrence.** With $\statevec_{-1} = \mathbf 0$,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\statevec_0 &= \discB\cdot 1 = (0.3935,\ 0.4877)^\top, & y_0 &= 0.3935 + 0.4877 = 0.8812,\\
\statevec_1 &= \discA\statevec_0 = (0.2387,\ 0.4639)^\top, & y_1 &= 0.2387 + 0.4639 = 0.7026,\\
\statevec_2 &= \discA\statevec_1 = (0.1447,\ 0.4413)^\top, & y_2 &= 0.1447 + 0.4413 = 0.5860.
\end{aligned}
$$

**Face two: the kernel.** On an impulse, $y_t = \kappa_t$ directly, and with $\discA^2 = \operatorname{diag}(0.3679, 0.9048)$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\kappa_0 &= \outputmat\discB = 0.3935 + 0.4877 = 0.8812,\\
\kappa_1 &= \outputmat\discA\discB = 0.6065\cdot 0.3935 + 0.9512\cdot 0.4877 = 0.2387 + 0.4639 = 0.7026,\\
\kappa_2 &= \outputmat\discA^2\discB = 0.3679\cdot 0.3935 + 0.9048\cdot 0.4877 = 0.1447 + 0.4413 = 0.5860.
\end{aligned}
$$
The two computations agree to every digit carried, as <XRef id="thm-conv-recurrence" /> demands: $y \approx (0.8812,\ 0.7026,\ 0.5860)$. Note the two channels forgetting at different rates: the fast mode ($\lambda = -1$) retains the factor $0.6065$ per step, the slow mode ($\lambda = -0.1$) retains $0.9512$ — after a few steps the response is carried almost entirely by the slow channel.
</Theorem>

## Gradients and controlled timescales

In the nonlinear recurrence, the Jacobian from $\statevec_t$ to $\statevec_T$ was a product of $T-t$ *different*, data-dependent factors, and the best available statement was an inequality — the bound of <XRef id="thm-gradient-bound" /> — whose base $\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf W_h)\cdot\sup\lvert\varphi'\rvert$ the designer can only crudely influence. Linearity replaces the inequality by an equality, and the product of unknowable factors by the power of one known matrix.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="Gradients flow through powers; eigenvalues set timescales" id="prop-gradient-timescale">
For the discrete SSM:

1. $\dfrac{\partial \statevec_T}{\partial \statevec_t} = \discA^{\,T-t}$ for all $t \le T$ — an exact identity, not a bound.
2. If $(\lambda, \mathbf v)$ is an eigenpair of $\statemat$, then $(e^{\Delta\lambda}, \mathbf v)$ is an eigenpair of $\discA = e^{\Delta\statemat}$, and its modulus is $\lvert e^{\Delta\lambda}\rvert = e^{\Delta\operatorname{Re}\lambda}$.
3. If $\statemat$ is diagonalizable, a perturbation of $\statevec_t$ with eigenexpansion $\boldsymbol\delta = \sum_i c_i\mathbf v_i$ reaches $\statevec_T$ as $\sum_i c_i\,e^{(T-t)\Delta\lambda_i}\,\mathbf v_i$: each mode is scaled by exactly $e^{(T-t)\Delta\lambda_i}$, a factor of modulus $e^{(T-t)\Delta\operatorname{Re}\lambda_i}$. With $\operatorname{Re}\lambda_i < 0$, mode $i$ forgets on the continuous timescale $\tau_i = 1/\lvert\operatorname{Re}\lambda_i\rvert$, i.e. it decays by the factor $e^{-1}$ every $\tau_i/\Delta$ steps.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (Unroll and differentiate).** Unrolling the recurrence from time $t$ (as in the proof of <XRef id="thm-conv-recurrence" />), $\statevec_T = \discA^{T-t}\statevec_t + \sum_{j=t+1}^{T}\discA^{T-j}\discB\,u_j$. The inputs do not depend on $\statevec_t$, so the map $\statevec_t\mapsto\statevec_T$ is affine with linear part $\discA^{T-t}$; its Jacobian is that linear part.

**Step 2 (Spectral mapping).** From $\statemat\mathbf v = \lambda\mathbf v$ we get $\statemat^k\mathbf v = \lambda^k\mathbf v$ for every $k$, hence, summing the exponential series termwise, $e^{\Delta\statemat}\mathbf v = \sum_k \frac{\Delta^k\lambda^k}{k!}\mathbf v = e^{\Delta\lambda}\mathbf v$.

**Step 3 (Modulus).** Writing $\lambda = \operatorname{Re}\lambda + i\operatorname{Im}\lambda$, $\lvert e^{\Delta\lambda}\rvert = e^{\Delta\operatorname{Re}\lambda}\,\lvert e^{i\Delta\operatorname{Im}\lambda}\rvert = e^{\Delta\operatorname{Re}\lambda}$.

**Step 4 (Timescales).** For diagonalizable $\statemat$, expand $\boldsymbol\delta$ in the eigenbasis and apply parts 1–2: $\discA^{T-t}\boldsymbol\delta = \sum_i c_i e^{(T-t)\Delta\lambda_i}\mathbf v_i$. The magnitude of mode $i$ falls to $e^{-1}$ of its initial value when $(T-t)\Delta\operatorname{Re}\lambda_i = -1$, i.e. after $(T-t) = 1/(\Delta\lvert\operatorname{Re}\lambda_i\rvert) = \tau_i/\Delta$ steps. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="formula" label="One dial">$\lvert\mu\rvert = e^{\Delta\operatorname{Re}\lambda}$. The step size $\Delta$ rescales every timescale at once; the spectrum of $\statemat$ sets their ratios.</MarginNote>

<MarginNote variant="warning" label="Non-normal transients">The spectrum controls only asymptotics: for non-normal $\discA$, $\norm{\discA^k}$ can grow enormously before decaying even with all $\lvert\mu_i\rvert < 1$ (Exercise 3.5).</MarginNote>

The contrast with the previous chapter is the point. There, gradient magnitude over a horizon was an *emergent* property of trained weights passed through a pessimistic bound — vanishing or exploding by accident. Here it is a design parameter: choosing $\operatorname{Re}\lambda_i$ and $\Delta$ fixes the memory horizon of each mode exactly. The gating mechanisms of the LSTM bought a controllable additive gradient path architecturally (<XRef id="prop-cec" />); the SSM gets a stronger version for free, because there is no nonlinearity for the gradient to fight. What linearity does **not** decide is which timescales are worth having — the eigenvalues are still hyperparameters. The next section replaces that guess with a derivation.

## HiPPO: deriving the state matrix from optimal memory

**Optional on a first pass.** The operational takeaway is that HiPPO chooses state and input dynamics so that the state coordinates track an optimal low-degree summary of the observed history. Readers following the core transformer route may continue at **The price of linearity**; the derivation below supplies the complete functional-analytic explanation and the structure used by later state-space models.

Reverse the question. Instead of asking what a given $(\statemat, \inputmat)$ remembers, prescribe what the state *should be* and solve for the dynamics. The prescription: at every time $t$, the $d_s$ numbers in $\statevec(t)$ should be the coefficients of the **best degree-$(d_s{-}1)$ polynomial approximation of the entire history** $u\vert_{[0,t]}$, best in the $L^2$ sense under the uniform measure on $[0,t]$. If the state is an optimal compressed transcript of everything seen so far, then whatever the downstream readout needs, no other $d_s$-number summary serves it better in that norm.<Sidenote>The HiPPO framework of <Cite key="gu2020hippo" />; the measure family here is the "scaled Legendre" instance LegS.</Sidenote> Remarkably, this optimal summary does not need to be recomputed from scratch as $t$ advances: it evolves by a linear ODE of exactly the form we have been studying, with a specific, derivable $(\statemat, \inputmat)$.

The tool is the family of Legendre polynomials — the orthogonal polynomials of the uniform measure — and the single object that encodes everything we need about them is their generating function. All three facts the derivation uses fall out of it by elementary calculus.

For $\lvert z\rvert < 1$ and $x\in[-1,1]$, the function $G(x,z) \defeq (1 - 2xz + z^2)^{-1/2}$ expands as a power series in $z$; expanding the binomial series in powers of $z(2x - z)$ shows the coefficient of $z^m$ is a polynomial in $x$ of degree $m$. These coefficients are the **Legendre polynomials** $P_m$:
$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 2xz + z^2}} = \sum_{m\ge 0} P_m(x)\,z^m,
\qquad
P_0 = 1,\quad P_1 = x,\quad P_2 = \tfrac{3x^2 - 1}{2},\ \dots
$$

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Before the proof: both sides of each identity are coefficient sequences of some function of $z$. Which single function should you integrate or differentiate to compare them all at once?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="lemma" name="Legendre identities from the generating function" id="lem-legendre">
For all degrees $m, k \ge 0$:

1. **(Orthogonality)** $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} P_m(x)P_k(x)\,dx = \frac{2}{2m+1}\,\delta_{mk}$.
2. **(Endpoint)** $P_m(1) = 1$.
3. **(Boundary derivative expansion)** $\displaystyle(1+x)\,P_m'(x) = m\,P_m(x) + \sum_{k=0}^{m-1}(2k+1)\,P_k(x)$.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (Endpoint).** Set $x = 1$: $G(1,z) = ((1-z)^2)^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{1-z} = \sum_m z^m$. Matching coefficients against $\sum_m P_m(1)z^m$ gives $P_m(1) = 1$.

**Step 2 (Orthogonality).** Fix $z, w \in (0,1)$ and integrate the product of two generating functions. Writing $f(x) = 1-2xz+z^2$ and $g(x) = 1-2xw+w^2$, differentiation verifies in one line that $-\frac{1}{\sqrt{zw}}\ln\!\big(\sqrt{w f(x)} + \sqrt{z g(x)}\big)$ is an antiderivative of $(f(x)g(x))^{-1/2}$. At the endpoints, $f(1) = (1-z)^2$ and $f(-1) = (1+z)^2$ (likewise for $g$), and the evaluations factor:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\sqrt{w}(1-z) + \sqrt{z}(1-w)
  &= (\sqrt z + \sqrt w)(1 - \sqrt{zw}),\\
\sqrt{w}(1+z) + \sqrt{z}(1+w)
  &= (\sqrt z + \sqrt w)(1 + \sqrt{zw}).
\end{aligned}
$$
The common factor $\sqrt z + \sqrt w$ cancels in the difference of logarithms, leaving
$$
\int_{-1}^{1} G(x,z)G(x,w)\,dx
= \frac{1}{\sqrt{zw}}\,\ln\frac{1+\sqrt{zw}}{1-\sqrt{zw}}
= \sum_{m\ge 0}\frac{2\,(zw)^m}{2m+1},
$$
where the final equality uses the Taylor series $\ln\frac{1+v}{1-v} = 2\sum_m \frac{v^{2m+1}}{2m+1}$ evaluated at $v = \sqrt{zw}$. To justify expanding the left side, choose $\rho$ with $\max\{z,w\}<\rho<1$. For $x\in[-1,1]$, the singularities of $G(x,\zeta)$ lie on $|\zeta|=1$, so $G$ is uniformly bounded on the compact set $[-1,1]\times\{|\zeta|\le\rho\}$. Uniform Cauchy bounds therefore make both generating series absolutely and uniformly convergent in $x$ at the chosen $z,w$; their Cauchy product is uniformly convergent as well. We may consequently multiply the series and integrate term by term. Thus the left side is $\sum_{m,k}z^m w^k\int_{-1}^1 P_mP_k\,dx$. The two convergent bivariate power series agree for every $z,w\in(0,1)$, so uniqueness of power-series coefficients gives $0$ for $m\ne k$ and $\frac{2}{2m+1}$ for $m = k$.

**Step 3 (The expansion).** Encode both sides of the claim as series in $z$ and compare closed forms. With $Q \defeq (1-2xz+z^2)^{-3/2}$, direct differentiation gives $\partial_x G = zQ$ and $z\,\partial_z G = z(x-z)Q$. The left side of the claim is the coefficient sequence of $(1+x)\partial_x G$. For the right side, $\sum_m mP_m z^m = z\partial_zG$, and, summing the inner sum by exchanging the order of summation,
$$
\sum_{m\ge 0}\Big(\sum_{k<m}(2k+1)P_k\Big)z^m
= \sum_{k\ge 0}(2k+1)P_k\,\frac{z^{k+1}}{1-z}
= \frac{z}{1-z}\big(2z\,\partial_z + 1\big)G .
$$
It remains to check the closed-form identity $(1+x)zQ = z(x-z)Q + \frac{z}{1-z}\big(2z(x-z)Q + G\big)$. Substituting $G = (1-2xz+z^2)Q$, the bracket becomes $\big(2zx - 2z^2 + 1 - 2xz + z^2\big)Q = (1-z^2)Q$, so the right side is $z(x-z)Q + z(1+z)Q = z(1+x)Q$ — the left side. Matching coefficients of $z^m$ proves the claim. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

Now fix $t > 0$ and set up the projection. Equip $[0,t]$ with the uniform probability measure and inner product $\inner{f}{g}_t \defeq \frac{1}{t}\int_0^t f(s)g(s)\,ds$, and rescale the Legendre family onto the window: $g^{(t)}_m(s) \defeq \sqrt{2m+1}\,P_m\big(\tfrac{2s}{t}-1\big)$. Substituting $x = \frac{2s}{t}-1$ in part 1 of the lemma shows $\{g^{(t)}_m\}_{m\ge 0}$ is **orthonormal** for $\inner{\cdot}{\cdot}_t$. By the standard Hilbert-space projection theorem, the best approximation of the history in the span of $g^{(t)}_0,\dots,g^{(t)}_{d_s-1}$ has coefficients $\inner{u}{g^{(t)}_m}_t$ — so those coefficients are what the state must store.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">As $t$ advances, new signal enters at the right endpoint while the stretching window slides the basis under the old signal. Which derivative term captures each effect?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="theorem" name="HiPPO-LegS coefficient dynamics" id="thm-hippo-legs">
Let $u:[0,\infty)\to\R$ be continuous and define, for $t > 0$ and $m = 0,\dots,d_s-1$,
$$
h_m(t) \defeq \inner{u}{g^{(t)}_m}_t = \frac{\sqrt{2m+1}}{t}\int_0^t u(s)\,P_m\!\Big(\frac{2s}{t}-1\Big)\,ds,
$$
the coefficients of the $L^2$-optimal degree-$(d_s{-}1)$ approximation of the history $u\vert_{[0,t]}$. Then $\statevec(t) = (h_0(t),\dots,h_{d_s-1}(t))^\top$ satisfies, for every $t>0$, the linear time-varying dynamics
$$
\dot{\statevec}(t) = -\frac{1}{t}\,\statemat\,\statevec(t) + \frac{1}{t}\,\inputmat\,u(t),
\qquad
\statemat_{mk} =
\begin{cases}
\sqrt{(2m+1)(2k+1)} & k < m,\\
m+1 & k = m,\\
0 & k > m,
\end{cases}
\qquad
\inputmat_m = \sqrt{2m+1}.
$$
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Throughout, $x = x(s,t) \defeq \frac{2s}{t}-1$, and all degree indices run over $0,\dots,d_s-1$ ($m$ and $k$ are polynomial degrees here, not positions).

**Step 1 (Isolate the $t$-dependence).** Write $h_m(t) = \sqrt{2m+1}\,\frac{1}{t}J_m(t)$ with $J_m(t) \defeq \int_0^t u(s)P_m(x(s,t))\,ds$. The integrand is continuous in $(s,t)$ and continuously differentiable in $t$, so the Leibniz rule applies to $J_m$.

**Step 2 (Differentiate: boundary term plus stretch term).** By the Leibniz rule, using $P_m(x(t,t)) = P_m(1)$ and $\partial_t x = -\frac{2s}{t^2}$,
$$
J_m'(t) = u(t)\,P_m(1) - \int_0^t u(s)\,P_m'(x)\,\frac{2s}{t^2}\,ds ,
$$
and therefore
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\Big(\frac{J_m}{t}\Big) = -\frac{1}{t^2}J_m + \frac{1}{t}J_m'
= \frac{u(t)P_m(1)}{t} - \frac{1}{t^2}\int_0^t u(s)\Big[P_m(x) + \frac{2s}{t}\,P_m'(x)\Big]ds .
$$
The boundary term is the new sample entering at the right endpoint; the integral is the stretching window sliding the basis under the old signal. Since $\frac{2s}{t} = 1 + x$, the bracket is $P_m(x) + (1+x)P_m'(x)$.

**Step 3 (Apply the Legendre identities).** By part 2 of <XRef id="lem-legendre" />, $P_m(1) = 1$. By part 3,
$$
P_m(x) + (1+x)P_m'(x) = (m+1)\,P_m(x) + \sum_{k<m}(2k+1)\,P_k(x).
$$

**Step 4 (Recognize the coefficients; assemble the ODE).** For every degree $k$, $\frac{1}{t}\int_0^t u(s)P_k(x)\,ds = h_k(t)/\sqrt{2k+1}$ by definition. Multiplying Step 2 by $\sqrt{2m+1}$ and substituting Step 3 termwise,
$$
\dot h_m(t)
= \frac{\sqrt{2m+1}}{t}\,u(t)
- \frac{1}{t}\Big[(m+1)\,h_m(t) + \sum_{k<m}\sqrt{(2m+1)(2k+1)}\;h_k(t)\Big],
$$
which is row $m$ of the stated system. Because $\statemat$ is lower triangular, $\dot h_m$ involves only $h_k$ with $k\le m$: the first $d_s$ coefficients form a **closed** subsystem, so the truncation to $d_s$ modes is exact, not an approximation. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

The dynamics are linear but time-varying — the $1/t$ reflects the ever-stretching window, so the system has no fixed timescale to prefer (its memory is scale-equivariant by construction). Practice transplants the derived matrices into the time-invariant template of <XRef id="def-ssm" />: S4 initializes $\statemat$ (up to sign) to the LegS matrix and learns all of $(\statemat, \Delta, \inputmat, \outputmat, \feedmat)$ from there — the state matrix at a reduced learning rate — discretizing by <XRef id="thm-zoh" />.<Sidenote>S4 <Cite key="gu2022s4" />; its diagonal simplification S4D <Cite key="gu2022s4d" />; the scan-based diagonal MIMO variant S5 <Cite key="smith2023s5" />.</Sidenote> What makes that computationally viable is a hidden symmetry of the LegS matrix: it misses being a *normal* matrix — unitarily diagonalizable, powers and exponentials computable in a stable eigenbasis — by exactly a rank-one term.

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="LegS is normal plus low-rank" id="prop-legs-nplr">
Let $\statemat$, $\inputmat$ be the HiPPO-LegS matrices of <XRef id="thm-hippo-legs" />. Then
$$
-\statemat + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\inputmat\inputmat^\top = -\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf I + \mathbf T,
\qquad \mathbf T^\top = -\mathbf T,
$$
i.e. $-\statemat = \big({-\tfrac12}\mathbf I + \mathbf T\big) - \tfrac12\inputmat\inputmat^\top$: a normal matrix (skew-symmetric plus a scalar multiple of $\mathbf I$) minus a rank-one term.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
Compute the $(m,k)$ entry of $\mathbf S \defeq -\statemat + \tfrac12\inputmat\inputmat^\top$, using $(\inputmat\inputmat^\top)_{mk} = \sqrt{(2m+1)(2k+1)}$, in three cases.

**Below the diagonal ($k < m$).** $\;-\sqrt{(2m+1)(2k+1)} + \tfrac12\sqrt{(2m+1)(2k+1)} = -\tfrac12\sqrt{(2m+1)(2k+1)}$.

**On the diagonal ($k = m$).** $\;-(m+1) + \tfrac12(2m+1) = -\tfrac12$.

**Above the diagonal ($k > m$).** $\;0 + \tfrac12\sqrt{(2m+1)(2k+1)}$.

So $\mathbf S = -\tfrac12\mathbf I + \mathbf T$ with $\mathbf T_{mk} = \tfrac12\sqrt{(2m+1)(2k+1)}\cdot\operatorname{sign}(k-m)$, which satisfies $\mathbf T_{km} = -\mathbf T_{mk}$. A skew-symmetric matrix commutes with its transpose, and adding $-\tfrac12\mathbf I$ preserves that, so $-\tfrac12\mathbf I + \mathbf T$ is normal. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

Unitarily diagonalizing the normal part turns $-\statemat$ into a *diagonal-plus-low-rank* matrix, which is what lets S4 evaluate the kernel $\kappa$ of <XRef id="thm-conv-recurrence" /> quickly and stably; dropping the rank-one term altogether — leaving a purely diagonal $\statemat$, as in our worked example — loses little, an observation due to <Cite key="gupta2022dss" /> that S4D turns into principled diagonal parameterizations, and S5 pairs diagonal dynamics with the scan of <XRef id="prop-scan" />. In all three, the memory structure derived here is the initialization, not an accident of training.

## The price of linearity

Everything in this chapter — exact discretization, three interchangeable evaluation schedules, gradients read off a spectrum, a derivable optimal memory — followed from two properties of <XRef id="def-ssm" />: the map from input sequence to output sequence is *linear*, and it is *time-invariant* (the kernel $\kappa_j$ of <XRef id="thm-conv-recurrence" /> depends on the offset $j$ only, never on the position $t$ or the data). The witness below isolates the resulting map's linearity, hence additivity; it does not claim that time-invariance alone defeats every nonlinear readout. The test is the simplest task that requires reading the data to decide what to keep: **selective copying** — remember the token that is marked, ignore the ones that are not.

For this section we allow vector inputs, $u_t\in\R^2$ with $\inputmat\in\R^{d_s\times 2}$ and $\feedmat\in\R^{1\times 2}$; the unrolled formula of <XRef id="thm-conv-recurrence" /> holds verbatim, with matrix-valued kernel entries.

<MarginNote variant="tip" label="Self-explanation">Superposition makes the response to a sum equal the sum of responses. Can such a map let the marker channel veto the value channel?</MarginNote>

<Theorem kind="proposition" name="No LTI system selects by content" id="prop-lti-obstruction">
Consider the **selective-copy specification**: inputs are length-$2$ sequences of pairs $u_t = (v_t, m_t)\in\R^2$ with exactly one marked position ($m_{t^\ast} = 1$, the other $0$), and the required final output is the marked value, $y_1 = v_{t^\ast}$. No discrete LTI state-space model — of any state dimension $d_s$ and any parameters $(\discA, \discB, \outputmat, \feedmat)$ — meets this specification on the four inputs displayed in the proof. More strongly, no map whose final output is additive in the input sequence does.
</Theorem>

<Theorem kind="proof">
**Step 1 (LTI outputs are additive).** By <XRef id="thm-conv-recurrence" />, the final output of any discrete SSM is $y_1 = \feedmat u_1 + \sum_{j=0}^{1}\outputmat\discA^{j}\discB\,u_{1-j}$, a fixed linear — in particular additive — function $F$ of the input sequence $(u_0, u_1)$: $F(\mathbf x + \mathbf x') = F(\mathbf x) + F(\mathbf x')$.

**Step 2 (Four well-formed task instances).** Each of the following inputs has exactly one marked position, so the specification assigns each a target:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf x^{(1)} &= \big((3,1),\,(7,0)\big)\ \mapsto\ 3,
\qquad &
\mathbf x^{(2)} &= \big((5,1),\,(7,0)\big)\ \mapsto\ 5,\\
\mathbf x^{(3)} &= \big((3,0),\,(7,1)\big)\ \mapsto\ 7,
\qquad &
\mathbf x^{(4)} &= \big((5,0),\,(7,1)\big)\ \mapsto\ 7.
\end{aligned}
$$

**Step 3 (Additivity forces $2 = 0$).** The two column-wise differences coincide:
$$
\mathbf x^{(2)} - \mathbf x^{(1)} = \mathbf x^{(4)} - \mathbf x^{(3)} = \big((2,0),\,(0,0)\big) \eqqcolon \mathbf w .
$$
If $F$ meets all four targets, additivity evaluates $F(\mathbf w)$ twice:
$$
F(\mathbf w) = F(\mathbf x^{(2)}) - F(\mathbf x^{(1)}) = 5 - 3 = 2,
\qquad
F(\mathbf w) = F(\mathbf x^{(4)}) - F(\mathbf x^{(3)}) = 7 - 7 = 0 .
$$
The same input $\mathbf w$ would need two different outputs; additivity fails numerically, and no additive $F$ — hence no LTI SSM — meets the specification. $\qquad\blacksquare$
</Theorem>

<MarginNote variant="pattern" label="The failure mode">Marker at position $0$: changing $v_0$ must move the output one-for-one. Marker at position $1$: the same change must not move it at all. A fixed kernel cannot do both.</MarginNote>

Linearity is the property the witness kills: selection is a *product* of marker and value — a bilinear interaction — and superposition admits no interactions. Time invariance explains why the **linear** convolution uses a fixed lag kernel, but it is not independently sufficient for this impossibility: a sufficiently rich fixed time-invariant representation followed by a nonlinear readout could form marker–value interactions. Selective SSMs later make the recurrence coefficients input-dependent as an efficient route to content-dependent retention; the proposition above does not claim that every nonlinear downstream map of a fixed representation must fail.

<Theorem kind="remark" name="Toward selectivity: two escapes" id="rem-toward-selectivity">
Two known modifications restore content-dependent selection, and they organize the mixer arc through Part 5. **Escape one: recompute the weights from content, pairwise.** Abandon the fixed kernel and let the weight on position $s$ at query position $t$ be a learned function of the *data* at both positions. This is attention (<XRef id="def-attention" />): it selects by pairwise content scores, with finite-margin retrieval quantified by <XRef id="thm-associative-lookup" />, at the price of comparing all pairs — quadratic cost, and no fixed-size state (the memory grows with the sequence). The guide develops that escape first, then pauses for Part 4's efficiency and conditional-compute interlude before reducing the pair set with sparse attention (<XRef id="def-sparse-attention" />). **Escape two: keep the recurrence, let the dynamics read the input.** Make the step size and projection matrices functions of the current input, $(\Delta_t, \inputmat_t, \outputmat_t)$, so the system can dilate time around content it wants to keep and contract it around content it wants to forget. The system is then time-varying: the convolution face of <XRef id="thm-conv-recurrence" /> dies with $\kappa$, but the parallel scan survives untouched — the associativity proof of <XRef id="prop-scan" /> never used time-invariance, and it will be reused verbatim by the selective state-space model (<XRef id="def-selective-ssm" />). Part 5 brings the sparse form of the first escape and the input-dependent second escape together, then closes on hybrids.
</Theorem>

<SolutionBox exercise="3.1">

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 3.2 (Prove).** Show that $\int_0^{\Delta} e^{\sigma\statemat}\,d\sigma = \sum_{k\ge 0}\frac{\Delta^{k+1}\statemat^{k}}{(k+1)!}$ for **every** $\statemat$, that this equals $\statemat^{-1}(e^{\Delta\statemat}-\mathbf I)$ when $\statemat$ is invertible, and that the series therefore defines $\discB$ even for singular $\statemat$. Evaluate it at $\statemat = \mathbf 0$.

<SolutionBox exercise="3.2">

On $[0,\Delta]$ the series $\sum_k \sigma^k\statemat^k/k!$ is dominated termwise by the convergent constant series $\sum_k \Delta^k\norm{\statemat}^k/k!$, so it converges uniformly (Weierstrass) and integration passes inside: $\int_0^\Delta \sigma^k d\sigma = \frac{\Delta^{k+1}}{k+1}$ gives the stated sum, with no hypothesis on $\statemat$. Multiplying the sum by $\statemat$ telescopes into the exponential series: $\statemat\sum_{k\ge0}\frac{\Delta^{k+1}\statemat^{k}}{(k+1)!} = \sum_{j\ge 1}\frac{\Delta^{j}\statemat^{j}}{j!} = e^{\Delta\statemat} - \mathbf I$, so for invertible $\statemat$ the sum is $\statemat^{-1}(e^{\Delta\statemat}-\mathbf I)$. At $\statemat = \mathbf 0$ only the $k=0$ term survives: the integral is $\Delta\,\mathbf I$ and $\discB = \Delta\inputmat$ — the inverse formula is undefined there, the series is not. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 3.3 (Derive).** For the scan operator (<XRef id="def-scan-op" />): (a) verify that $(\mathbf I, \mathbf 0)$ is a two-sided identity; (b) from $P_t = p_t\bullet P_{t-1}$ derive the closed form $P_t = (a_t\cdots a_0,\ \statevec_t)$; (c) for $n = 8$, list which composite each position holds after each of the $\lceil\log_2 8\rceil = 3$ doubling rounds.

<SolutionBox exercise="3.3">

(a) $(a,\mathbf b)\bullet(\mathbf I,\mathbf 0) = (a\mathbf I,\ a\mathbf 0 + \mathbf b) = (a,\mathbf b)$ and $(\mathbf I,\mathbf 0)\bullet(a,\mathbf b) = (\mathbf I a,\ \mathbf I\mathbf b + \mathbf 0) = (a,\mathbf b)$. (b) Induction: $P_0 = p_0 = (a_0, \mathbf b_0)$ and $\statevec_0 = \mathbf b_0$; if $P_{t-1} = (a_{t-1}\cdots a_0, \statevec_{t-1})$ then $P_t = p_t\bullet P_{t-1} = (a_t\cdots a_0,\ a_t\statevec_{t-1} + \mathbf b_t) = (a_t\cdots a_0,\ \statevec_t)$. (c) Write $[s..t]$ for the composite of $p_s$ through $p_t$. After round 1 (offset 1): position $t$ holds $[\max(t{-}1,0)..t]$, i.e. $[0..0], [0..1], [1..2], \dots, [6..7]$. After round 2 (offset 2): $[\max(t{-}3,0)..t]$, i.e. $[0..0], [0..1], [0..2], [0..3], [1..4], \dots, [4..7]$. After round 3 (offset 4): $[\max(t{-}7,0)..t] = [0..t]$ for every $t$ — all eight prefixes, in 3 rounds. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

\qquad
\tfrac12\inputmat\inputmat^\top = \begin{pmatrix} 0.5 & 0.8660 & 1.1180\\ 0.8660 & 1.5 & 1.9365\\ 1.1180 & 1.9365 & 2.5\end{pmatrix}.

Then

and $\mathbf T^\top = -\mathbf T$ by inspection ($0.8660 \approx \tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$, $1.1180 \approx \tfrac{\sqrt5}{2}$, $1.9365 \approx \tfrac{\sqrt{15}}{2}$), confirming <XRef id="prop-legs-nplr" />. $\qquad\blacksquare$

**Exercise 3.5 (Construct).** Construct a $2\times 2$ matrix $\discA$ with spectral radius $0.9$ whose powers transiently **grow**: exhibit an entry of $\discA^k$ that exceeds $38$ for some $k$ before decaying to $0$, and locate the peak. Conclude that part 3 of <XRef id="prop-gradient-timescale" /> genuinely needs its diagonalizability hypothesis read with care for non-normal matrices.

<SolutionBox exercise="3.5">

Take $\discA = \begin{pmatrix}0.9 & 10\\ 0 & 0.9\end{pmatrix}$: both eigenvalues are $0.9$, so the spectral radius is $0.9 < 1$. By induction, $\discA^k = \begin{pmatrix}0.9^k & 10k\,(0.9)^{k-1}\\ 0 & 0.9^k\end{pmatrix}$ (the step: the new corner entry is $0.9^k\cdot 10 + 10k(0.9)^{k-1}\cdot 0.9 = 10(k{+}1)(0.9)^{k}$). The corner $f(k) = 10k(0.9)^{k-1}$ satisfies $f(k{+}1)/f(k) = 0.9\,\frac{k+1}{k} \ge 1$ iff $k \le 9$, so it rises to its peak $f(9) = f(10) = 90\cdot 0.9^{8} \approx 38.74$ before the geometric factor wins and drives it to $0$. A perturbation along $(1,0)^\top$'s complement is thus amplified nearly $39\times$ over ten steps despite every eigenvalue promising decay — the spectrum controls asymptotics only; for non-normal $\discA$ (here, a Jordan-type block) the transient is governed by $\norm{\discA^k}$, not the spectral radius. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>

**Exercise 3.6 (Construct).** In the pattern of <XRef id="prop-lti-obstruction" />, construct a numeric additivity witness proving that no LTI system (indeed no additive map) computes the running maximum $y = \max_t u_t$ over length-$2$ scalar sequences.

<SolutionBox exercise="3.6">

Take $\mathbf x^{(1)} = (1, 0)$ and $\mathbf x^{(2)} = (0, 1)$: the specification assigns $F(\mathbf x^{(1)}) = \max(1,0) = 1$ and $F(\mathbf x^{(2)}) = 1$. Additivity forces $F\big((1,1)\big) = F(\mathbf x^{(1)}) + F(\mathbf x^{(2)}) = 2$, but the specification demands $\max(1,1) = 1 \ne 2$. The schema is the same as in the chapter: two inputs whose targets do not add. The maximum, like selective copying, is a content-dependent decision — which entry wins depends on the data — and superposition admits no such decisions. $\qquad\blacksquare$

</SolutionBox>
